# Diversity and Inclusion

[[Kat Manalac]], YC's Managing Outreach Officer, organized a panel with six YC alumni to address diversity and inclusion at the earliest stages of a startup's life [1]. The panelists represented companies ranging from 3 to 750 employees: Makinde Adeagbo (Dev/Color, 3 people), Mitchell Lee (Penny, 5 people), Cat Perez (HealthSherpa, 20 people), Ashu Desai (Make School, 25 people), Jennifer Kim (Lever, 100+ people at 50/50 gender split), and Cristina Cordova (Stripe, 750 people) [1].

The central argument: founders building companies from scratch have the opportunity to create a culture where everyone can do their best work. Waiting until the company is large to address diversity and inclusion is too late [1].

## Defining the Terms

Manalac uses a definition from Verna Myers, a lawyer and DNI consultant: "Diversity is being invited to the party. Inclusion is being asked to dance" [1].

**Diversity** is not a code word for gender or race. It encompasses academic backgrounds, ages, national origins, religions, work experiences, and perspectives. Each company must decide what diversity means in its context and what to focus on first [1].

**Inclusion** is about building a culture where everyone feels safe and can perform their best work without having to spend cognitive energy on fitting in. It is one aspect of building a great [[Company Culture]] from scratch [1].

## Why It Matters Early

### The Business Case

A 2015 McKinsey study found correlations between diverse teams and better business performance [1]. Porter Brazwell of Jopwell frames the question: "How can you build world-changing products or services if you don't have a complete understanding of the different perspectives that exist in the world?" [1].

### The Ketchup Example

Scott Paige, a University of Michigan professor who studies teams, demonstrates how background shapes problem-solving with the ketchup question. British people and African-Americans from the south tend to keep ketchup in the cupboard. Most other groups keep it in the fridge. When you run out of ketchup, where you kept it determines what you replace it with -- the fridge person reaches for mustard or mayo; the cupboard person reaches for malt vinegar [1]. More diverse backgrounds produce more associations and more pathways to solving problems [1].

### The Retention Problem

The Kapor Center's Tech Leavers survey found that DNI issues drive voluntary turnover in tech. Recruiting talent is expensive; losing them because the culture is unwelcoming is more expensive [1]. Jennifer Kim of Lever describes the positive flip side: at a company with strong culture, "people have strong personal bonds, they are loyal to your mission, they stay a long time" [1].

### The Airbnb Warning

Brian Chesky admitted publicly that Airbnb was late to address discrimination on its platform. Three white male co-founders built the system and "there were a lot of things we didn't think about" [1]. Retroactive fixes are harder and more painful than building with awareness from the start.

## When to Start: Now

Makinde Adeagbo states bluntly: "Inclusion is something you can tank early on" [1]. Even as the founder of a diversity organization (Dev/Color), he fell into the trap of starting with people he already knew, which skewed the initial membership toward black men and underrepresented black women [1].

Mitchell Lee of Penny (5-person team) describes his turning point. Penny is a conversational finance app with a personality expressed through emojis. His fiancee told him: "I think Penny's flirting with me." He realized that if the product was meant to serve people who find finances intimidating -- which correlates with lower-income people -- the team needed to represent those users [1].

## Inclusion Failures Are Often Subtle

Ashu Desai of Make School describes a female student, Sophie, who appeared confident and capable on the surface. A year later, conversations revealed she had struggled throughout the program -- not because the environment was explicitly discriminatory, but because the culture and language made her feel she did not belong [1].

The specific dynamic: when Sophie did not know something, classmates would say "Oh, you don't know that?" For male students, this was just surprise. For Sophie, it triggered a second line of questioning: "Is it just that I didn't learn this, or is it because I don't fit here?" That second question made her less willing to ask for help [1].

Manalac shares a personal example: for her first year at YC, she wore jeans and graphic tees instead of the dresses she preferred, spending "way more brain cycles to try to fit in and be taken seriously" than she needed to [1].

## Feigned Surprise

Make School identified "feigned surprise" as a damaging pattern. When a student says "I don't know how to zip up a folder," responding with "Wait, you don't know how to zip up a folder?" assumes the student has had the same education and upbringing as the person expressing surprise. For a teacher, this is particularly harmful [1]. The fix: assume negligence, not malice, and focus on where people are going rather than where they are [1].

## Making Inclusion Companywide

### The "Sorry, I Didn't Mean to Offend" Model

Jennifer Kim learned from Candace Morgan at Pinterest about an event called "Sorry, I didn't mean to offend" -- a panel of majority-group members (white male engineers) sharing their own mistakes and missteps in a safe space [1]. This set a tone: "You are not going to be vilified by saying the wrong thing. We are going to assume best intentions" [1].

Lever adapted this into a seminar called "DNI for Supporters" (subtitle: "DNI for Dummies"), led by a straight white male engineer. The framing was deliberate: people who had previously stayed on the sidelines of DNI discussions attended because it felt designed for them. Multiple employees who had never participated before shared personal stories [1].

A black male co-worker articulated the core barrier: "When the cause isn't your own, it can feel really uncomfortable to speak out on behalf, even if you really, really want to, because of that fear of saying the wrong thing, being misinterpreted, being attacked" [1].

### The Lever Dudes Incident

At Lever, someone created a private Slack channel called "Lever Dudes" in response to the "Leverettes" channel for women employees. The channel's tone was mocking -- "Well, they have a channel so it's only fair." Nothing egregious, but it undermined the purpose of the women's group and risked creating an us-versus-them dynamic [1].

Co-founder Randall addressed the employee privately, then addressed the entire company at an all-hands meeting. He began by admitting his own past mistake: when the Leverettes group first formed, he had made a joke about grabbing engineers for pizza the same night. Only later did he realize the women's group existed not for socializing but to address real professional challenges specific to women in tech [1].

The result: employees thanked Randall throughout the day. One said: "My previous startup founders would never do anything like that. I feel really grateful and lucky to work for a place where the leaders really care" [1]. The lesson: address problems publicly even when it is uncomfortable, because employees remember the tone leaders set and it embeds into the culture as the company grows [1].

## Stripe's Engineering Hiring Goal

Cristina Cordova describes Stripe's early mistake: setting an ambitious diversity target for a large engineering hiring batch without any plan for how to meet it. They hired all the engineers they wanted, but very few were women or people of color. They admitted the failure at an all-hands meeting [1].

The fix: Stripe made every engineering manager personally responsible for bringing at least one underrepresented candidate (a person of color or woman) all the way through to the on-site interview stage. A public internal spreadsheet tracked which managers had met the goal. "That really put the power in the hands of the hiring managers to say, how can I go out there and meet people who are not in my direct network?" [1]. The result: Stripe 3x'd the number of women hired in engineering during that period [1].

### Alternative Application Paths

For interns, Stripe moved from 11% women and underrepresented minorities to over 41% in one year by creating multiple entry points beyond the traditional career fair and website application [1]:

1. **Coding challenge**: Candidates could complete a challenge on their own time and skip straight to a phone interview
2. **"Show us your code"**: At events like the Black Society of Engineers and Grace Hopper Conference, candidates could open their laptop, show a project, and advance if the code was strong

The insight: at the internship stage, you are looking for capabilities, not experience. Most college students do not have meaningful resumes. Evaluating them only by resume disadvantages candidates from non-traditional backgrounds [1].

## HealthSherpa: Diversity Aligned with Mission

Cat Perez of HealthSherpa explains that their diversity focus was driven directly by their customer base. Over 55% of the uninsured population under 65 in the United States are people of color, primarily Latinx/Hispanic and Black communities. "You can't serve that audience if you have a team that does not reflect that audience" [1].

HealthSherpa used a tool called Unitive (now Talent Owner, a Kapor Capital portfolio company) that scored job descriptions for biased language, enabled blind resume review, and standardized interview questions mapped to required competencies [1]. After rolling out the tool, the company 2x'd its Hispanic/Latinx and Black team members and saw 85% revenue growth [1].

## Tactical Advice

**Hire a team, not individuals**: If you come up with a template for the ideal employee and hire to that template, you end up with a homogeneous team. Great teams have complementary strengths and weaknesses [1].

**Start before it is comfortable**: Mitchell Lee (team of 5, white male) started thinking about diversity when expanding from 2 people. "If you end up as a team of 15 white and Asian dudes sitting in a room and start trying to incorporate diversity at that point, good luck. People don't want to be a token diversity candidate" [1].

**Use reset moments**: Company offsites and new year kickoffs are natural times to establish cultural expectations. "Whatever impression you set and whatever culture you start building in the first few weeks of the new year will typically last throughout the year" [1].

**Talk about the numbers even when they are bad**: Make School had one woman out of 22 students in their first cohort. They talked about it openly, made plans to improve, and reached seven out of 36 with 100% retention the following year [1].

**Examine hidden biases in hiring**: Lever discovered that requiring "previous college athletic experience" for sales roles excluded strong candidates. The assumption that athletes make the best salespeople is common but limiting [1].

**Provide mentorship from people who look like your underrepresented members**: Make School's alumna Leslie created a support group for female students that improved retention [1].

## The Standard You Walk Past

Lever operates by the principle: "The standard that you walk past is a standard you accept" [1]. Uncomfortable situations -- a mocking Slack channel, a biased interview question, a teammate expressing surprise that someone does not know something -- must be addressed, not ignored. Left alone, they compound into culture-defining norms.
