# Fundraising

Fundraising is one of the most written-about topics in the YC library, covered from seed mechanics to Series A diligence to the psychology of convincing investors.

## When to Raise

Graham argues that the time to raise money is not when you need it — it is when you can convince investors ([How to convince investors](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/98-how-to-convince-investors)). Fundraising when desperate is a losing position. Ralston recommends raising seed when the startup has a story that is "interesting enough to get an investor to open their checkbook" ([A guide to seed fundraising](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4A-a-guide-to-seed-fundraising)). Startups that are [[Financial Survival|default dead]] face a weaker negotiating position when approaching investors.

## How Much to Raise (Seed)

Ralston's "Guide to Seed Fundraising" recommends raising enough to get to the next milestone, typically 12-18 months of runway ([A guide to seed fundraising](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4A-a-guide-to-seed-fundraising)). The amount depends on the type of business. In Graham's framing, both raising too little (#11 in "18 Mistakes") and raising too much (#13) are dangerous — too little leaves no margin for error, too much creates complacency and overspending ([The 18 mistakes that kill startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8t-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups)). How much to raise is closely tied to [[Financial Survival]] — founders should model their [[Financial Survival|burn rate]] and path to [[Default Alive or Default Dead|default alive]] status.

## Financing Instruments

Ralston covers three main options at seed ([A guide to seed fundraising](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4A-a-guide-to-seed-fundraising)):

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity).** YC's preferred instrument. Simpler than convertible notes, no interest, no maturity date. Converts to equity at the next priced round.

**Convertible Notes.** Debt that converts to equity. Simpler than a priced round but more complex than SAFEs. Include interest and maturity dates.

**Priced Equity Round.** Standard stock purchase. More expensive and complex at seed stage. More common for larger seed rounds.

## Convincing Investors

Graham identifies three things investors need to see: formidable founders, a promising market, and evidence of success so far ([How to convince investors](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/98-how-to-convince-investors)).

**Seeming formidable.** Not just confident — justifiably confident. Graham argues the best way for inexperienced founders to do this is to stick to the truth. "Convince yourself first, then explain." Investors can sense the difference between genuine conviction from domain expertise and the Dunning-Kruger effect. This connects to [[Founder Mindset]] — the conviction required to fundraise is the same conviction required to build.

**Market sizing.** Investors think in markets, founders think in [[Startup Ideas|ideas]]. Framing the opportunity as a large addressable market, and answering "why now?" (what changed to make this possible), are essential.

**Handling rejection.** Graham recommends being candid about why investors turned you down and why they are mistaken. The best investors already believe most other investors are conventional-minded.

## Pitch Decks

Hale argues that a pitch deck should answer three questions: What do you do? How big is the market? What is your traction? He recommends keeping decks to 10-15 slides and leading with the clearest possible statement of the problem and solution ([How to design a better pitch deck](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4T-how-to-design-a-better-pitch-deck)).

Tam's Series A deck guide emphasizes that Series A pitches must show metrics and traction, not just vision. The shift from seed to Series A is from "promising" to "delivering" ([How to build a great Series A pitch and deck](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8d-how-to-build-a-great-series-a-pitch-and-deck)). [[Growth]] metrics — revenue, engagement, retention — become the centerpiece.

## Series A Mechanics

Harris's diligence checklist covers what founders need to have ready: corporate records, business plan and financials, IP documentation, securities, material agreements, and employee/benefits records ([Series A diligence checklist](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/3h-series-a-diligence-checklist)). Having a complete data room before signing a term sheet can cut a week off closing.

A critical detail: all pending equity grants should be completed before starting the Series A process. Once a term sheet exists, the old 409A valuation is no longer usable, which hurts employees hired under the prior valuation. This intersects with [[Hiring]] — founders should plan equity grants relative to fundraising timelines.

## Investor Pitch vs. Customer Pitch

Seibel draws a sharp distinction between pitching investors and pitching customers ([How pitching investors is different than pitching customers](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7u-how-pitching-investors-is-different-than-pitching-customers)). Customers know the problem and care about the solution's specifics -- jargon helps, demos help, and the customer should talk more than the founder. Investors often do not have the problem at all -- jargon hurts, marketing language ("platform," "first ever") is fluff that must be filtered out, and the pitch must describe what the product does in plain language.

Seibel illustrates with Google: "Google organizes the world's information" tells an investor nothing. A better pitch: "Google is a search engine. You type what you want to know, click enter, and we show you websites ranked by how other websites link to them." Investor pitches should answer six questions: what do you do, how far along are you, how big is the market, how will you charge, what do you know that others do not, and who is on the team. Customer pitches focus on functionality, onboarding, and specific pricing. If a startup's website and pitch deck say the same thing, something is wrong.

## Handling Investor Rejection

YC's advice: "Believe the no, don't believe the why" ([Investors said no, now what](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/J1-inside-the-group-partner-lounge-investors-said-no-now-what)). Seibel, Taggar, and Flora observe that founders routinely let a 30-minute Zoom call with a distracted investor shake their conviction about a product they have been building for months. The investor's stated reason for passing is rarely the real reason -- it is a polite fabrication. Founders who pivot their company based on an investor rejection email are signaling low conviction, which makes them less attractive to subsequent investors.

Flora frames it starkly: "Investor spends two minutes writing the email and then later hears that you've pivoted your entire company because of it. Not a huge signal of conviction." The partners note that investors frequently reject companies pre-YC and then seek to invest post-YC with nothing having changed -- further evidence that the "why" in a rejection carries little signal.

## The Investor Exploitation Pattern

Caldwell and Seibel describe a recurring pattern where early-stage founders are exploited by investors before they have leverage ([The truth about Y Combinator](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IU-dalton-michael-the-truth-about-y-combinator)). Horror stories from YC founders include: an investor buying over a third of a company for a few hundred thousand dollars, poisoning the cap table for future rounds; investors demanding $50K in legal fees for a priced round that should cost far less; investors committing to lead but requiring the founder to raise the rest first; and investors "negging" founders ("your plan will never work") while simultaneously pressuring them to take more money.

Caldwell compares the early-stage investor ecosystem to the entertainment industry: the most approachable people are often the most exploitative. "A lot of the folks who are most approachable when you're a first-timer... the friendly people that want to talk to you are often the most exploitative." The cure is building something before fundraising, which creates leverage. "If your idea for how you start a startup is to first create a pitch deck and go pitch VCs, you're setting yourself up to get screwed."

The YC $500K standard deal was designed partly to shift this dynamic. Founders enter fundraising with capital already in hand, reducing desperation. Post-YC companies typically receive 10-70 inbound investor inquiries, reversing the usual dynamic where founders beg for money. "Investors cold email you. You don't cold email investors."

## Legal Mechanics of Fundraising

Levy covers the legal foundations every startup needs before raising ([Startup legal mechanics](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7R-startup-legal-mechanics)). Incorporate in Delaware using platforms like Clerkie or Stripe Atlas. Issue founder stock with four-year vesting and a one-year cliff. Maintain a cap table from the first share issued. Open a corporate bank account immediately. Do not use employment agreements (they can inadvertently change at-will status); use offer letters instead. Standard university spin-out equity stakes of 3-5% align with [[Equity and Compensation]] guidance.
