# Startup Ideas

The YC canon on startup ideas centers on a counterintuitive claim: the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of them. Graham argues that the verb is "notice," not "think up" ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). The best ideas come from founders solving problems they personally experience.

## How to Find Ideas

Graham's core advice is to "live in the future, then build what's missing" ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). Founders who work at the frontier of a changing field will naturally encounter problems that are also startup ideas. The key is to train yourself to notice when something is broken or missing, rather than sitting down to brainstorm.

Organic ideas -- those that grow from the founder's own experience -- are almost always stronger than ideas generated on demand. When a founder has the problem themselves, they have built-in conviction, domain expertise, and an unfakeable understanding of what users need. This deep [[Founder Mindset]] is what separates real insight from surface-level analysis.

Friedman presents seven ranked recipes for generating ideas explicitly ([How to get startup ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8g-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). The best recipe: start with what your team is especially good at and generate ideas where you have an unfair advantage. About half of YC's most successful companies trace back to this pattern. SnapDocs came from a founder with 10 years in mortgages. Lattice came from founders who noticed how bad their own performance review software was. Mixpanel came from a founder who saw his employer's internal analytics tools and realized other companies needed them.

Lower-ranked recipes include building what you wish existed (DoorDash), pursuing a 10-year passion (Boom), riding a technology change (PlanGrid and the iPad), copying a successful model to a new variant (Standard Cognition), crowdsourcing ideas from experts, and looking for broken industries (LendUp). Friedman warns that the lower-ranked methods are more likely to produce solutions in search of a problem.

## The Well Model

Good startup ideas serve a small number of people who want the thing intensely. Graham calls this a "well" -- deep and narrow ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). The alternative, a broad shallow hole (many people want it a little), is a much worse starting point. Nearly all good startup ideas have the well shape initially. Facebook started with just Harvard students. Stripe started with just programmers. This principle connects directly to the [[Early-Stage Tactics]] strategy of the "contained fire" -- dominate a narrow market before expanding.

## Larval Markets

Graham introduces the concept of a "larval market" -- a small but growable market ([Billionaires build](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/9M-billionaires-build)). Apple in 1976 is the canonical example: not many people wanted a personal computer, but the market grew until every 10-year-old wanted one (but calls it a "phone"). The ideal combination is founders who are "living in the future" and building something they themselves want. The crucial feature of the initial market is that it must exist -- "there have to be some people who want what you're building right now, and want it so urgently that they're willing to use it, bugs and all." A larval market can also be regional: build for one location, then expand. This concept extends the well model -- the initial well grows into a lake.

## The Idea Quality Score

Friedman formalizes idea evaluation into a four-criteria score ([How to get startup ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8g-how-to-get-startup-ideas)): how big the idea could be (look for existing large companies in the space), founder/market fit (are you an expert?), confidence you are solving a real problem (ideally from personal experience), and whether you have a new insight others lack. Airbnb scored high on insight -- the founders had actually hosted strangers and discovered it was fun, while everyone else assumed it was dangerous.

Caldwell applies a similar framework in [[Pivoting]], scoring ideas on market size, founder/market fit, ease of getting started, and early market feedback. Brex scored 2.5/10 on its original VR idea and 7.75/10 on the credit card for startups idea.

## Where Great Ideas Come From

Caldwell and Seibel analyze Airbnb, Coinbase, and Stripe to identify three recurring patterns ([Where do great startup ideas come from](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DU-dalton-michael-where-do-great-startup-ideas-come-from)). First, timing: existing products existed but were 10x improvable. VRBO did not facilitate payments. Mt. Gox was unreliable. Authorize.net took three months to integrate. Second, most investors and founders thought the ideas were bad -- Airbnb was "Couchsurfing but charging money," Coinbase was a "tiny bubble market," Stripe was "two teenagers in the banking space." Third, the actual market turned out far bigger than the founders projected.

Seibel draws a direct lesson: "You shouldn't give a shit if there's an existing product in the market" if users hate it. And founders should "be very careful about your initial market math" -- transformational products create use cases the founders cannot predict.

## Simple Products Win

Caldwell and Seibel argue that the most common early mistake is building too many features before getting a single user ([Simple products that became big companies](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DT-dalton-michael-simple-products-that-became-big-companies)). Gusto started as ZenPayroll doing exactly one thing: running payroll online. OpenSea spent two years grinding with a simple buy-and-sell NFT website. Coinbase was a website where you typed how much Bitcoin you wanted and pressed a button. "A product that doesn't work with lots of features is infinitely worse than a product with one feature that works," Seibel states. This connects to the [[Product Development]] principle of shipping a lean MVP.

## Dangerous Filters

Four unconscious filters cause founders to systematically overlook the best ideas:

**The Schlep Filter.** Founders avoid ideas that involve tedious, messy real-world work. Graham cites Stripe as the canonical example: thousands of programmers saw the payments problem but unconsciously shrank from the schlep of dealing with banks and regulations ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). Friedman adds Gusto (payroll) as another example -- thousands of programmers knew payroll software was broken but nobody wanted to work on something so boring ([How to get startup ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8g-how-to-get-startup-ideas)).

**The Unsexy Filter.** Founders avoid ideas in boring-seeming domains. The most valuable ideas are often in areas that seem unexciting -- infrastructure, logistics, compliance -- precisely because few people pursue them.

**The Too-Ambitious Filter.** Founders shy away from ideas that seem too big. Friedman cites Altman's observation that hard startups are often paradoxically easier because they attract better talent and face less competition.

**The Competitor Filter.** Founders avoid spaces with existing competitors. Friedman argues founders should actually err toward spaces with competitors -- when there are no competitors, it usually means nobody wants the product. Dropbox launched into a space with dozens of file-syncing tools and won with a better product.

## Solution in Search of a Problem

Caldwell and Seibel describe founders who build around hot technology without knowing who the customer is ([Should you follow your passion](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DW-dalton-michael-should-you-follow-your-passion)). These founders pattern-match startups to school: they seek validation from authority figures (investors, press) rather than from customers. "Getting validation from all those things" -- investors, press, friends, startup competitions -- "and it could still not work," Caldwell warns. See [[Talking to Users]] for how to replace this with ground-truth customer evidence.

## Passion and Ideas

Caldwell and Seibel reframe the common advice to "follow your passion." True passion means working on something even without money or approval from authority figures -- like the early Microsoft and Apple founders tinkering with microcomputers nobody thought would matter. For founders without a burning personal problem, Seibel offers a practical alternative: "Guess what gives you passion? That damn thing working. Success, users, revenue numbers." Building something that works creates passion retroactively. Twitch is the canonical example -- the founders were not passionate about live video streaming, but 30 million monthly visitors kept them motivated for years ([Should you follow your passion](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DW-dalton-michael-should-you-follow-your-passion)).

## Creating Luck Through Speed

Caldwell frames idea discovery as a function of speed ([How do you create luck](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7L-how-do-you-create-luck)). Brex pivoted from VR headsets to credit cards for startups by iterating through half a dozen ideas in six weeks. Retool and Magic followed the same pattern. "The founders that get the luckiest are the founders that move the fastest to give themselves as much surface area as possible." Pre-company, the equivalent is making things constantly and showing them to people -- Zuckerberg was building prototypes and music plugins long before Facebook.

## Starting With Curiosity

In "Before the Startup," Graham argues that the right preparation is not studying startup mechanics but becoming genuinely expert in a domain ([Before the Startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8y-before-the-startup)). At age 20, the optimal strategy is "just learn" -- follow curiosity aggressively, because the breadth of knowledge compounds into unexpected startup ideas later. Friedman adds that working at an existing startup is particularly helpful -- many of YC's most successful companies came from ideas founders had while working at someone else's startup.

Butterfield describes the Slack origin as an unconscious three-and-a-half-year design process where the team built internal tools for their own communication without realizing they were designing a product ([How to get ideas and find what's working](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8B-how-to-start-a-startup-how-to-get-ideas-and-find-what-s-working)). The team underestimated the market by 100x. He favors execution over ideas but acknowledges both matter: "You can execute a lot on a terrible idea and then not get anywhere."
