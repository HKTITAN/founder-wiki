# Startup Ideas

The YC canon on startup ideas centers on a counterintuitive claim: the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of them. Graham argues that the verb is "notice," not "think up" ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). The best ideas come from founders solving problems they personally experience.

## How to Find Ideas

Graham's core advice is to "live in the future, then build what's missing" ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). Founders who work at the frontier of a changing field will naturally encounter problems that are also startup ideas. The key is to train yourself to notice when something is broken or missing, rather than sitting down to brainstorm.

Organic ideas — those that grow from the founder's own experience — are almost always stronger than ideas generated on demand. When a founder has the problem themselves, they have built-in conviction, domain expertise, and an unfakeable understanding of what users need. This deep [[Founder Mindset]] is what separates real insight from surface-level analysis.

## The Well Model

Good startup ideas serve a small number of people who want the thing intensely. Graham calls this a "well" — deep and narrow ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)). The alternative, a broad shallow hole (many people want it a little), is a much worse starting point. Nearly all good startup ideas have the well shape initially. Facebook started with just Harvard students. Stripe started with just programmers. This principle connects directly to the [[Early-Stage Tactics]] strategy of the "contained fire" — dominate a narrow market before expanding.

## Dangerous Filters

Three unconscious filters cause founders to systematically overlook the best ideas:

**The Schlep Filter.** Founders avoid ideas that involve tedious, messy real-world work. Graham cites Stripe as the canonical example: thousands of programmers saw the payments problem but unconsciously shrank from the schlep of dealing with banks and regulations ([How to Get Startup Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8z-how-to-get-startup-ideas)).

**The Unsexy Filter.** Founders avoid ideas in boring-seeming domains. The most valuable ideas are often in areas that seem unexciting — infrastructure, logistics, compliance — precisely because few people pursue them.

**"Sitcom" Ideas.** Ideas that sound plausible on paper but nobody actually needs. Graham calls these "made-up" or "sitcom" ideas — the kind of thing a character on a TV show would think of ([Why Smart People Have Bad Ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/91-why-smart-people-have-bad-ideas)). They have a surface-level logic but no genuine demand underneath.

## What Makes a Bad Idea

Graham identifies several patterns in his "18 Mistakes" essay ([The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8t-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups)). Derivative ideas — minor variations on existing products — fail because they lack the conviction and insight that comes from solving a genuine problem. Marginal niches fail because there is no path to a large enough market for [[Growth]]. Ideas pursued without a specific user in mind fail because the founder cannot tell whether they are making progress toward [[Product Development]] milestones.

## Starting With Curiosity

In "Before the Startup," Graham argues that the right preparation is not studying startup mechanics but becoming genuinely expert in a domain ([Before the Startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8y-before-the-startup)). At age 20, the optimal strategy is "just learn" — follow curiosity aggressively, because the breadth of knowledge compounds into unexpected startup ideas later.

"Turn your mind into the type that startup ideas form in without any conscious effort," Graham writes. This means cultivating genuine expertise and noticing real problems, not trying to reverse-engineer ideas from market analysis. This advice connects to the broader [[Founder Mindset]] theme that domain expertise matters more than startup expertise.
