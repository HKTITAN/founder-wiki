# Founder Mindset

Graham argues that the most important quality in a startup founder is determination. Not intelligence -- determination ([The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8w-the-hardest-lessons-for-startups-to-learn)). Across several essays, he builds a picture of the founder mindset as a specific combination of relentlessness and flexibility.

## Relentlessly Resourceful

In the essay of that title, Graham proposes "relentlessly resourceful" as the two-word answer to what makes a good founder ([Be Relentlessly Resourceful](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/94-be-relentlessly-resourceful)). Not merely relentless -- that produces stubbornness. Not merely resourceful -- that produces aimlessness. The combination is what matters.

He uses the running back metaphor: a good running back does not just run straight ahead (relentless) or dance around (resourceful). They do both -- pushing forward while constantly adapting to the field. This same metaphor appears in "The Hardest Lessons."

Graham writes that "Make something people want" is the destination, but "Be relentlessly resourceful" is how you get there. He also argues this quality can be taught -- "surprisingly often it can. Not to everyone, but to many people." This resourcefulness is what enables the [[Early-Stage Tactics]] of doing things that don't scale.

## Determination Over Intelligence

Graham argues in "Why to not not start a startup" that determination is "probably the single best predictor of success" ([Why to not not start a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8x-why-to-not-not-start-a-startup)). Most hackers underestimate their determination -- he has seen many become visibly more determined as they get used to running a startup. Founders who would have been delighted at first to sell for $2M become set on world domination.

He argues that intelligence requirements are modest. "If you're smart enough to worry that you might not be smart enough to start a startup, you probably are." Most companies do mundane work where effort, not brains, is the decisive factor. See also [[Becoming a Founder]] for Graham's full analysis of which reasons to hesitate are valid.

## Genuine Interest as the Real Driver

Graham's "Billionaires build" reveals that what keeps the most successful founders working long-term is not money but genuine interest in what they are building ([Billionaires build](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/9M-billionaires-build)). "One thing few people realize about billionaires is that all of them could have stopped sooner. They could have gotten acquired, or found someone else to run the company."

Scholl echoes this from the [[Biotech and Deep Tech|hard tech]] perspective: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist. Startups are hard -- all startups are hard -- and I think what makes the difference many times is are you working on something that is so meaningful to you, that it is worth it for you to go through hell to make it happen?" ([On starting and scaling aerospace company Boom](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5Q-on-starting-and-scaling-aerospace-company-boom)).

Founders driven primarily by money will take the first large acquisition offer. Founders doing it to seem cool will find less painful ways to seem cool. The ones who succeed at the largest scale are building their project.

## Counterintuitive Nature of Startups

"Before the Startup" identifies six ways startups are counterintuitive ([Before the Startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8y-before-the-startup)). The most important: the way to succeed is not to be an expert on startups but to be an expert on users and the problem. "Starting a startup is where gaming the system stops working. There is no boss to trick, only users."

Graham warns specifically about "playing house" -- going through the motions of having a startup (office, business cards, [[Hiring]]) without doing the hard work of making something people want. This connects to his advice in [[Startup Ideas]] that domain expertise matters more than startup mechanics.

## What Actually Kills Startups

Graham's "18 Mistakes" identifies the single meta-mistake: not making something users want ([The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8t-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups)). "Nearly all failure funnels through that." The most common form of startup death is not a spectacular flameout but a half-hearted side project that never gets anywhere (#18: half-hearted effort).

In "The Hardest Lessons," he identifies three self-inflicted causes of death: internal disputes between [[Cofounders]], inertia (not releasing, not iterating), and ignoring users ([The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8w-the-hardest-lessons-for-startups-to-learn)). Competitors almost never kill startups -- startups kill themselves.

## Optimism and Deals

Graham treats founder optimism as a nuclear reactor: enormously powerful but dangerous if unshielded. Founders should be optimistic about their ability to build something great, but cynical about deals, partnerships, and anything that requires other people to follow through ([The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8w-the-hardest-lessons-for-startups-to-learn)). "The most dangerous thing is to let them go to your head." This skepticism is especially important during [[Fundraising]] and [[Sales and Distribution]] negotiations.

## Formidable

In the [[Fundraising]] context, Graham describes the quality investors look for as "formidable" -- not just confident, but justifiably confident ([How to Convince Investors](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/98-how-to-convince-investors)). The best way for inexperienced founders to be formidable is to stick to the truth about what they know and what they have built.

## What Makes Unicorn Founders Different

Livingston identifies nine traits that separate massively successful founders from merely successful ones ([What's different about unicorns](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8L-what-s-different-about-unicorns)). Luck and timing come first -- the best ideas grow organically from founders' lives, not from deliberate planning. Beyond luck, the unicorn founders are never primarily motivated by money or status. They are "fanatically interested in what the company is doing." They are independent-minded, ambitious enough to embrace a frighteningly large opportunity rather than shrink from it, and possess exceptional drive that may not be visible until the authority vacuum of a startup reveals it.

Livingston adds a critical late-stage requirement: the founder must become a manager. "Designing cool products and managing people are really different things. It is a rare person who can be great at both. But you have to be if you want to create one of the really big startups." See [[Management]] for the maker-to-manager transition.

## Resilience Over Confidence

Taggar, after working with nearly a thousand founders, concludes that resilience is the quality that matters most ([Should you start a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/GV-should-you-start-a-startup)). He initially used confidence as a proxy for resilience but found this wrong -- some outwardly confident founders crumbled at the first setback, while quiet founders like Sajith of Benchling (now valued at $6B+) endured years of rejection to transform into formidable leaders.

Taggar argues that no simple test predicts resilience. Neither school performance nor work success reliably correlates with startup resilience. The practical advice: assess whether you can live with the worst-case scenario (typically a year of low or no salary). "Be honest with yourself. If you can't actually handle the worst case, then your own constant anxiety will probably self-sabotage your startup efforts."

## Never Lose Momentum

Altman argues that one of a founder's most important jobs is never losing momentum ([How to succeed with a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/89-how-to-succeed-with-a-startup)). "If you have momentum, people keep delivering results beyond what they think they're capable of. If you lose momentum, it's very difficult to get it back." The startup should maintain a cadence of winning on short, predictable intervals. This means the first few years offer no real work-life balance -- a reality YC tries to be honest about.

Altman also describes the team qualities that sustain momentum: optimists who maintain internal conviction when the world says they will fail, idea generators who constantly propose new approaches (most bad, some essential), and people with a "we'll figure it out" spirit who act with incomplete data rather than waiting for certainty.

## Conviction

Caldwell and Seibel identify conviction as a distinct, trainable founder trait ([Building confidence in yourself and your ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Kg-building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas)). A founder with low conviction -- who pivots after 25 cold emails go unanswered, or abandons an idea because investors say no -- will never build anything. Conviction does not mean religious belief; it means not getting "blown off course" by every piece of negative feedback.

"The most important customer is yourself" -- the first step in building a company is convincing your own brain that the idea is worth working on. Founders who skip this step do what Caldwell calls a "random walk" -- changing direction at random intervals, never accumulating forward progress, and running out of energy in 1.5-2 years with nothing to show for it.

Fear is the primary cause of bad thinking. Founders fabricate benchmarks ("75% of the batch has already launched"), accept anonymous internet advice as gospel, and let fundraising anxiety override domain expertise. YC's $500K standard deal was designed partly to reduce this desperation.

## Don't Think Like an Investor

Caldwell and Seibel warn founders against applying investor frameworks -- market sizing, competitive analysis, exit strategies -- to early-stage decisions ([Why founders shouldn't think like investors](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Kk-why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors)). Founders who think like VCs tend to choose ideas based on what raises money rather than what solves problems, spend time on macro analysis instead of getting customers, and filter out ideas that seem too small. The cure: "Turn off the pollution" of investor content, spend time with users who do not care about fundraising trends, and build expertise in a specific domain rather than analyzing markets from an apartment.

## When to Trust Your Gut

Caldwell and Seibel distinguish between founders with expertise and founders without ([When should you trust your gut?](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Kw-when-should-you-trust-your-gut)). A founder who spent ten years building mortgage underwriting software should trust their instincts about what mortgage underwriters need. A founder who just bought a house and has ideas about real estate should not. The common failure: the expert is fearful and acts like the non-expert, while the non-expert is overconfident and acts like the expert.

## Stop Innovating on the Wrong Things

Caldwell and Seibel argue that startups have a limited amount of "innovation juice" and should concentrate it entirely on making something people want ([Stop innovating on the wrong things](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Kc-stop-innovating-on-the-wrong-things)). Common anti-patterns include: innovating on corporate structure (Wyoming LLCs, nonprofit-owns-for-profit), choosing idiosyncratic programming languages, inventing novel [[Pricing|pricing]] models that confuse customers, and trying to disprove all startup advice simultaneously. Seibel frames it: "Making a startup work is a miracle. The odds that you are going to be able to perform five miracles at once is much, much, much lower."

## How Billion-Dollar Startups Start

YC partners examining the origins of top companies find consistent patterns ([How do billion dollar startups start?](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Li-how-do-billion-dollar-startups-start)). The companies looked ordinary at the start -- Solugen brought a beaker of hydrogen peroxide to their interview and sold bottles from a garage. Amplitude applied with a voice-to-text Android app that the partners did not believe in, but funded because of the founder's intensity. Brex came in with a VR startup. Segment pitched classroom polling software with zero traction.

Seibel identifies a quality he calls "obstinance" in the best founders: "Whatever they're working on, they don't do it at 60 or 70 or even 80 percent. They're always just 100 percent committed." Diana Hu adds that the best early-stage founders are "not well-rounded. They're very quirky people."

## The Asymmetric Upside of Optimism

Caldwell and Seibel argue that optimism is not naive -- it is asymmetric ([The asymmetric upside of being positive](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/KS-the-asymmetric-upside-of-being-positive)). They catalog advances since the 1990s -- information access, communications, transportation, healthcare, entertainment -- and note that none of them seemed transformative at the time. Pessimism is rewarded on social media and feels sophisticated, but it produces nothing. Builders must be optimistic to invest years in a project.

Seibel frames it directly: "If you really care about those problems, you have to be optimistic to try to solve them." The snarky critic who never builds anything is nobody's hero. Caldwell adds that the greatest change agents in history were default optimistic -- "they believed effort wasn't a waste of time."

This connects to the broader theme of [[Financial Survival|balancing optimism with realism]] -- founders must believe the future is worth building while acknowledging current constraints.

## Confronting Reality

Garry Tan describes the danger of the opposite extreme: living in a "dreamland" where the founder avoids hard truths ([The Main Function: How to go from startup dream to reality](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JG-the-main-function-how-to-go-from-startup-dream-to-reality)). At Posterous, two years of 20-30% monthly growth went flat, and Tan looked for statistics that confirmed they were still fine rather than confronting the competitive shift. He compares this to The Truman Show -- the founder's constructed reality.

The solution is not to abandon the dream but to bridge it with reality. Tan cites Ray Dalio: "Whatever success I've had in life has had to do more with my ability to know how to deal with my not knowing." Surrounding yourself with smart, independent thinkers who challenge groupthink -- not yes-men -- converts individual dreamland into collective reality. Karl Popper's falsifiability principle applies: founders discover truth through experimentation and criticism, not certainty.

## Perseverance Through Crisis

Edrizio De La Cruz's path from selling guavas in Santo Domingo to selling his fintech company Arcus to Mastercard illustrates perseverance under extreme conditions ([From Underdog to Undefeated](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/KG-from-underdog-to-undefeated-the-edrizio-de-la-cruz-founder-story)). After 144 investor meetings with no results for his Series A, the one term sheet he received collapsed four weeks before running out of cash. He flew to Mexico for a backup meeting, survived an earthquake that cancelled it, refused to leave, delivered his first-ever pitch in Spanish, and raised $8M before running out of money.

His guiding principle: "You got to stick around long enough to get lucky. Just don't die." During COVID, he was voted out of his own company temporarily, experienced a personal miscarriage, and faced a stalled Series B -- then won a Visa competition and used the momentum to raise from SoftBank and Citi, eventually attracting Mastercard's acquisition. De La Cruz credits Marcus Aurelius: "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."

## Riding Exponentials and Platform Shifts

Buchheit, reflecting on Gmail and his time at Google and Facebook, argues that every company worth $100B+ is sitting on top of an exponential change in the world ([The path to $100B](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/82-the-path-to-100b)). Intel and Microsoft rode the microcomputer wave. Google rode the internet's information explosion. The founder's job is to identify a change that will be fundamentally different in ten years and position the company to capture it.

Buchheit describes founder conviction as almost irrational. Zuckerberg turned down Yahoo's $1B offer when Facebook had 10 million users and replaced the management team that pressured him to sell. When 8 million of 10 million users protested the News Feed, he held firm. Musk invested his entire fortune in three rockets that all blew up, then scraped together enough for a fourth. "That is insane. I would not take all of my money and plough it into some harebrained rocket scheme. That's a level of irrationality that is I think wonderful."

## Surviving Cyclical Industries

Chandan Lodha of CoinTracker describes building a company through crypto's boom-bust cycles, where "the entire world is telling us that the entire space that we're working in is irrelevant, dead, and doesn't make sense" ([How to survive the crypto boom & bust cycle](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Km-how-to-survive-the-crypto-boom-bust-cycle)). Six months from shutting down in 2020, CoinTracker survived by having an excellent cofounder relationship, returning to first principles about mission, and being transparent with the team about the company's financial reality. The company grew 14x during the 2021 bull run, then used the subsequent bear market to completely rebuild their product over nine months. CoinTracker shifted a guiding principle from "think long-term" to "think long-term, ship today" -- a small change that "gave everyone in the company the permission to take risks and experiment."

## Beware of Startup Experts

Caldwell and Seibel warn that bad startup advice is worse than no advice ([The problem with startup experts](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LM-the-problem-with-startup-experts)). Local startup ecosystems, university innovation labs, and corporate accelerators often have incentives misaligned with founder success -- they exist to create local jobs or hit quarterly KPIs, not to build billion-dollar companies. "No startup advice is better than bad startup advice." Founders should source advice from people who have actually started successful companies, not from people who studied or invested in them.

## Beginner's Mind and the Midwit Trap

Caldwell and Seibel use the "midwit meme" to illustrate how overthinking destroys startups ([Elon Musk and the midwit meme](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IW-dalton-michael-elon-musk-and-the-midwit-meme)). In the meme, the genius and the beginner reach the same conclusion, while the person with intermediate knowledge overthinks their way into paralysis. "What would an idiot do?" is a surprisingly reliable heuristic for the correct action.

They apply this to fundraising (raise what you need and get back to work), MVPs (build fast and ship), [[Cofounders|cofounder]] selection (work with your friend), and [[Product-Market Fit]] (you know it when you cannot sleep). The exception: "trap door decisions" involving law, regulation, or people's health require careful thought. For everything else, the founders who move fastest tend to win.

## Give More Than You Take

Caldwell and Seibel argue that the core of a good startup is creating more value than it captures ([How generosity built tech giants](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IO-dalton-michael-how-generosity-built-tech-giants)). Google Ads made early advertisers far more money than they paid Google. Excel made office workers 10-100x more productive. The founders who fear being "consultants" and avoid deeply understanding customer problems end up building products nobody wants.

The historical context reinforces the point: Linux, GCC, SQLite, HTML, and web browsers were all created by people giving away value. "If you leave a lot of the value on the table for your customers instead of trying to take it all, you're in the game."

## Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum Thinking

Caldwell and Seibel distinguish founders playing positive-sum games (building products that create new value) from those playing zero-sum games (capturing value from others) ([Game theory and startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ib-dalton-michael-what-basic-game-theory-teaches-us-about-startups)). The positive-sum founder's company keeps running even when markets drop. The zero-sum player -- dependent on rising asset prices, leverage, or information asymmetry -- collapses when the music stops.

Seibel frames FOMO as the primary temptation: seeing others get rich from zero-sum games while you build houses. The antidote is asking whether what you are building would survive a downturn. "If you're solving someone's real problem and the economy goes down, it's not like they still don't have that problem."

## Setting Goals That Matter

Caldwell and Seibel identify common goal-setting failures ([Setting smarter goals](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Id-dalton-michael-setting-smarter-goals)). Goals that are too aggressive become demotivating. Fake metrics (registered users instead of revenue, downloads instead of active users) create false confidence. Sandbagging -- setting easy goals to look impressive -- is rational in a big company but self-defeating when you are your own boss.

The "play stupid games, win stupid prizes" framework applies: raising as much money as possible wins the prize of losing board control and being unable to pivot. Hiring 50 employees wins the prize of painful layoffs. Scaling negative margins wins the prize of investors who stop returning calls. The goal should be to work on the hardest problem in the company, not to hit a number that impresses someone else.

## Founder Health and Lifestyle

Caldwell and Seibel argue that founders must "adult harder" than their peers ([Lifestyle habits of successful founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ii-dalton-michael-most-important-lifestyle-habits-of-successful-founders)). Sleep, diet, exercise, and sunlight form the 80/20 of preventative mental health. Social media consumption is an information diet that affects mood as much as food affects the body -- successful founders aggressively limit it.

When setbacks occur, the first question should be: "Do I have to do anything right now?" Sleeping on it is the minimum response. "No action" is one of the best founders' most-used plays -- 20-40% of the time, the right move is to do nothing. The best people respond to crises by hiking; the worst respond by drinking. Therapy and medication are destigmatized tools that many successful founders use but rarely discuss publicly.

For fatal punches -- when the startup truly fails -- Caldwell and Seibel tell founders: "Your startup failed. That doesn't mean you failed." The experience has value even if the company does not survive. But founders should not sacrifice their savings, families, or mental health past the point of recovery.

## The CEO Role Evolves Every Six Months

Houston describes the founder transition from coder to CEO as a series of identity crises. "Nobody tells you that your job as CEO changes every six months." At 20 people, you manage by intuition. At 50, that breaks. Houston recommends asking: "One year from now, what will I wish I had been learning?" He credits reading -- High Output Management (Andy Grove), The Effective Executive (Drucker), Poor Charlie's Almanack (Munger) -- and maintaining relationships with founders at different stages ([On starting and scaling Dropbox](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6S-on-starting-and-scaling-dropbox-yc-w07)).

Greene, who cofounded three startups including VMware ($19.1B market cap at IPO), uses a sailing metaphor: "Building a company is so much like racing a sailboat. You need a plan and then the ability to reevaluate the plan in real time." At VMware, she learned to make innovation non-disruptive to the established order. At Bebop, she learned that tenaciousness pays off when the initial design is wrong ([Diane Greene's advice for founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6Q-diane-greene-s-advice-for-founders)).

Collison distinguishes happiness from fulfillment. Running Stripe was "not especially happy" -- constant awareness of product deficiencies and investor skepticism. But it felt fulfilling. He recommends optimizing for fulfillment, while noting genuine unhappiness is a signal to reconsider ([Patrick Collison on effectively running a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/80-patrick-collison-on-effectively-running-a-startup)).

## Customer Obsession as Identity

Xu describes DoorDash's founding as following a thread discovered by watching a macaroon shop owner's daily life -- a booklet of turned-down delivery orders. The founders validated by doing deliveries themselves, a practice the entire company still performs annually twelve years later ([How to build the future: Tony Xu](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MJ-how-to-build-the-future-tony-xu)).

Lieb describes persistence through a different lens: Bump reached 150 million users but could not find a business model. Rather than quitting, he pivoted, got acquired by Google, and built Google Photos -- one billion users in four years. "The path to success is very rarely a straight line" ([How David Lieb turned a failing startup into Google Photos](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ly-how-david-lieb-turned-a-failing-startup-into-google-photos)).

Heller at Casetext spent ten years building AI legal tools. When large language models arrived, a decade of domain expertise let Casetext compress weeks of legal work into minutes. Sold for $650 million ([The 10-year overnight success story of Casetext](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/KJ-the-10-year-overnight-success-story-of-casetext)).

## Competitors Are Not the Threat

Seibel, Taggar, and Flora address founder obsession with competitors. The "most depressing fact" is that most likely everyone dies -- both you and your competitors fail. The questions that matter: are customers talking about the competitor, are you losing deals, and how does the product compare? ([How to compete with Amazon and Google](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iz-inside-the-group-partner-lounge-how-to-compete-with-amazon-and-google))

## Productivity as Survival

YC partners frame productivity as the core survival skill. The primary failure mode: spending time on accessible, fun tasks rather than the drudgery that moves the business. The fix: write down priorities, audit the calendar, resist tools that impose someone else's mental model ([Productivity tips](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/K5-yc-s-group-partners-share-productivity-tips-and-advice-on-actually-getting-things-done)).

## Irrational Optimism Plus Uncompromising Realism

Mohan (Windsurf CEO) describes the dual mindset required for a successful [[Pivoting|pivot]]: "irrational optimism because if you don't have the optimism, you just won't do anything," combined with "uncompromising realism, which is that when the facts change, you actually change your mind" ([Windsurf CEO](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MO-windsurf-ceo-betting-on-ai-agents-pivoting-in-48-hours-and-the-future-of-coding)). He pivoted from a profitable GPU infrastructure company to AI coding over a weekend. The tension between these two qualities is precisely what makes execution hard: "the thing that makes you succeed through irrational optimism is exactly opposite to the things that allow you to be a very realistic company."

Mohan also articulates a philosophy of depreciating insights: "Every single insight that we have is a depreciating insight. If we don't continually have insights that we are executing on, we are just slowly dying." He is comfortable with 50% of bets failing: if 100% succeed, the company is not trying hard enough.

## Build for the Love of Building

Palicha (Zepto CEO, age 22) advises his 17-year-old self: "Really do it for the love of building. The end goal should not be I build so I can make XYZ. I'm building so that I have the opportunity to wake up in the morning and build again" ([How Zepto became India's fastest growing startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/ML-how-zepto-became-india-s-fastest-growing-startup)). Zepto went from a WhatsApp group to $3B+ GMV and $5B valuation.

Pei (Nothing founder) echoes from hardware: "There is a beauty to not having another option. It forces you to survive" ([Nothing founder Carl Pei](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LO-how-nothing-founder-carl-pei-built-a-multi-million-dollar-smartphone-brand-in-just-2-years)). When 90% of his first earbud shipment failed, he put 15 engineers on the factory floor until quality recovered. Nothing reached $600M annualized revenue within two years.

## Follow the Scaling Line

Truell (Cursor CEO) describes the conviction that drove Cursor: "follow the line" -- plan for where the scaling laws predict models will be, not where they are today ([Cursor CEO](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MU-cursor-ceo-going-beyond-code-superintelligent-ai-agents-and-why-taste-still-matters)). He warns against optimizing for the demo. The Cursor team dogfooded relentlessly and tracked paid power users rather than vanity metrics. They hired extremely slowly: "If you really nail the first 10 people, they will both accelerate you in the future" and act as an immune system against bad hires.

## The Long Game and Burnout

Altman argues that very few founders make an actual long-term commitment. Those who plan for a 10-year journey have a huge advantage ([Later stage advice for startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4q-later-stage-advice-for-startups)). But the long game requires sustainability. Founders who go three or four years without vacation will burn out. Altman advises monitoring the team for burnout, encouraging vacation, and providing new challenges.

As success grows, so does criticism. "People start rooting against you. Journalists and people on the internet will find ways to pick apart what you're doing." Learning to ignore detractors early is essential. Losing focus -- to burnout, to potential acquirers, or to distractions -- destroys the advantage that focus created in the first place.
