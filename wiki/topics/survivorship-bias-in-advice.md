# Survivorship Bias in Startup Advice

Prominent technology leaders frequently give advice that contradicts the path they actually followed to become successful. [[Dalton Caldwell]] and [[Michael Seibel]] call this the "do as I say, not as I did" problem and argue that founders who take famous people's advice at face value -- without researching their actual histories -- are making a dangerous mistake [1].

## The Pattern

Caldwell introduces the pattern: a successful person gives advice that sounds reasonable but does not reflect what they did to become successful. The audience, often in their twenties, only knows these people as successes. They have no reason to question the advice. Seibel, who has known many of these people personally, describes the disconnect: "We saw what they did. We know their story. And we're just like, 'That's weird. You didn't do that'" [1].

## Three Examples

### Sam Altman

Seibel describes Altman recently suggesting that the YC-style approach of starting small may not be optimal -- that founders should think bigger, raise more money, hire teams, and build labs [1]. Caldwell traces Altman's actual path: his first startup was Loopt, a mobile social product to see where your friends were in the city. He went to Stanford, did YC, raised a small amount of money, had a small team, and iterated. Caldwell describes it as "a pretty garden variety path" with a "pretty normal outcome" [1].

Caldwell argues Altman could not have started OpenAI as his first company. He needed the network, credibility, and experience that Loopt and YC provided. "If you actually wanted to emulate Sam, you would not immediately go try to start a lab and raise a hundred million dollars. Because that's not at all what he did" [1].

Seibel adds that the people most influenced by Altman's "raise a hundred million" advice are precisely the people who will not be able to do it. "If you're one of the people out there that can't do a startup because they're like, 'If I can't raise a hundred million dollars, why even bother?' -- that's not real" [1].

### Elon Musk

Seibel describes Musk advocating for ambitious hardware projects -- atoms over bits, going to space, fixing the world. Seibel recounts a direct conversation with Musk about his actual path. Musk said he wanted to go to space, knew it required a lot of money, and came to Silicon Valley during the dot-com boom specifically to make money fast through conventional software startups (Zip2, then X.com/PayPal). Caldwell notes: "His ideas were pretty conventional. It wasn't hard tech. It wasn't deep tech" [1].

Caldwell explains that Musk leveraged his wealth and network from those exits to start SpaceX, and repeatedly bailed out Tesla with personal funds. "If he were not an extremely wealthy person, there's no way Tesla could have existed" [1].

Caldwell concludes: "Most mere mortals out in the world need to do step one and can't skip to step two, even though step two sounds cooler. Mere mortals, including Elon -- literally Elon himself -- was a mere mortal" [1].

### Peter Thiel

Seibel describes Thiel as known for critiquing college. But he attended Stanford for both undergraduate and law school, worked at a famous law firm and a famous bank. "He went to college. And not only did he go to college -- he went to college" [1].

Seibel acknowledges Thiel's critique is valid in parts. But the A/B test does not exist. "My sense is there was some aspect of those experiences that had a profound impact on his later success" [1].

## Why Leaders Give This Advice

Caldwell assumes positive intent. These are successful people trying to help. Three mechanisms explain the disconnect [1]:

1. **Blindness to backstory.** Caldwell observes that they are so used to their own history that they "almost become blind to it, just like we can." What they focus on is what they would do now, given what they know now.

2. **Advice they wish they could tell their younger selves.** Seibel suspects much of this advice is what these people wish they had done differently. Altman might have raised more money sooner. Thiel might have skipped the law firm. But those were formative experiences that may have been necessary.

3. **Context collapse.** Seibel explains that videos and blog posts cannot be personalized. YC office hours are personalized -- "we read their application, we interviewed them, we accepted them." Public advice is aimed at the void. The nuance that would make it useful for a specific person is lost.

## The Billion-Dollar Origin Stories

A complementary YC discussion reveals that the biggest companies all had humble, indistinguishable beginnings [2]. Multiple group partners share stories of interviewing companies that became massive.

**Solugen** (industrial chemicals, now a major company) showed up to their YC interview with a beaker of hydrogen peroxide -- most of the hydrogen peroxide they had ever produced. During the batch, they sold bottles from their garage. [[Jared Friedman]], who interviewed them, was convinced not by the technology but because "these guys were doers. They weren't waiting for anyone's permission to go do the thing. They had made the hydrogen peroxide and brought it in, and they were trying to find somebody to sell it to. They clearly had a bias for action" [2].

**Amplitude** (now a public analytics company) applied with a voice-to-text Android app. Paul Buchheit "hammered" the founder Spencer, arguing Google would crush them. Spencer was "just so intense in his comebacks and just determined to work on this idea." They pivoted a year and a half after YC but that intensity was the signal [2].

[[Michael Seibel]] identifies a key trait shared by these founders: obstinance. "There's almost like a sort of obstinance about the best founders where they're just, whatever they're working on, they don't do it at like 60 or 70 or even 80 percent. They're always just like 100 percent committed" [2].

**Segment** ($3.2 billion acquisition by Twilio) applied with a tool for professors to poll students during lectures. Seibel recounts: "No one wanted this. It had zero traction." The partners pushed them to work on something technically hard, but the founders were obstinate -- "not obstinate without direction. They were like, 'What we are really passionate about is fixing education, and we think this is a really good place to start.'" Eventually they found the Segment idea, but it took time [2].

**Brex** started as a VR startup. Seibel also cites this as an example of a founder entering YC with an objectively bad idea but possessing the intensity to find the right one [2].

Diana Hu and Seibel discuss the founder archetype that succeeds at these pivots. Hu argues that the best founders are "very not well-rounded. They're very quirky people for a good reason" -- by definition, building a successful large company requires being "out of multiple standard deviations out of the norm." Seibel adds that the strategy that got founders into elite schools and FAANG jobs -- hedging, being well-rounded -- "is not going to be the strategy that actually gets them to be successful as a startup founder" [2].

**[[Brad Flora]]** adds that the best founders at interview are clear, succinct, and honest about what they do not know. He uses the Jeeves interview as an example: the application had two sentences for progress: "We've completed a deal with our first bank partner and are ready to start onboarding initial customers." Flora was impressed by the confidence to say so little: "All of those things are in your control. You can write succinctly if you choose to." During the interview itself, the Jeeves founders were "pretty upfront about things that they did know and didn't know. And they didn't try to razzle-dazzle us with fake traction" [2].

Aaron Epstein warns founders against comparing themselves to successful companies in their current state. "The stories that you read in the press and in the media, has this PR gloss over it where they're only showing the positives." He advises founders going through tough times to "imagine the story that they want to tell in ten years, and how boring it would be if everything was just up and to the right and successful the whole time" [2].

## How YC Tries to Avoid the Trap

Three practices [1]:

1. **Tell their own full stories.** Caldwell describes how at the beginning of each batch, partners share their complete backstories -- everything they screwed up, every mistake. "It helps people get to know us, to contextualize us as humans. And it helps to know how many mistakes you can make and still do something interesting."

2. **Give advice they would give their own children.** Caldwell states: "If I tell this to my kids, probably it's good for you too." Seibel attended Yale and was not impressed, but recognizes it is where he met his co-founders: "Without them, I wouldn't be here right now. So if that's the only thing Yale did for me, it changed my life."

3. **Personalize advice in office hours.** Seibel explains that in private, YC partners sometimes give advice that contradicts the public YC canon because the founder's specific situation warrants it. A multi-time founder known to be a strong fundraiser might be advised to raise more money. "That doesn't apply to most people" [1].
