# Sales and Distribution

## Enterprise Sales

Junee reframes enterprise sales as hacking a complex system. "A large enterprise is a complex network of people and processes — and it can be understood and influenced in the same way" as a computer system ([Enterprise sales for hackers](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4m-enterprise-sales-for-hackers)). The best [[Early-Stage Tactics|early-stage]] enterprise sellers are hackers who learn the system, not "script kiddies" running a repeatable playbook.

**Stakeholder mapping** is essential. Each enterprise deal involves a champion (your internal advocate), detractors, IT, procurement, legal, and finance — each with distinct incentives. Champions are gold: Junee advises building relationships deep enough that you can text them any time to revive a stalled deal ([Enterprise sales for hackers](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4m-enterprise-sales-for-hackers)).

**[[Hiring]] salespeople:** match to your company stage. "Coin-operated" reps from big companies are the wrong fit at early stage. You need people comfortable with uncertainty who can learn the system ([Enterprise sales for hackers](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4m-enterprise-sales-for-hackers)).

## The B2B Sales Progression

Blomfield describes the typical founder sales journey as a sequence of stages, from worst to best ([The sales playbook for founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Mo-the-sales-playbook-for-founders)):

**Design partnerships** are the most common trap. They are too long (3-6 months), poorly defined, and suffer from low customer engagement. 90% of founders get stuck here. The fix: identify a narrow burning problem, build a wedge product in 48 hours, bring it back. Do not overbuild a broad platform.

**Free trials/pilots** must have defined success metrics. A customer service AI pilot should measure: "Can you solve 20% of inbound queries?" The internal champion uses the proven value equation to justify the purchase to their CFO.

**Paid pilots** get financial commitment upfront, which forces customer engagement. Ask about willingness to pay for the full product before the pilot starts. Keep pilots as short as possible -- seven to 14 days. Book the post-pilot meeting before the pilot begins.

**Recurring revenue contracts with opt-out.** The pro move: a monthly or annual contract with a 30-60 day money-back guarantee. If the customer does nothing, it converts to a full contract. One sales process, recurring revenue.

Blomfield emphasizes tracking time-to-first-value as a north star metric. Never do a full API integration during a pilot -- use Excel imports, email the data, do whatever gets value in front of the customer fastest.

## Cold Outbound as Validation

Zaparde (Zip, YC S20) decided his first 10 customers would come entirely from cold outreach -- no referrals, no friends -- to prove genuine market demand ([Building a $2 billion SaaS company](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/M1-building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-founder)). He maxed out LinkedIn connections daily, messaged those who accepted, and initially asked for advice rather than selling. This produced 107 pages of customer discovery notes in two weeks. He then converted those conversations into sales by returning with products built from their feedback.

## The Enterprise Sales Funnel

Koomen provides a step-by-step guide to enterprise sales from his experience scaling Optimizely to $100M ARR ([Enterprise sales for founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LF-enterprise-sales-for-founders)). He structures the process as a funnel: prospecting, outreach, qualification, [[Pricing|pricing]], closing, and implementation.

**Prospecting** starts with a sales hypothesis: "Customer X has problem Y, and our product will help them solve it." Use tools like Builtwith, Apollo, and LinkedIn Sales Navigator to build a target list. The biggest anti-pattern: talking to anyone who will take your call rather than pursuing ideal customers. "Talking to bad customers gives you the illusion that you're making progress when you're not."

**First calls** should focus on qualifying, not pitching. Ask questions: What made you take this call? How long have you had this problem? Who else does it affect? What is your budget? How does your organization buy software? Koomen argues: "Great salespeople spend most of their time listening."

**Demos** should tell a story, not tour features. Start with the customer's problem (demonstrating you listened), then show exactly how the product solves it. Personalize with the customer's logo, website, and team names. Koomen notes the Optimizely team spent weeks building a feature that demoed directly on the prospect's website rather than a dummy site.

**Implementation** is the founder's responsibility, not the customer's. Optimizely discovered that six-figure customers sometimes ran zero A/B tests because the marketing team that bought the software could not convince engineering to install it. Koomen learned to treat customer implementation like an internal engineering project: shared roadmap, task owners, regular check-ins. "Your sales funnel only really ends when your customer is using your product habitually."

## Cold Email Conversion

Epstein provides a detailed framework for cold outreach ([How to convert customers with cold emails](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LZ-how-to-convert-customers-with-cold-emails)). The math is stark: at typical conversion rates, 800 emails produce one customer. Founders should plan to send dozens of emails per day, manually at first.

Seven principles for effective cold emails: (1) focused single goal -- move to the next funnel step only; (2) be human -- use emotion words, informal language, even typos; (3) personalize beyond template variables -- show genuine research; (4) keep it short -- readable on a mobile phone; (5) establish credibility -- YC, past employers, customer logos; (6) focus on the reader, not yourself -- reframe "I" as "you"; (7) clear call to action as its own paragraph at the end.

The highest-leverage improvement is better targeting, not better copy. "It's better to send 100 targeted emails than 1,000 untargeted emails." Follow up 2-4 times. Warm intros convert at 2-3X the rate of cold emails.

## Influencing Decision Makers

Caldwell and Seibel address the broader skill of influencing decisions that happen when you are not in the room ([How to influence decision makers](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LQ-how-to-influence-decision-makers)). Track record and being "irreplaceable" matter most. Desperation is detectable and counterproductive -- this is why the YC deal giving founders $500K changed [[Fundraising|fundraising]] dynamics by removing desperation. Seibel advises: "How you respond to a decision not going your way will be judged too."

## Press and PR

Seibel argues that founders should build reporter relationships directly. "Reporters want to talk to CEOs and [[Cofounders|co-founders]] — not PR people." His own company wasted over $100K on PR agencies before learning this ([Getting press for your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4c-getting-press-for-your-startup)).

**Press cadence** beats big splashes. One story every 1-2 months is more effective than one mega-story per year. Maintain a queue of 3-5 upcoming news items. Seibel identifies four categories of startup news: [[Product Development|product]] launches, milestones, significant BD deals, and [[Fundraising|fundraising]] ([Getting press for your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4c-getting-press-for-your-startup)).

**Offer exclusives.** Almost all startups should pitch their story to one reporter at a time rather than blasting many outlets. PR should consume no more than 5% of the CEO's time, and benefits are typically delayed by weeks or months ([Getting press for your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4c-getting-press-for-your-startup)).
