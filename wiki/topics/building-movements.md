# Building Movements

[[Garry Tan]] argues that every billion-dollar startup begins as a movement -- "a team and a direction and a thing that helps millions and possibly billions of people" [1]. He draws parallels between punk rock, the personal computer revolution, and startup founding to explain how movements emerge, grow, and eventually change the world.

## Steve Jobs on Shaping the World

Tan opens with Jobs's 1994 observation: "Everything around you that you call life was made up by people that were no smarter than you. The minute that you understand that you can poke life and actually something will pop out the other side -- that you can change it, you can mold it -- that's maybe the most important thing" [1].

## Stage One: Emergence

Movements begin as "social ferment" -- small groups of passionate people working on something nobody else cares about [1]. The personal computer started this way: in the 1970s, only governments, universities, and large businesses had computers. Hobbyist clubs like the Homebrew Computer Club in San Francisco were where enthusiasts showed off home-brewed machines. "From these modest beginnings came a series of startup companies" [1].

Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak were two of those hobbyists. Wozniak designed the computer; Jobs worried about which parts to use and how to make it buildable. "I was not designing a computer with any idea we'd ever start a company. It was just to go down to the club and show off" [1]. Jobs saw the interest and started thinking about how to turn it into a product.

Tan distills the lesson: "What do you want that nobody else cares about yet? What problem do you see first? Try to solve it for yourself. Then you might find that a lot of other people need it too" [1].

## The First Follower

Citing Derek Sivers's TED talk, Tan argues that the first follower is more important than the leader: "The first follower is what transforms a lone nut into a leader" [1]. Critical dynamics:

- The leader must embrace the first follower as an equal -- "So now it's not about the leader anymore. It's about them, plural" [1].
- New followers emulate followers, not the leader [1].
- Three is a crowd, and a crowd is news. After three, there is momentum [1].
- As more people join, it becomes less risky for fence-sitters to participate [1].

The implication for startups: "You could be the first leader or the first follower, but either way, you've got to have intrinsic motivation -- really wanting it from the bottom of your heart, not because someone else told you to" [1].

## DIY: Do It Yourself

Tan draws the deepest parallel between punk rock and startups through the DIY aesthetic. Punk rock's power was not musical complexity (many songs used only three chords) but authenticity and self-sufficiency [1]:

- "You don't wait for someone to give you something. You don't wait for someone to teach you how to play. You don't wait for a record deal. You just pick up a guitar and you do it. You pick up a keyboard and you start writing code" [1].
- Kurt Cobain of Nirvana: "We usually just play really cheap equipment, and a lot of times before a show we're scrambling for guitar parts and gluing things together just to have a guitar for that night" [1].

Tan connects this directly to [[Brian Armstrong]] building the first version of Coinbase with Twitter Bootstrap -- "he begged, borrowed, and stole the skills necessary to get a website up. He didn't look for a designer. He used the free, open-source Twitter Bootstrap library, and it worked great" [1].

The principle: "Humans have a visceral reaction to experiencing authenticity, even at the cost of perfection. Everyone is so focused on being perfect these days, and to that I say, fuck perfection. Do it yourself" [1].

## You Don't Need Permission

Tan identifies waiting for perfect conditions as the most common obstacle: "I can't tell you how often it is that people waiting for perfect conditions is what actually prevents them from starting in the first place" [1].

His historical examples: "If Steve Jobs looked to Hewlett-Packard for a playbook, he never would have started Apple. If Brian Armstrong looked to big finance for approval, he never would have started Coinbase. If the Velvet Underground just copied whatever was mainstream at the time, the rock and roll greats like the Ramones and Nirvana would never have emerged. To be first, you have to really want it. You can't ask for permission" [1].
