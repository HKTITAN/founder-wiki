# Cofounders

YC data shows that only 7.9% of YC's top companies were founded by solo founders. Graham lists "single founder" as mistake #1 in his "18 Mistakes" essay ([The 18 mistakes that kill startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8t-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups)). The canon is clear: cofounders matter, and finding the right one is among the most consequential decisions a founder makes.

## Where to Find Cofounders

Graham and Seibel both argue that school is by far the best place to find cofounders ([A student's guide to startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/93-a-student-s-guide-to-startups)). The window closes quickly after graduation — the density of ambitious, available, risk-tolerant people drops sharply once people enter full-time jobs.

YC's cofounder matching platform has produced successful results. Sequin and Kiwi Biosciences both met through the platform and were accepted into YC S21. Li describes a "trial project approach" where matched cofounders work together on a real project before committing ([Does co-founder matching work](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Cq-does-co-founder-matching-work)). This mirrors [[Early-Stage Tactics]] — test before you commit.

## Evaluating Compatibility

YC recommends discussing 10 key questions before committing: personal goals, roles and CEO designation, equity split, location, pivot willingness, full-time triggers, personal finances, working schedule, team culture vision, and disagreement resolution ([10 questions to discuss with a potential co-founder](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Hj-10-questions-to-discuss-with-a-potential-co-founder)).

Kiwi Biosciences took a "very structured approach" — answering all 50 questions from First Round's cofounder questionnaire and finding deep alignment on conflict resolution and culture before committing. YC advises: "No questionnaire can be a substitute for actually spending a lot of time together in person. It usually takes several months."

Amy Buechler recommends "founder syncs" — recurring weekly 1-on-1s specifically about how the cofounder relationship is going, designed to surface tensions before they become breakups. This is analogous to the 1:1 practice in [[Management]].

## Equity and Roles

The equity split should reflect expected future contributions over the 7-10 year marathon ahead, not past contributions. This is especially important for scientific founders who may feel their prior research entitles them to a larger share.

Deciding who is CEO early prevents ambiguity. YC advises treating cofounder selection with the same rigor as [[Hiring]] — including reference calls on each other ([10 questions to discuss with a potential co-founder](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Hj-10-questions-to-discuss-with-a-potential-co-founder)).

## What Kills Cofounder Relationships

Graham identifies fights between founders as mistake #17 ([The 18 mistakes that kill startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8t-the-18-mistakes-that-kill-startups)). In "The Hardest Lessons," he names internal disputes as one of three self-inflicted causes of startup death (alongside inertia and ignoring users). Cofounder conflict is one of the most common reasons startups fail before they run out of money — making it a [[Financial Survival]] threat that does not show up on a balance sheet.

A strong cofounder relationship also supports the [[Founder Mindset]] required to endure the years of uncertainty that building a startup demands. Graham argues in "Why to not not start a startup" that having a cofounder makes the emotional burden bearable ([Why to not not start a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8x-why-to-not-not-start-a-startup)).
