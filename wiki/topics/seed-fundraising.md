# Seed Fundraising

Seed fundraising is the process by which startup founders raise initial outside capital to fund operations, growth, and product development. Most startups require external capital because the cost of reaching profitability exceeds what founders and their personal networks can finance [1]. Geoff Ralston, drawing on his experience at YC, describes fundraising as "brutal" but a path "almost all companies and founders must walk" [1].

## When to Raise

Investors write checks when three conditions align: the idea is compelling, the founding team appears capable of executing, and the opportunity is real and sufficiently large [1]. For most founders, this requires having a product with measurable traction. Ralston specifies that a growth rate of 10% per week for several weeks is "impressive" and sufficient to attract investor interest [1].

Seibel quantifies this: "In 97 percent of the time, the demo, the product, the MVP, the MVP with customers gives you so much more leverage when fundraising" [4]. Founders who fundraise before having metrics are often engaged in what Seibel identifies as fear-based decision-making -- they secretly believe their product is bad and want to raise before the world figures it out [4]. See [[Fundraising Mistakes]] for the full treatment.

## Where to Raise

Location matters. [[Michael Seibel]] explains that Bay Area investors behave differently from investors in most other locations because they have experienced the pain of passing on companies that became very successful [3]. This makes them more willing to dig into execution quality rather than dismiss ideas out of hand [3]. Founders who have been discouraged by local investor feedback should consider whether the same pitch would perform differently in Silicon Valley or another active startup community [3]. See [[Silicon Valley Fundraising]] for the full analysis.

## How Much to Raise

The ideal amount is enough to reach profitability [1]. When that is not realistic, the target should be enough to reach the next "fundable" milestone, typically 12 to 18 months away [1].

Caldwell relays Brian Chesky's (Airbnb) reframe: "Maybe money's more like food. You definitely need food to survive, but people are dying from too much food as opposed to too little food" [4]. Caldwell observes that companies that raise less tend to have founders who own more at exit and are more innovative because constraint forces creativity [4]. See [[Fundraising Mistakes]] for case studies of Facebook and Google's leverage-based approach.

## How to Reach Investors

### Cold Email

[[Michael Seibel]] provides detailed guidance on cold emailing investors. The core principle: the goal of a cold email is to get a reply, not to close an investment [5]. Keep the email under 60 seconds of reading time. Include the problem, solution, traction, market size, team composition, and a controversial insight. Use plain language, send from a company email, and track opens [5]. See [[Cold Emailing Investors]] for the complete playbook.

### Warm Introductions

The best way to meet investors remains warm introductions. Demo days provide a concentrated opportunity to meet motivated seed investors [1].

## Building the Seed Pitch Deck

[[Aaron Harris]] provides YC's canonical seed deck template, emphasizing that "founders should strive for clarity and concision" [2]. Harris distills the philosophy to a single line: "Focus on narrative. The rest is commentary" [2]. See the existing article for the full 10-slide template.


## Modeling the Raise Decision (Austen Allred / Lambda School)

[[Austen Allred]] was anti-VC after his first company's Series A fell through on December 23rd with documents already signed. For Lambda School (already profitable), he modeled the trade-off: the dilution (~15%) would enable 10x growth. "That's an easy trade off if you actually model it out" [6]. Lambda School raised $48 million within a year and a half [6].

## The Deal Isn't Done Until the Money Is in the Bank (Ooshma Garg / Gobble)

[[Ooshma Garg]] structured her entire first Series A attempt around a single investor who wanted to lead. She met only five investors instead of forty. After the partner meeting, the lead investor could not get partnership support. Lessons: "I didn't meet many different investors. I met five, not forty. I thought that a deal was done before it was done" [7]. She succeeded on the third attempt -- from the same firms that had previously said no [7].

## The Fundraising Conversation: Jess Lee and Aaron Harris

[[Jess Lee]] (Sequoia Capital) and [[Aaron Harris]] (YC) provide a joint perspective on the fundraising process, combining the investor's evaluation criteria with the founder's tactical playbook [NEW].

### What Sequoia Looks For

Three qualities [NEW]:
1. **A special founder with unique insight.** Sequoia has backed Apple, Google, Yahoo, Airbnb, Dropbox, and Stripe. Lee looks for founders with a unique perspective that others do not have.
2. **A clear understanding of the problem.** Many founders pitch solutions without explaining the problem. "It should be so crisp and clear what the problem is that the solution almost just flows from that."
3. **A really, really big market.** Line of sight to billions of dollars of market cap.

### Grit Takes Many Forms

Lee identifies multiple manifestations of grit [NEW]:
- **Passion for the problem**: An inspiring origin story where the founder must solve this problem.
- **Track record**: Demonstrated ability to "run through walls" in previous work or ventures.
- **Naive optimism**: "Sometimes it's almost better to be a little naive and inexperienced because you don't know how hard it's going to be. You just keep going."
- **Chip on shoulder**: Being underestimated drives relentless effort to prove oneself.

### Running a Process

Harris advises treating fundraising as a structured process [NEW]:
- Build a spreadsheet with every person you want to talk to
- Track when you reached out, responses, meeting outcomes
- Probability-weight each conversation by amount and likelihood
- Talk to people in parallel to create a competitive dynamic

Lee concurs: "You can't go into fundraising as a casual side thing that you do 20%. Go all in on the process." Be transparent about timelines: "I'm hoping to get this done by this date." Keeping everyone informed creates competitive pressure [NEW].

### The Seed-A Line Is Blurring

Lee notes that seed rounds are getting larger and priced higher. Polyvore's Series A in 2007 was $2.5 million -- "now that seems more like a seed." The distinction between seed and Series A is increasingly fuzzy [NEW].

Harris warns against optimizing for valuation: "People who get hung up on the amount of money or the price are doing a disservice to the company." Raising too much at too high a valuation sets the hurdle too high [NEW].

### Choosing Investors

Lee advises evaluating investors on four dimensions [NEW]:
1. **Valuation and terms** -- important but often over-optimized
2. **The individual partner** -- will they be a shock absorber in lows and a sparring partner in highs?
3. **The firm's brand and network**
4. **The founder community** -- peers who elevate your game

The critical framing: "You can't kick your investors off your cap table very easily. It's almost like someone you're going to be married to for 10 years, except you can't get divorced" [NEW].

### Investor Red Flags

Lee describes three bad investor models [NEW]:
- **The cheerleader**: Just wants to be best friends and confirms everything. Not optimal.
- **The uninvolved**: Takes your money and disappears.
- **The too-prescriptive**: Tells you exactly where UI buttons should go based on their sister's feedback.

The ideal: constructive feedback without being adversarial. Lee's Sequoia partner Brian Shrier "was super supportive about the good things. On the things that needed fixing, he didn't yell at me, but he pointed them out logically" [NEW].

### Counterintuitive: Avoid Investor Office Space

Harris advises against accepting free office space from investors. The investor's proximity inhibits experimentation: "If you know that your investor is going to look at your spreadsheet or product, you're not going to try the super weird, crazy idea that needs room to grow" [NEW].

### When to Raise

At the earliest stage (friends and family), the tipping point is personal commitment: being genuinely okay with the near-certainty that the money will be lost. "There's got to be enough personal commitment" [NEW].

At later stages, milestones provide guidelines but they are fuzzy. For SaaS, roughly $1 million ARR growing 20-30% monthly might signal Series A readiness, but "you can never look at someone else's experience fundraising and say that perfectly ports over to me" [NEW].

### Investor Incentive Dynamics

Harris identifies a structural tension: "If you think about the incentive dynamic, it is in the investor's best interest to wait as long as possible before making a decision, because they gather more evidence. But as a founder, that's bad for you" [NEW].
