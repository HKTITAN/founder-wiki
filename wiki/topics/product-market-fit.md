# Product-Market Fit

Product-market fit is the state where a product satisfies strong market demand. Rusenko calls it "the top problem that anyone deals with" and ranks it as the hardest challenge in a startup, ahead of [[Hiring|hiring a world-class team]] and far ahead of monetization ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)). Seibel describes PMF more viscerally: "If you're asking, you don't have it." When PMF arrives, the founder stops thinking about new features and distribution and instead struggles just to keep the product online for the users flooding in ([How to plan an MVP](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6f-how-to-plan-an-mvp)).

## Measuring PMF with Retention

Alstromer argues that retention is the single best, least biased measure of product-market fit ([Growth for startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6k-growth-for-startups)). The method: identify the metric that represents the core value of the product (bookings for Airbnb, rides for Lyft, payroll runs for Gusto), determine the natural frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, annual), then plot a cohort retention curve. If the curve flattens — meaning some percentage of users continues returning indefinitely — the product has PMF for those users. If the curve trends to zero, it does not.

DoorDash retained 21% of users 20 months after signup. GitHub retained 30% after 60 months. Both indicate strong PMF. Net Promoter Score, surveys, registered user counts, visitor counts, and conversion rates are all poor substitutes — they do not capture whether users come back ([Growth for startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6k-growth-for-startups)).

A key distinction: if a product is expected to charge money, free usage does not demonstrate PMF. "If someone says, 'I love this if it's free, but if it costs money, I'm not gonna use it,' that's pretty bad" (Alstromer). See [[Pricing]] for related advice on charging early.

## The Timeline to PMF

Rusenko documents Weebly's path: 14 months in, the user graph was trending downward despite press in TechCrunch and Newsweek. At 18 months, still no PMF. The inflection came at 20 months, when organic daily signups began exceeding what any press spike had generated ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)). This timeline has implications for [[Financial Survival]]: if it takes 20-30 iterations, founders must keep burn low enough to survive that many cycles.

Rusenko estimates that however many iterations a founder expects it will take, they should multiply by ten. This means outsourcing development rarely works — outsourcing buys one shot, but PMF requires twenty or thirty ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)).

## How to Find PMF

Rusenko breaks the process into a literal loop ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)):

1. Talk to customers and develop a market thesis
2. Listen to their problems, not their solutions
3. Rapid prototype and user-test
4. Build a solution to their problems
5. Test the solution with them
6. If it did not work, return to step 1

He emphasizes UX testing sessions as the most important tool: sit users in front of the product, ask them to perform a task, and do not touch the keyboard or speak until they complete it. Three to five sessions reveal the most critical usability failures.

Seibel adds a problem-definition checklist: Can you state the problem in two sentences? Have you experienced it yourself? Can you define it narrowly? Is it solvable? ([Building product](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7s-building-product)). He recommends targeting desperate customers first — "whose business is gonna go out of business without using you?" — rather than impressive but non-desperate prospects.

## The Uncanny Valley of PMF

Caldwell identifies a dangerous middle zone: a startup with a little traction but no real PMF. A complete failure frees founders to pivot immediately. A small amount of traction traps them. "I've seen a little bit of traction be a trap that has captured a lot of really talented people for long periods of time" ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)). See [[Pivoting]] for more on recognizing when to change ideas.

## Creating Markets

Rusenko notes that the best companies create their own market — Airbnb, iPhone, Weebly. By definition, market research cannot identify a market that does not yet exist. The pattern: find a hidden need, build a product that initially looks like a worse version of something in an existing category, but actually serves a new population of users entirely ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)). The iPhone did not compete with existing smartphones. It made everyone a smartphone user.

## Minimum Remarkable Product

Rusenko prefers "minimum remarkable product" over "minimum viable product." The standard: launch when the product is better than what is currently available. Not perfect — but better. Paul Buchheit's formulation: "Launch when your product is better than what's out there" ([How to find product-market fit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7V-how-to-find-product-market-fit)). See [[Launching]] and [[Early-Stage Tactics]] for more on launch timing.
