# Becoming a Founder

Multiple YC sources address the pre-founding question: should you start a startup, and when? Graham, Seibel, and YC staff collectively address 16+ reasons people hesitate, what college students should do to prepare, and how the founder path compares to alternatives.

## Dismantling Excuses

Graham's "Why to not not start a startup" systematically evaluates 16 reasons people don't start companies ([Why to not not start a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8x-why-to-not-not-start-a-startup)). Most are not valid:

**Too young.** The lower bound may be as low as 16. Sam Altman was 19 when YC funded him. Graham identifies two tests of adult readiness: whether you've lost the "kid flake reflex" (crying "I can't" to get out of hard things), and whether you respond to challenges as an equal rather than from a position of submission.

**Too inexperienced.** Graham reversed his earlier view that founders should be at least 23. "The best way to get experience if you're 21 is to start a startup." A normal job may actually reduce startup readiness by turning people into "tame animals."

**Not smart enough.** "If you're smart enough to worry that you might not be smart enough to start a startup, you probably are." Most startups require effort more than intelligence.

**Know nothing about business.** "You don't need to know anything about business to start a startup." The initial focus is product. Revenue follows from making something people want.

**No idea.** 70% of YC startup ideas change in the first three months. YC cares more about builders than ideas. See [[Startup Ideas]].

The valid reasons: having a family to support (#9), being independently wealthy and unmotivated (#10), and not being ready for a multi-year commitment (#11).

Graham frames the broader argument historically: jobs as the default way to make a living are only about a hundred years old. Startups may represent another shift comparable to the move from farming to manufacturing.

## The Student Advantage

Graham identifies five advantages young founders have: stamina, poverty, rootlessness, access to [[Cofounders|cofounders]], and ignorance ([A student's guide to startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/93-a-student-s-guide-to-startups)).

**Poverty** is an advantage because living cheaply reduces burn rate. The Apple II was cheap because Wozniak designed it for himself and couldn't afford anything more. Low cost creates products that are affordable and therefore popular.

**Cofounders access** is the scarcest resource. School concentrates smart, ambitious, unattached people. By 30, potential cofounders are tied to jobs and families. Google, Yahoo, and Microsoft were all founded by people who met in school.

**Ignorance** acts as a counterweight to intimidation. Wozniak: "All the best things that I did at Apple came from (a) not having money and (b) not having done it before, ever."

The biggest disadvantage of student founders: building things that look like class projects. Graham identifies two missing elements: (1) an iterative definition of a real problem, and (2) intensity. "The market doesn't give a shit how hard you worked."

Despite these advantages, YC's official policy shifted to telling undergrads to wait until graduation. The reasoning: graduation changes the prevailing winds of social pressure. A startup during college reads as a summer job; after graduation, it reads as your identity.

## What to Do in College

Seibel advises college students to learn to code, meet people with real skills, take leadership roles, and be their own career counselor ([A letter to college students](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4d-a-letter-to-college-students)). He warns against four decisions that block later startup attempts: not learning to code, never befriending technical people, accumulating expenses early, and settling in a non-startup city.

"If you are between the ages of 22 and 32 you are in your best startup years. Every year you are not doing a startup you are losing 10% of this prime time."

Khemani and Manalac's student guide recommends three self-assessment questions from Seibel: Do I like being the underdog? Do I seek hard challenges? Do I thrive taking personal responsibility? ([Startups for students](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/D0-startups-for-students))

## Three Career Paths

Seibel frames the tech career as three options: founder, executive, or employee ([Three paths in the tech industry](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4h-three-paths-in-the-tech-industry-founder-executive-or-employee)). He attaches no value judgment to any path. The founder path requires accumulating prerequisites: technical teammates, financial runway, a problem you care about, and an idea. Experience is "massively over-valued" -- most founders learn what they need after starting.

The executive path requires either picking a fast-growing company early (like joining Facebook's first 100 employees) or moving diagonally up between established companies. The employee path offers stability and more time for personal life but less control and wealth.

## What YC Actually Looks For

Graham's "Billionaires build" reveals what YC evaluates in interviews: whether founders understand their users deeply enough to make what they want ([Billionaires build](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/9M-billionaires-build)). The best answer to "how do you know people want this?" is "because we and our friends want it" -- followed by evidence of a crude prototype spreading by word of mouth.

Graham introduces the concept of a "larval market" -- a small but growable market. Apple in 1976, Facebook at Harvard, Stripe with programmers. The crucial feature is that the initial market exists. "There have to be some people who want what you're building right now, and want it so urgently that they're willing to use it, bugs and all."

What keeps the best founders going long-term is not money but genuine interest in what they are building. "One thing few people realize about billionaires is that all of them could have stopped sooner." This connects directly to [[Founder Mindset]] -- the conviction that sustains a decade of work.

## The FAANG Trap

Caldwell and Seibel address the specific case of technical founders stuck at large tech companies ([Why you should leave your FAANG job](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ih-dalton-michael-why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job)). They identify a retention system engineered to prevent departures: signing bonuses, equity vesting over four years, refresher grants that stack, and lifestyle inflation driven by peers who intend to stay. The result is a loss-aversion trap where the unvested equity always exceeds the founder's savings, making departure feel irrational.

Most FAANG employees who aspire to found companies overestimate what they will learn. Caldwell observes that most programmers work on "some shitty ad server" rather than core infrastructure. The tools and processes learned at large companies often must be unlearned at a startup -- a Google engineer's standard for shipping a beta exceeds what a startup MVP requires by an order of magnitude.

Tan describes his own $200 million mistake: turning down Peter Thiel's offer to co-found Palantir because he valued a promotion at Microsoft to "level sixty" ([My $200 million startup mistake](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Jh-garry-s-channel-my-200-million-startup-mistake)). His advice: "I'm not saying you shouldn't go work at a big tech company. I am saying you should probably leave before it makes you soft."

For those who do take FAANG jobs, Seibel advises: have a plan for how long you will stay, keep personal burn low, and remember you can always return. The message applies to roughly 1% of FAANG engineers -- those who fantasize about quitting daily.

## The Bay Area Advantage

Caldwell and Seibel disagree on city versus suburbs but agree on one point: the Bay Area provides network effects that founders outside it cannot perceive ([Does your startup need to be in San Francisco?](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IN-dalton-michael-does-your-startup-need-to-be-in-san-francisco)). Caldwell frames it as blackjack basic strategy -- an easy percentage multiplier in a game that is already hard. Seibel compares it to an aspiring NBA player: you must aim for greatness, not merely participation.

Both observed that founders who left the Bay Area during COVID quietly returned. The community effect was invisible until it was absent. Seibel: "I never really understood that I was consuming the community."

## Conformists vs. Non-Conformists

Caldwell and Seibel trace how the startup world shifted from a domain of non-conformists to one increasingly populated by conformists seeking status and money ([The cult of conformity in Silicon Valley](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IY-dalton-michael-the-cult-of-conformity-in-silicon-valley)). When they were in college (early 2000s), tech was uncool -- Yale graduated 10 CS majors in a class of 1,200. Startups attracted people who hated having a boss and refused to wait in line. Now, CS is a prestige credential, and many graduates seek FAANG leveling and status rather than building.

The non-conformist founder signals they observe: wanting to build profitably on YC's $500K alone, having no awareness of tech Twitter drama, being excited that the work is harder than expected, and betting on their own growth curve rather than seeking external validation.

## Skills to Build Before Founding

Caldwell and Seibel advise high schoolers and college students to focus on five areas ([The student's guide to becoming a startup founder](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IZ-dalton-michael-the-student-s-guide-to-becoming-a-successful-startup-founder)): learn to code (even if you do not plan to be a full-time programmer), learn basic design, launch products (even small side projects), learn to talk to people, and learn to play the credential game without being consumed by it.

Caldwell uses a Final Fantasy analogy: grinding to level up before fighting the boss produces a ten-times-more-powerful character later. The skills compound. Tan provides a complementary list of six founder skills: engineering, product, design, sales/marketing, finance, and leadership ([6 skills for startup founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Jl-garry-s-channel-6-skills-for-successful-startup-founders)). A founder should be deep in one or two and "broad enough to be dangerous" in the rest.

## Learn, Earn, or Quit

Tan offers a career framework: at any point, you should be learning, earning, or both ([Learn, earn, or quit](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Jg-garry-s-channel-learn-earn-or-quit-my-job-career-advice)). If neither, leave. Learning comes first -- acquire craft skills (maker or hustler) before optimizing for income. The best earning comes from equity ownership, not salary. The hedonic treadmill -- earning to consume, consuming to feel better -- is the trap that prevents people from creating.

## Wealth vs. Money

Tan draws on Paul Graham and Naval Ravikant to distinguish wealth from money ([Stop chasing money, chase wealth](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Je-garry-s-channel-stop-chasing-money-chase-wealth)). Money is a big integer in a database. Wealth is the underlying engine that produces value. A "wealth machine" takes money in and produces more money plus more of the thing that makes money -- three virtuous cycles of talent, customers, and capital. Skills are the path to creating wealth, and skills that are rare command disproportionate value. Tan cites Dennis Rodman developing a rebounding technique nobody else practiced, winning five NBA championships.

## Choosing a Startup to Join

For those not yet ready to found, joining a startup is a common stepping stone. Three sources converge on how to evaluate startups as an employee-investor.

Taggar advises thinking "a lot like an investor does when they're picking a startup to invest in, because that's essentially what you are" ([How to choose a startup to join](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/9t-how-to-choose-a-startup-to-join)). His three criteria: (1) look for growth trajectory, not absolute numbers -- $10K in revenue that doubled last month beats $1M that has been flat for twelve months; (2) evaluate founders by what they have accomplished relative to their starting point, not by traditional credentials; (3) do not try to size the market -- every great startup has a core group of early users who are fanatical, even if small.

Altman extends this framework with eight counterintuitive investment principles that also apply to employees choosing startups ([Sam Altman on how to pick which startup to work at](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/K0-sam-altman-on-how-to-pick-which-startup-to-work-at)). The most important: "it's more important that a startup have a small number of users that really love the product rather than a lot of users that really like the product." He emphasizes trusting exponential growth even though almost nobody can intuit it -- "I still have not managed to get good at intuiting exponential growth. So I have to do it in a spreadsheet every time."

Altman adds that the biggest companies emerge during platform shifts -- the iPhone App Store (2008), the rise of mobile, now AI. His test for real vs. fake trends: "When is there a new platform that people are using many hours every day?" The iPhone passed; VR headsets that sit on shelves did not. He also observes that early employees are undercompensated: "The difference -- the amount of value that you create as an early employee versus a founder is not the one-hundred-x difference that you usually see reflected on a cap table." See [[Equity and Compensation]] for more.

Justin Kan offers three reasons to join a startup, all centered on learning rate ([How to pick which startup to work at](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/AQ-how-to-pick-which-startup-to-work-at)). First, access to roles you are unqualified for -- an early [Justin.tv](http://Justin.tv) hire went from unknown French programmer to running the Rails backend of a top-100 site within a year, then became a cofounder of Socialcam and later TripleByte. Second, joining a startup is the best gateway to starting one -- proximity to founders changes how you think. Third, you learn whether the company succeeds or fails. Kyle Vogt learned to architect live video systems at [Justin.tv](http://Justin.tv) (on the way up), then cofounded Cruise. Daniel Kan learned negotiation by closing a bad acquisition deal for Exec (on the way down), then applied those lessons when Cruise sold to GM for over $1B. Kan cites Graham: "It's not your y-intercept, but it's your slope that's important."

Kan also warns honestly: startup management generally sucks, you are statistically unlikely to get rich, and if you want stability or career pathing, "go join Facebook."

Caldwell and Seibel add a ZIRP-specific lens: companies recovering from ZIRP-era habits -- through executive turnover, return to office, vanity project elimination, and increased work intensity -- are better bets for ambitious employees ([Signs your company is recovering from ZIRP](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/La-signs-your-company-is-recovering-from-zirp)). Companies not recovering are places to leave, not stay. See [[Financial Survival]] for the full ZIRP discussion.

## The Long Game

Caldwell recounts his lawyer telling him (at age 23) that the average startup takes seven to ten years to exit. He did not believe it. His company sold seven years later -- he was 30, the age his lawyer had been when giving the advice. True ambition means patience over many years. "A lot of people have dreams when they're young, and they lose their dreams." The people at the top kept their dreams and put in 17 to 20 years of work ([The student's guide](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IZ-dalton-michael-the-student-s-guide-to-becoming-a-successful-startup-founder)). This connects to the sustainability themes in [[Founder Mindset]].
