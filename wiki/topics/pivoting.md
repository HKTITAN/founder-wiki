# Pivoting

Caldwell defines a pivot simply as changing your idea. At the earliest stages, the word should feel lightweight — "changing your ideas constantly is kind of the norm." A founder not rapidly changing assumptions is likely moving too slowly ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

## Why to Pivot

The core reason is opportunity cost. Every month spent on a non-working idea is a month not spent on one that could work. Caldwell offers a heuristic: if (progress / months of full-time effort) is less than (excitement for an alternative + confidence you can find something better), pivot ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

Good reasons to pivot: you hate working on it; growth is flat despite months of effort; you lack founder/market fit; you rely on an external factor outside your control (mainstream VR adoption, crypto adoption); you are out of ideas for how to make it work.

Bad reasons to pivot: running away from hard work (especially sales); chronic idea-hopping without ever [[Launching|launching]]; chasing a hot trend you read about in TechCrunch.

## Why Founders Take Too Long

Most founders pivot too late, not too early. Caldwell identifies several causes: loss aversion (sunk cost fallacy), a little traction creating false hope, politeness from potential customers mistaken for real interest, fear of admitting defeat, blaming the market instead of the product, and inspirational anecdotes about founders who simply persisted ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

On persistence anecdotes, Caldwell is blunt: "That's super cool, but that is not actionable for you. I would much rather give people advice to play the statistics."

## Shots on Goal

Each high-quality pivot is an additional shot at [[Product-Market Fit]]. "It's much easier to be lucky when you get half a dozen shots on goal than one." The key qualifier is "high-quality" — each attempt must involve building, shipping, and putting a real product in front of real users. Pivoting through ideas without launching them does not count ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

## Evaluating Pivot Ideas

Caldwell proposes a four-factor quality score (1-10 each, averaged) ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)):

1. **How big does it seem?** Can you imagine this as a publicly traded company?
2. **Founder/market fit.** Are the founders domain experts?
3. **How easy is it to get started?** Ideas that require years of R&D or millions in capital before a prototype are poor pivot targets.
4. **Early market feedback.** Do customers say yes immediately, or is the sales process agonizing?

Caldwell applies this scorecard to real YC pivots. Brex went from a VR headset (score 2.5/10) to a credit card for startups (score 8.25/10) — the founders had deep fintech expertise but zero hardware knowledge, making the pivot obvious in hindsight. Retool went from Venmo for the UK (5/10) to a no-code internal tools builder (8.75/10). Both illustrate a common pattern: high founder/market fit on the new idea correlates with success. Friedman's idea evaluation framework echoes the same four factors with slight variations ([How to get startup ideas](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8g-how-to-get-startup-ideas)).

## Pivot Traps

**Whiplash.** Pivoting too frequently causes founders to become demoralized and give up. Paradoxically, working on a suboptimal idea while having fun is less fatal than pivoting into depression. "If you get really sad and hate your life while you're working on your startup, you will definitely not succeed because you will give up" ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

**Employees during pivots.** Having employees while searching for an idea slows the process and makes them unhappy. Caldwell recommends adding team members only after the idea is validated. See [[Hiring]] for more on when to hire.

**When to pivot immediately:** You have launched and tried to get users for weeks or months with no results. The idea requires $100 million before a prototype. You know in your heart it is not going to work ([All about pivoting](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6p-all-about-pivoting)).

## Pivot Stories from Group Partners

YC group partners shared their favorite pivot stories, illustrating how large outcomes emerge from unlikely starting points ([Pivot stories](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iy-yc-s-group-partners-share-their-favorite-pivot-stories)):

**Brex.** Applied to YC with a VR headset idea. The founders had zero hardware experience but had previously built a Stripe-like payments company in Brazil as teenagers. Within three to four weeks of the batch, after talking to hardware experts, they returned to fintech. Taggar observes that smart people often dismiss easy ideas -- the Brex founders felt payments was "been there, done that" and wanted a harder challenge.

**GOAT.** Funded as "Grub with Us," a social dining platform. The investor community in San Francisco liked it, but retention was terrible. Two years post-YC, the founders -- who personally collected sneakers -- pivoted to a sneaker marketplace. In 2015, this seemed eccentric. Caldwell notes: "A lot of the really great pivots are when you're years ahead of consensus."

**GoCardless.** Blomfield's first company started as Group.io, a bill-splitting app for college students. After two weeks of cold-calling hundreds of sports club treasurers at 3-4 AM and gaining only one new user, the founders realized the consumer idea was dead. They took the same payments infrastructure and applied it to businesses collecting recurring payments. Twelve years later, GoCardless is a multi-billion dollar company. Seibel summarizes: "When you're young, you think of the entire world as consumer."

**Clipboard Health.** Alstromer describes We Deng's journey from an Indeed-for-nurses to a software-enabled nursing staffing agency. Deng spent years selling to hospitals before discovering the real pain point: when a nurse calls in sick, hospitals must go to expensive agencies. She solved this manually first -- physically coordinating nurses in front of hospitals -- before building software. Alstromer calls it a textbook example of [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].

## Pivot Hell

Friedman describes "pivot hell" -- changing to a completely new idea every single week during a batch. A perfectionist mindset causes founders to abandon ideas at the first sign of imperfection. One company went so deep into pivot hell that they had to defer Demo Day because they had nothing to present. Six months later, they hit on a regulatory change and built the first product to capitalize on it. The bad ideas were necessary: working through them gave the founders deep industry knowledge that led to the breakthrough ([Pivot stories](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iy-yc-s-group-partners-share-their-favorite-pivot-stories)).

Epstein frames the basketball-pivot analogy: "When you pivot you have to keep one foot stationary." You may change the audience or the product, but ideally you build on learnings from prior ideas rather than starting from zero each time.

## AI-Era Pivots

The AI era has produced several notable pivot stories with distinctive characteristics.

**Windsurf.** Varun Mohan pivoted from GPU virtualization (Exofunction) to AI coding (Codium/Windsurf) over a single weekend with eight employees and $28M raised ([Windsurf CEO](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MO-windsurf-ceo-betting-on-ai-agents-pivoting-in-48-hours-and-the-future-of-coding)). The company was making a few million in revenue and was free-cash-flow positive, but Mohan saw that the Transformer architecture would commoditize their GPU infrastructure business. He picked coding because the entire team was excited about it -- even though dev tools historically had poor outcomes. "When you have no other options, it's a very easy decision. You're going to be a zero with a high probability anyways." Mohan describes the mindset required: "irrational optimism" to pursue something new combined with "uncompromising realism" to recognize that the current path is dead.

**Cursor.** Truell and co-founders pivoted from a CAD co-pilot to coding after nine months ([Cursor CEO](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MU-cursor-ceo-going-beyond-code-superintelligent-ai-agents-and-why-taste-still-matters)). The CAD work was technically interesting but the science was not ready and they were not personally passionate about mechanical engineering. "Building a company's hard and so you may as well work on the thing that you're really excited about." The false start was not wasted: they learned model training at scale, inference infrastructure, and data scraping -- all of which became core Cursor capabilities.

Both stories illustrate a pattern: prior technical work compounds into the new direction even when the product is entirely different.
