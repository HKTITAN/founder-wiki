# Pricing

Pricing is the most neglected of the three growth levers (acquisition, retention, monetization), yet a 1% improvement in pricing effort yields a 12.7% return -- roughly four times the return of equivalent acquisition effort. Hale argues that startups consistently undercharge, and that fixing pricing is the single highest-leverage growth action most founders can take ([Startup pricing 101](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6h-startup-pricing-101)).

## The Pricing Thermometer

Hale introduces the pricing thermometer as a first-principles framework. Three variables interact: cost, price, and value. The gap between price and cost is the incentive to sell (margin). The gap between value and price is the incentive to buy. Startups can set price by working up from cost (cost-plus pricing) or down from value (value-based pricing). Value-based pricing allows charging more and is universally recommended, but requires understanding how the customer perceives the product's worth.

Four mistakes arise when founders do not understand these relationships: pricing too low, underestimating costs, not understanding value, and targeting the wrong customers.

## Charge a Premium

Caldwell argues that "most of the time, people are way undercharging for their product" ([Most startups are undercharging](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7M-most-startups-are-undercharging)). Competing on price is dangerous because it masks whether the product genuinely solves a problem. A startup whose entire acquisition strategy is being cheaper than alternatives "could just be getting people that want the cheapest possible product" rather than validating demand. Successful YC companies -- Instacart, DoorDash, Airbnb, Dropbox -- charged premiums, not discounts. Zapier charged money while its direct competitor IFTTT was free.

Seibel reinforces this with the Stripe example: the founders tested their hypothesis by charging more than the competition, not less. "When you're the most expensive thing and people are still desperate for it, that's how you know you have an insight" ([Where do great startup ideas come from](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DU-dalton-michael-where-do-great-startup-ideas-come-from)). This connects to [[Talking to Users]] -- willingness to pay is the strongest form of validation, stronger than surveys or polite feedback ([Successful founders are OK with rejection](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/DV-dalton-michael-successful-founders-are-ok-with-rejection)).

## Early Adopters Are Not Price-Sensitive

Startups sell to early adopters -- the first 2-5% of potential buyers. These customers care about benefits above all else and are willing to take risks on unproven products. They are not price-sensitive. In fact, Hale argues that if a startup has built a better product and charges less, early adopters may see reputation risk: "Why is it too good to be true? What is the catch?" Charging more to this audience can actually increase trust and shorten sales cycles.

## Price Determines Acquisition Strategy

Hale maps price ranges to the sales motions they can support:

- **Under $2,000/year (self-serve):** All marketing must be inbound. No sales team. Same-day conversions.
- **$2,000-$10,000 (transactional):** Marketing generates qualified leads. Inside sales reps, SDRs, product demos. Sales cycle: 1-3 months.
- **Over $25,000 (enterprise):** Branding and trust-building marketing. Dedicated customer success. Sales managers, territory divisions, sales engineers. Sales cycle: 6-12 months.

The danger zone is high-complexity sales with low price -- where the cost of acquiring a customer exceeds what the customer pays. If closing deals takes months but revenue per customer is small, founders must either raise the price or reduce acquisition costs. This framework connects directly to [[Sales and Distribution]] strategy.

## The Value Equation (B2B)

Blomfield introduces a complementary framework for B2B pricing: the value equation ([How to price for B2B](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LI-how-to-price-for-b2b)). Sit with the customer's champion and quantify the value your product delivers -- cost savings, time savings, or revenue increases. Write the assumptions down and have the customer challenge them. Then price at roughly 25-50% of the value delivered. The customer keeps two-thirds, you keep one-third.

For example: an AI customer service tool that eliminates 20% of queries for a team costing $10 million/year saves $2 million. Pricing at $700K gives the customer a clear ROI to present to their CFO. The value equation also defines success metrics for pilot projects.

Blomfield adds two constraints: costs should serve as a floor only (never start with cost-plus pricing), and aim for 80-90% gross margins. Competition should be addressed through differentiation, not price wars. "Competing solely on pricing really is not a winning maneuver."

## The 10/5/20 Rule

Hale's rule of thumb for price optimization: set price so the perceived value is 10X the price. Raise prices by 5% iteratively. Keep raising until you lose 20% of deals. That balance point indicates the price is neither too high nor too low.

Blomfield offers a simpler alternative for founders who struggle with the value equation: pick a number similar to what customers pay for comparable software, then increase by 50% for each successive prospect until more than 25% of deals are lost on price alone. "If every single deal is closing straight away, you're almost certainly underpricing."

Koomen reinforces this from his Optimizely experience: "I remember my co-founder Dan coming out of a sales call and telling me that he'd worked up the nerve to quote the prospect $10,000 a month for our software, and the prospect ended up talking us down to $2,000 a month and then buying" ([Enterprise sales for founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LF-enterprise-sales-for-founders)). Their initial quote was 5X what the customer was willing to pay, and they still bought.

## Pricing Structure and Sales Channels

Blomfield maps pricing to sales team economics: at a 5:1 ratio between new ARR and total salesperson compensation, a $100K/year salesperson must close $500K in new ARR. That could be one $500K contract, twenty $25K contracts, or 500 $1K contracts -- each requiring a fundamentally different sales motion ([How to price for B2B](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LI-how-to-price-for-b2b)).

Keep pricing simple. Overcomplicating pricing kills sales processes. Committed recurring revenue (MRR/ARR) is preferable to usage-based pricing because it survives downturns. One technique: start with usage-based pricing, observe actual usage for a month or two, then offer a minimum monthly commitment with volume discounts.

Enterprise pricing should not be published on the website -- the value equation differs for each customer. Instead, use tiered plans with a self-serve option for individuals and teams, gating enterprise features (SSO, compliance, audit logs) behind a "contact sales" plan. This enables pricing differently for small and large customers.

To sanity-check market size, Hale recommends calculating how many customers are needed at a given price to reach $100 million in annual revenue. At $100/year, you need 1 million customers (consumer scale). At $100,000/year, you need 1,000 customers (enterprise). The SMB range in between ($1,000-$10,000) is where many startups struggle because customers behave like consumers but look like enterprises.
