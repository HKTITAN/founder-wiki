# Company Culture

Brady defines company culture as the implicit set of behaviors inside a company — what employees do when no one has told them what to do. Done right, the right behaviors support a good business in intangible ways over the life of the company ([Building culture](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6r-building-culture)).

## Why It Matters Early

The first ~20 employees are the company's cultural DNA. These people will hire and train the next wave when the company scales after [[Product-Market Fit]]. If the early employees embody the right values, the culture propagates. If they do not, mistakes compound and become nearly impossible to correct later. "If you think you're gonna wake up at 100 employees and then start a diversity program, you're fooling yourself" ([Building culture](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6r-building-culture)).

## Six Things Founders Can Do Now

Brady's six-step framework for early-stage culture-building ([Building culture](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6r-building-culture)):

**1. Be proud of the problem you are solving.** If founders chose the idea with their ego — because it sounds impressive at parties — they will lack the energy to sustain the company through hard times. Everyone around the founders will see how they feel. That emotional signal sets the tone for culture.

**2. Create a long-term vision that others will follow.** The vision should describe purpose, not the product. Tesla: "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" (no mention of cars). Microsoft: "A computer on every desk in every home" (crazy talk in the early 1980s). Google: "Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful" (no mention of a search engine). These visions attract people who identify with the mission.

**3. Define values as a hiring filter.** Have a conversation with your [[Cofounders|cofounder]] about the behaviors you want inside the company. Keep the list to fewer than five items. At this stage, it is informal — the seed of a future corporate values list. Examples: Spotify ("Innovative. Collaborative. Sincere. Passionate. Playful.") and Atlassian ("Open company, no bullshit. Don't fuck with the customer. Play as a team.").

**4. Make values externally focused.** Culture statements focused on the customer provide better behavioral guidance than internally-focused ones. Brady contrasts Facebook's "Move fast and break things" (internally focused, no guidance on what not to break — "Shouldn't be a surprise when you look at this that some of the privacy violations have occurred") with Google's "Don't be evil" (externally focused, providing guardrails that protected user trust).

**5. Have a conversation about diversity.** Not just ethnic and gender diversity, but diversity of opinions. Research suggests that companies fostering strong, opposed opinions are more creative. The natural tendency in early [[Hiring|hiring]] — tapping friends and former colleagues — produces homogeneity. Founders should discuss diversity explicitly before it becomes an afterthought.

**6. Put a hiring plan in place.** From the first employee, follow a structured process that incorporates the values conversation. Assess whether the process is working after the first few hires. Plan to evolve it before scale-up requires it. See [[Hiring]] for more on the hiring process.

## Culture and Prioritization

Seibel warns that many founders "cargo cult" the startup — prioritizing press, conferences, [[Hiring|hiring]], and investors over getting product to users. "You are pretending to be a startup, but the actual work of startups is pushing product, getting it in the users' hands, and seeing if they like it" ([Biggest mistakes first-time founders make](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/66-biggest-mistakes-first-time-founders-make)). This prioritization failure is itself a culture problem: what founders model in the early days becomes the implicit behavior of the company.

## The Cargo Culting Problem

Caldwell and Seibel dedicate an entire episode to cargo culting -- superficially copying successful companies without understanding why they did what they did ([Silicon Valley's cargo culting problem](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/IR-dalton-michael-silicon-valley-s-cargo-culting-problem)). Classic examples: copying Google's open offices, free snacks, and flat org structure without recognizing that Google hired brilliant engineers to solve a genuinely hard technical problem. Copying Facebook's "don't charge users, build an ads business" model without having a product people use two hours per day. Copying Uber's aggressive expansion without having unit economics working in a single city.

They identify three levels of cargo culting. Classic: copying a successful company's surface features. Modern: copying a company that raised money but has not succeeded (copying WeWork or Zumi Robotics based on fundraising announcements). Super-modern: copying another struggling startup because they "look like a startup."

Caldwell uses a plagiarism analogy: the difference between copying three papers into your report and reading broadly, digesting, and synthesizing original work. "The thing that sucks about startups is that you do have to think about a lot of things. There isn't just a checklist." The antidote is starting from the user's perspective: what do they actually care about? Users care that Google returns good search results, not about its logo styling. Users care that Uber arrives in five minutes, not how many cities it operates in.

## The Airbnb Approach

Chesky describes how Airbnb wrote its core values before hiring a single employee -- a process that took roughly six months. The first engineer took four to five months to find because Chesky screened for cultural fit alongside technical ability. His reasoning: the first engineer is "like bringing in DNA to your company. There were going to be a thousand people just like him or her" ([Building company culture](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JN-how-to-start-a-startup-building-company-culture-part-i)).

Airbnb uses dedicated core values interviewers -- people outside the functional team being hired for -- to assess cultural alignment separately from technical skill. Chesky personally interviewed the first 300 employees. His screening question evolved from "if you had a year left to live, would you take this job?" (too extreme) to the ten-year version. He wanted people on a calling, not looking for a job: "There is a job and then there's a calling."

Lin (former Zappos COO) provides the theoretical framework. Culture is "the everyday core values and actions of each member of the team in pursuit of the company mission." He introduces Patrick Lencioni's five dysfunctions pyramid: teams break down from lack of trust, which prevents healthy conflict, which prevents genuine commitment, which prevents accountability, which prevents results. Culture that builds trust at the foundation enables everything above it.

Lin and Chesky both emphasize that culture does not pay off in the short term. Chesky states: "If you wanted to in one year build a company and sell it as quickly as possible, the number one piece of advice I give you is screw the culture." Culture is an investment that compounds over years, and the founding DNA -- the stories, habits, and decisions from the earliest days -- propagate through the organization as it scales.

## Missionaries vs. Mercenaries

Chesky applies cultural thinking to strategic decisions. When the Samwer brothers cloned Airbnb in 2011 with $90 million and 400 employees (vs. Airbnb's 40 employees and $7 million), the pragmatic move was to acquire them. Chesky refused because "we were missionaries and they were mercenaries." The decision was controversial but vindicated: Airbnb now generates more than 50% of revenue from Europe.
