# First Principles Thinking

[[Garry Tan]] argues that the default education system trains people to think by analogy -- "you learn the history, you learn how it's been done, and then you're programmed to believe that is how they will be forever" [1]. First principles thinking is the antidote: starting from what you are absolutely sure is true and reasoning upward, rather than copying existing approaches. Tan identifies three prerequisites: curiosity, empathy, and serenity [1].

## Curiosity: The Five Whys

Curiosity means asking questions and digging further to understand how systems actually work. Tan highlights the Five Whys analysis from the Toyota Production System, using an example from Eric Ries (The Lean Startup) [1]:

1. Why was the website down? CPU utilization on front-end servers hit 100%.
2. Why did CPU spike? Code contained an infinite loop.
3. Why was that code deployed? A programmer made a mistake.
4. Why did the mistake get checked in? No unit test was written.
5. Why was there no unit test? The new employee wasn't trained in test-driven development.

Ries observes: "Because the most common problems keep recurring, your prevention efforts are automatically focused on the 20 percent of your product that needs the most help" [1]. The Five Whys are why the Toyota Production System made Toyota's cars so reliable -- "they had to come up with a process to get there" [1].

## Empathy: Direct Experience with Users

Empathy means developing an intuitive sense for the lived experience and feelings of customers. Tan uses Airbnb's founding story as the canonical example: Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia needed money for rent, so they listed air beds during a design conference. They hosted three strangers and discovered something unexpected: "The arc of a friendship gets contracted from a year to a day. In other words, if you were to meet somebody, how much time does it take to invite them over your house? It might take months, even a year. What it did -- it contracted this year-long friendship into a couple days. These people came as strangers; they literally left as friends" [1].

This direct, unmediated experience -- sleeping under the same roof as users -- gave Airbnb's founders conviction that could not have come from market research or surveys. "They had direct experience, which gave them direct belief and conviction that this was something that was going to work" [1]. Airbnb is now worth over $100 billion.

## Serenity: Accepting What You Cannot Change

The most counterintuitive prerequisite. Tan references the Serenity Prayer by Reinhold Niebuhr: "God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and the wisdom to know the difference" [1].

[[DoorDash: Creating a Market from Scratch|DoorDash]] illustrates this principle. Facing competitors like Uber (with 50x their marketing budget) and Seamless (with a 17-year head start), DoorDash could not win a head-on battle in major cities. Instead, they "retreated to Long Island, New Jersey, Westchester, and Connecticut. They found product-market fit there and were profitable in a matter of months" [1].

The key insight: "There are two ways to win. One is just to raise more money. But what can you do if you can't raise more money? Be more profitable, outflank, and outsmart. Don't fight a losing battle; fight the battle you can win" [1]. DoorDash took suburban profits and used them to win the cities, eventually reaching 50% U.S. market share and a $55 billion+ market cap.

## Breaking the Script

Tan frames first principles thinking as escaping the societal script: "A whole system, a whole society, a whole civilization dedicated to telling you how to live and what to do and how to think" [1]. The classroom and workplace train thinking by analogy. First principles thinking challenges preconceived notions by demanding direct experience and reasoning from ground truth rather than precedent.
