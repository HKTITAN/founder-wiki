# Equity and Compensation

Equity decisions shape startup outcomes more than most founders realize. Graham, Altman, and Friedman each address different facets: the math of equity trades, the mechanics of compensation at scale, and the special case of scientific founders.

## The Equity Equation

Graham introduces a general formula for any equity trade: give up n% of the company only if what you receive in return makes the remaining (100 - n)% worth more than 100% was before. The test is 1/(1 - n) ([The equity equation](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8u-the-equity-equation)).

For a 7% stake (YC's standard at the time), the investor must improve the startup's average outcome by more than 7.5%. For a 30% stake (a typical Sequoia investment), the bar is a 43% improvement. Graham argues that taking money from a top VC firm at these rates is often "an extraordinary bargain" because the brand alone improves outcomes by more than the equity cost.

The same formula applies to employee grants. If a hire increases the company's expected outcome by 20%, the break-even equity grant is 16.7%. But salary and overhead must be subtracted -- Graham recommends multiplying annual compensation by 1.5 to translate it to equity cost, since startups either grow 3x per year (making next year's salary payable from a higher valuation) or die.

"Incidentally, notice how important it is for early employees to take little salary. It comes right out of stock that could otherwise be given to them."

## Compensation at Scale

Altman addresses equity and compensation for later-stage startups (12-24 months in, 25+ employees). He recommends founders plan to give away 3-5% of the company per year for 10 years from founding. Most successful YC companies give out substantial equity ([Later stage advice for startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4q-later-stage-advice-for-startups)).

**Compensation bands** prevent the chaos that occurs when employees discover inconsistent pay. A mid-level engineer falls in one range; a senior engineer falls in another. Bands keep things fair and reduce adversarial negotiation.

**Refresher grants** matter as much as initial grants. Altman warns founders to stay ahead of vesting schedules. An employee approaching their four-year cliff and thinking about leaving because their equity is nearly vested is a preventable retention failure. The cost of losing a good employee far exceeds the cost of a refresher grant. This connects directly to [[Hiring]] retention strategy.

## Scientific Founders and Equity Splits

Friedman addresses equity for university spin-outs, where founding teams often include a mix of full-time founders and academic advisors ([How to spin your scientific research out of a university and into a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8f-how-to-spin-your-scientific-research-out-of-a-university-and-into-a-startup)). Two rules:

1. Full-time founders should get equal or nearly equal equity.
2. Academic [[Cofounders|cofounders]] who remain at the university should typically own no more than 10%.

The biggest conceptual mistake: treating equity allocation as a reward for past contributions rather than an anticipation of future ones. A university spin-out may feel halfway done at founding, but Friedman frames it as mile 2 of a 26-mile marathon. The full-time founders will run the remaining 24 miles. The equity split must reflect that.
