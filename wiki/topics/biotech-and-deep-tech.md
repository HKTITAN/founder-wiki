# Biotech and Deep Tech

YC has funded over 75 university spin-outs and numerous hardware and deep tech companies. The sources converge on a theme: the same startup principles that work for software -- start cheap, iterate fast, keep founder control -- are increasingly applicable to biotech, hardware, and aerospace.

## The Cost to Start Has Plummeted

Friedman argues that raising money for a biotech company in 2019 looks like raising money for a tech company in 2005. The same forces Paul Graham predicted for software -- more startups, cheaper to start, technical founders keeping control -- are playing out in life science ([How biotech startup funding will change in the next 10 years](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4L-how-biotech-startup-funding-will-change-in-the-next-10-years)).

New infrastructure has reduced costs: low-cost CROs, rentable lab space by the bench, lab robots from companies like OpenTrons, computational drug discovery tools, and streamlined FDA submission software. YC bio companies routinely clear major scientific hurdles during the 3-month batch. Shasqi showed localized chemotherapy outperforming conventional chemo in a mouse model using only YC's seed funding. Athelas built a working prototype blood diagnostic device for $40K and ran a 350-patient study during the batch ([On starting a company from a hackathon project](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4x-on-starting-a-company-from-a-hackathon-project)).

## University Spin-Outs

Friedman identifies four common misconceptions among scientific founders ([How to spin your scientific research out of a university and into a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8f-how-to-spin-your-scientific-research-out-of-a-university-and-into-a-startup)):

1. **You can start while staying in academia.** A company has little chance of success unless someone who worked on the research leaves to run it full-time.
2. **You should find an outside CEO.** The original researchers are almost always the best CEO candidates. Domain knowledge outweighs general business skills at the early stage.
3. **You need someone with business experience.** Most YC-funded scientists have no prior business experience. Business skills at the early stage are minimal and learnable.
4. **You should raise money before leaving.** VCs rarely fund idea-stage spin-outs. Founders should self-fund, get grants, or raise a small pre-seed to start, then use progress as leverage for larger rounds.

The far more common mistake is waiting too long to leave, not leaving too early. Founders often discover that work done in the final year at the university was wasted because market assumptions were wrong.

For tech transfer negotiations, standard university equity stakes are 3-5%. Above 10% causes problems with future [[Fundraising]]. Royalties should be minimized or eliminated. Express license agreements are becoming more common at leading universities.

## Hard Tech at YC

Scholl describes building Boom (supersonic airplanes) through YC W16. His two key lessons from YC: get out of stealth mode, and go sell airplanes. He was skeptical that YC could help an aerospace company, but found that capital risk -- not technical risk -- was the biggest early threat ([On starting and scaling aerospace company Boom](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5Q-on-starting-and-scaling-aerospace-company-boom)).

For hardware companies in YC, Scholl advises finding something that "is and looks like actual, substantive progress" within three months. Boom built a physical model of the airplane and 3D-printed a model of the jet engine. They also brought one of the actual engines that would power the first aircraft to Demo Day. "Find the thing that you're gonna get done in three months that is progress and looks like progress."

Scholl secured $5 billion in letters of intent by Demo Day, including Virgin's commitment to take the first 10 airplanes. His [[Sales and Distribution|sales]] advice: "Be so persistent that you're sure you're being annoying. And you'll feel like you must be annoying, but when you have that feeling that means you're probably doing it right."

## A New Kind of Founder

The biotech funding shift has drawn a new founder profile: grad students and postdocs who previously had only two career options (academia or big pharma). Starting a company is now a viable third path. These founders raise $1-5M seed rounds after YC and retain control of their companies -- a pattern that parallels software founders in 2005 retaining control from VCs. This connects to the broader [[Founder Mindset]] theme that domain expertise matters more than business credentials.

Scholl frames the [[Founder Mindset]] for deep tech: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist. Startups are hard -- all startups are hard -- and I think what makes the difference many times is are you working on something that is so meaningful to you, that it is worth it for you to go through hell to make it happen?"
