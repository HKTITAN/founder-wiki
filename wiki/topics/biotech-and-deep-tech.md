# Biotech and Deep Tech

YC has funded over 75 university spin-outs and numerous hardware and deep tech companies. The sources converge on a theme: the same startup principles that work for software -- start cheap, iterate fast, keep founder control -- are increasingly applicable to biotech, hardware, and aerospace.

## The Cost to Start Has Plummeted

Friedman argues that raising money for a biotech company in 2019 looks like raising money for a tech company in 2005. The same forces Paul Graham predicted for software -- more startups, cheaper to start, technical founders keeping control -- are playing out in life science ([How biotech startup funding will change in the next 10 years](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4L-how-biotech-startup-funding-will-change-in-the-next-10-years)).

New infrastructure has reduced costs: low-cost CROs, rentable lab space by the bench, lab robots from companies like OpenTrons, computational drug discovery tools, and streamlined FDA submission software. YC bio companies routinely clear major scientific hurdles during the 3-month batch. Shasqi showed localized chemotherapy outperforming conventional chemo in a mouse model using only YC's seed funding. Athelas built a working prototype blood diagnostic device for $40K and ran a 350-patient study during the batch ([On starting a company from a hackathon project](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/4x-on-starting-a-company-from-a-hackathon-project)).

## University Spin-Outs

Friedman identifies four common misconceptions among scientific founders ([How to spin your scientific research out of a university and into a startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8f-how-to-spin-your-scientific-research-out-of-a-university-and-into-a-startup)):

1. **You can start while staying in academia.** A company has little chance of success unless someone who worked on the research leaves to run it full-time.
2. **You should find an outside CEO.** The original researchers are almost always the best CEO candidates. Domain knowledge outweighs general business skills at the early stage.
3. **You need someone with business experience.** Most YC-funded scientists have no prior business experience. Business skills at the early stage are minimal and learnable.
4. **You should raise money before leaving.** VCs rarely fund idea-stage spin-outs. Founders should self-fund, get grants, or raise a small pre-seed to start, then use progress as leverage for larger rounds.

The far more common mistake is waiting too long to leave, not leaving too early. Founders often discover that work done in the final year at the university was wasted because market assumptions were wrong.

For tech transfer negotiations, standard university equity stakes are 3-5%. Above 10% causes problems with future [[Fundraising]]. Royalties should be minimized or eliminated. Express license agreements are becoming more common at leading universities.

## Hard Tech at YC

Scholl describes building Boom (supersonic airplanes) through YC W16. His two key lessons from YC: get out of stealth mode, and go sell airplanes. He was skeptical that YC could help an aerospace company, but found that capital risk -- not technical risk -- was the biggest early threat ([On starting and scaling aerospace company Boom](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5Q-on-starting-and-scaling-aerospace-company-boom)).

For hardware companies in YC, Scholl advises finding something that "is and looks like actual, substantive progress" within three months. Boom built a physical model of the airplane and 3D-printed a model of the jet engine. They also brought one of the actual engines that would power the first aircraft to Demo Day. "Find the thing that you're gonna get done in three months that is progress and looks like progress."

Scholl secured $5 billion in letters of intent by Demo Day, including Virgin's commitment to take the first 10 airplanes. His [[Sales and Distribution|sales]] advice: "Be so persistent that you're sure you're being annoying. And you'll feel like you must be annoying, but when you have that feeling that means you're probably doing it right."

## Outsider Founders in Hard Tech

Scholl's extended account of building Boom Supersonic demonstrates that non-domain founders can succeed in aerospace ([Building a supersonic plane company from the ground up](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MA-building-a-supersonic-plane-company-from-the-ground-up)). He went from product manager at Groupon to building the first independently developed supersonic plane, a trajectory he attributes to starting from first principles rather than inherited industry assumptions. He spent two weeks on a spreadsheet model -- one tab for global air routes, one for aircraft physics -- and took it to a Stanford professor, who told him his assumptions were conservative.

Scholl's recruiting method was recursive: "If you could wave a magic wand and get anybody on the planet to come work with you on this, who would your top five people be?" He asked that question at every meeting, and within a few levels of recursion was talking to the best people in the field. His team of fifty built the XB-1 prototype. He hired young engineers from SpaceX and "early career at Boeing before they were corrupted" rather than senior people who "had been around the loop a couple times" and believed the problem was impossible.

Scholl's central lesson: "Knowledge and skills are variable. Smart people underestimate what they can learn, particularly if they're motivated. But what you can't change is your passions." Paul Gross at Remora echoes this. Gross, a non-engineer college graduate, started building the world's first commercial mobile carbon capture device for trucks and trains by reading science papers, reaching out to experts, and finding a PhD cofounder ([The world's first commercial mobile carbon capture device](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Mz-the-world-s-first-commercial-mobile-carbon-capture-device)). His advice: "You don't have to be the expert. You have to create a team of experts."

## The Bystander Effect in Hard Tech

Scholl identifies a form of the bystander effect as the reason many hard tech ideas go unbuilt: "Supersonic flight would so obviously be a good thing. Nobody's doing it. There must be something wrong." The internet was full of qualitative objections ("People won't pay more for speed") that collapsed under quantitative analysis. He spent eighteen months expecting to find a fatal flaw in his spreadsheet model before concluding that "if the math was wrong, I would know by now."

Friedman, reflecting on the 2016 YC batch that produced three billion-dollar aerospace companies (Astranis, Boom, Relativity Space), observes that hard tech ideas are "surprisingly achievable and sometimes hiding in plain sight" ([Building a supersonic plane company from the ground up](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MA-building-a-supersonic-plane-company-from-the-ground-up)).

## Scrappy MVPs for Hardware

Gedmark at Astranis built the company's first prototype satellite in an apartment with Ikea desks, ESD mats, and a clean room made of PVC piping and shower curtains sprayed with staticide ([You don't have to be a billionaire to launch satellites](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Lm-you-don-t-have-to-be-a-billionaire-to-launch-satellites)). During the YC W16 batch, Astranis set a goal of building a prototype satellite in three months -- "anything that doesn't get done until after demo day, forget about it." They brought the satellite to Demo Day and raised one of the largest seed rounds at YC at the time. Eight years later, the company has over 400 employees and launched satellites on a dedicated Falcon 9 rocket.

Gedmark's lesson mirrors the broader startup canon: "Minimum viable product for a geostationary telecommunication spacecraft is not very minimal," but it was still possible to start small and iterate. The prototype satellite was a demo, not a production system -- they were flexible on requirements. Gross at Remora took a similar approach, building a diesel engine test simulator in a shipping container to dramatically increase iteration speed before testing on the open road.

## Locating Talent for Hard Tech

Gross made the deliberate decision to locate Remora outside Detroit rather than Silicon Valley because "this is where the talent is" for mechanical and electrical engineers with packaging and vibration experience. He frames the decision generically: "Think about where the talent density is for the talent that matters for your problem." Gedmark similarly built Astranis's manufacturing facility at Pier 70 in San Francisco, bringing testing capabilities (thermal vacuum chambers, vibration shakers) in-house to accelerate iteration.

## Energy and Climate as Hard Tech Frontiers

Gustaf Alstromer profiles Helion Energy's approach to fusion -- a linear, pulsed reactor using deuterium and helium-3 fuel that can generate helium-3 in its own reactors, solving the scarcity problem ([Fusion energy is tech's next big unlock](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/MT-fusion-energy-is-tech-s-next-big-unlock)). Helion adopted a hardware-first iteration model: "You don't just design a system and then build it and then test it. You actually want to do all of that in parallel." While operating their sixth-generation machine, they were building the seventh and prototyping factories for mass production. This approach moved them from early prototypes to seventh-generation Polaris in under a decade.

Remora's carbon capture system for trucks and trains represents another climate-tech frontier. The device captures 80-90% of CO2 from vehicle exhaust and sells it to food and beverage companies. Gross frames the ambition argument: "If you're going to spend 10 years of your life on this, you might as well just do the thing that's hardest, that's most interesting, because you'll get the best team, the best advisers, the best investors. And ironically, it may end up being easier." This echoes Scholl's point and Altman's argument that [[Hiring|recruiting top talent]] is easier when the mission is ambitious.

## A New Kind of Founder

The biotech funding shift has drawn a new founder profile: grad students and postdocs who previously had only two career options (academia or big pharma). Starting a company is now a viable third path. These founders raise $1-5M seed rounds after YC and retain control of their companies -- a pattern that parallels software founders in 2005 retaining control from VCs. This connects to the broader [[Founder Mindset]] theme that domain expertise matters more than business credentials.

Scholl frames the [[Founder Mindset]] for deep tech: "Find something in the world that you desperately want to exist. Startups are hard -- all startups are hard -- and I think what makes the difference many times is are you working on something that is so meaningful to you, that it is worth it for you to go through hell to make it happen?"
