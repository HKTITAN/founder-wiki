# Launching

YC treats launching not as a single moment but as a continuous practice. Manalac states: "I want to destroy the idea that launching is just this one moment in time, because assuming you do well, and assuming you ship new products and new features, you are never going to be not launching" ([How to launch again and again](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6i-how-to-launch-again-and-again)).

## Launch Early

Most founders overthink their first launch. Manalac describes a team that spent six months perfecting every pixel before sharing their product, and warns that "if it took you six months to get there, your startup is probably dead before you get another chance to launch" ([The best way to launch your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ir-the-best-way-to-launch-your-startup)).

The worst-case scenario of launching too early is that people think the product is ugly, investors hear about it before it is ready, or no one notices at all. Airbnb launched three times before gaining traction. The appropriate response to a failed launch is to iterate and launch again.

A dedicated YC Office Hours episode reinforces this ([Why startup founders should launch sooner](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/LG-why-startup-founders-should-launch-companies-sooner-than-they-think)). Seibel identifies "pop culture knowledge" as the root cause of launch fear -- widely believed but false ideas. The two most dangerous beliefs: (1) your launch is important and you only get one, and (2) if people try your product once and don't like it, they will never return. Alstromer rebuts: "No one ever says, 'I will never go to this website ever again because this was bad.' That never happens."

Founders who worked at large companies are especially susceptible. Apple-style launches with multi-year development create a mental model that does not apply. Epstein reframes the purpose: "If your goal with your launch is to learn, then you would do it way faster and you will be successful no matter what happens." Brex launched without a way to create user accounts -- customers emailed their password and the team set up accounts manually.

Seibel reinforces: "How many here remember the day that Google launched? No. Facebook? No. Twitter? No. So it turns out that launches aren't that special at all" ([How to plan an MVP](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6f-how-to-plan-an-mvp)). He distinguishes between a "launch" (getting any customers) and a "press launch" (getting media attention), and argues founders should pursue the first immediately while deferring the second.

## Types of Launches

Manalac catalogs multiple launch channels that founders should use sequentially and repeatedly:

**Silent launch.** A domain, company name, short description, and call to action. Can be built in a day.

**Friends and family launch.** Share the product with people you know, watch them use it, collect feedback. Reddit's first users were just the founders in their YC batch.

**Launch to strangers.** Lugg's founders rented a truck, parked outside IKEA, and approached shoppers struggling to tie furniture to their cars.

**Online communities.** Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit subreddits, and industry-specific forums. Magic went from zero to 40,000 signups overnight via Reddit and Hacker News.

**Request-for-access / waitlist launch.** Superhuman built viral elements by allowing referrals to skip the waitlist line. Robinhood got 10,000 signups on day one and 50,000 in the first week from a waitlist posted on HN. But Manalac warns: the longer you sit on a waitlist, the harder it is to convert people ([The best way to launch your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ir-the-best-way-to-launch-your-startup)).

**New feature / product launches.** Stripe launches every new product on Hacker News with founders actively participating. Glossier releases products every 6-8 weeks, hitting every launch channel each time.

## The DoorDash MVP

Tang describes the canonical example of a minimal launch. DoorDash started as PaloAltoDelivery.com -- a static HTML page with PDF menus and the founders' personal phone number, built in one afternoon. No backend, no dispatch system. Google Docs tracked orders. Apple's Find My Friends tracked drivers. Square processed payments. When someone called to order Thai food, the founders drove to the restaurant and delivered it themselves. The founders used these early deliveries to understand the entire delivery process, talk to restaurants, and learn what a dispatch algorithm should look like ([Getting started](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JL-how-to-start-a-startup-getting-started-getting-press-and-doing-things-that-don-t-scale)). The approach connects directly to [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] and [[Early-Stage Tactics]].

## Continuous Launching for Technical Founders

Hu describes Segment's continuous launch strategy as a model. They launched five times in about a month on Hacker News, each time adding features based on user feedback. Their first launch generated high engagement -- a hint of [[Product-Market Fit]]. They kept adding integrations (Node, PHP, WordPress) and iterating. Hu argues that after launching, the technical founder's goal shifts to iterating toward product-market fit using both hard data (analytics) and soft data (user conversations) ([Tips for technical founders](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Is-tips-for-technical-startup-founders)).

## Writing a Clear Pitch

Manalac emphasizes that a clear one-sentence description is the foundation of every launch. Lead with what, not why. Avoid jargon. Airbnb's YC application: "We built the first online marketplace that lets travelers book rooms from locals instead of hotels" ([The best way to launch your startup](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Ir-the-best-way-to-launch-your-startup)).

Garry Tan adds three rules: (1) show, don't tell -- mention real users, real products, real payments; (2) use plain English, not buzzwords; (3) use the inverted pyramid -- put the conclusion first, then stack proof underneath ([Garry Tan's tips for applying to YC](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/J7-garry-tan-s-tips-for-applying-to-yc)).
