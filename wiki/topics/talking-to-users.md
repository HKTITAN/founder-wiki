# Talking to Users

YC's core advice to founders -- "build stuff and talk to users, and nothing else" -- appears across dozens of sources. Livingston states that after funding over 1,000 startups, she does not know of "a single case of a startup that felt they spent too much time talking to users" ([How not to fail](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/5l-how-not-to-fail)). Yet the practice is harder than it sounds. Most founders either avoid it (preferring to write code) or do it poorly (asking for features instead of problems).

## Why Talk to Users

Alstromer argues that users keep founders honest. "They are the only stakeholders actually paying you anything. If anyone will tell you the truth, it will be them" ([How to talk to users](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iq-how-to-talk-to-users)). He cites Brian Chesky's experiment of living in 50 different Airbnbs to get unfiltered host feedback, and notes that Chesky and his co-founders still receive calls from hosts on their personal cell phone numbers years later.

The Airbnb founders flew to New York early in YC, posed as hired photographers, and while one took photos of listings, the other sat with hosts asking about product problems. This generated insights that could not have been found sitting in front of a computer. It is the canonical example of [[Early-Stage Tactics|doing things that don't scale]] applied to user research ([Growth for startups](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6k-growth-for-startups)).

Hale frames the user relationship through a marriage research lens: Jared Spool's research shows a "direct correlation to how much time we spend directly exposed to users and how good our designs get." The exposure must be direct (not reports or graphs), at minimum every six weeks, and for at least two hours per session. At Wufoo, developers were exposed to users four to eight hours every week through customer support shifts ([Building products users love](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JK-how-to-start-a-startup-building-products-users-love)).

## How to Find Users

Alstromer recommends starting with personal networks and former coworkers, then expanding to LinkedIn, Reddit, Slack communities, Discord, and in-person events. He advises keeping outreach messages short, mentioning a personal connection if one exists, and asking for only a 20-minute call ([How to talk to users](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iq-how-to-talk-to-users)).

A survey of 50+ YC founders confirmed the pattern: the majority found their first customers through cold emails, LinkedIn outreach, personal networks, and door-to-door visits. One founder waited outside a food truck all day for the owner to take a break. Another converted inbound recruiting emails into sales leads. Reddit waitlists, Twitter launches, and friends-and-family pilots were common starting points ([Founder FAQ: first customer](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JE-founder-faq-how-did-you-get-your-first-customer)).

## The Interview Process

Alstromer provides a specific question framework for user interviews: (1) Tell me how you do X today. (2) What is the hardest thing about X? (3) Why is it hard? (4) How often do you have to do X? (5) Why is it important for your company? (6) What do you do to solve this problem today? The most critical rule: do not introduce your idea until the end of the call or not at all. Doing so biases answers. The interviewer's role is to listen, not to talk ([How to talk to users](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iq-how-to-talk-to-users)).

Alstromer also recommends watching users interact with the product through screen-sharing during calls. Observing behavior reveals more than listening to descriptions of behavior, because changing behavior is the hard part.

## Ask About Problems, Not Features

Seibel draws a sharp line between useful and harmful user feedback. "Never ask users for features. Never ask users to tell you what they want. It's not the user's job to come up with features. That's your job. The user's job is to give you problems" ([How to plan an MVP](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6f-how-to-plan-an-mvp)). Useful questions focus on how often the user experiences the problem, how intense it is, whether they would pay for a solution, and whether they know others with the same problem.

Alstromer illustrates with two examples of users providing good problems but bad solutions. Early Gmail users asked to view the inbox and an email simultaneously -- the real problem was Gmail's slow loading speed. Early Airbnb guests asked for hosts' phone numbers -- the real problem was insufficient trust in the platform ([How to talk to users](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iq-how-to-talk-to-users)).

In the [[Product Development]] context, Seibel advises: "Hold the problem you're solving tightly, hold the customer tightly, hold the solution you're building loosely."

## Choosing the Right Users

Seibel warns that talking to the wrong customers can be worse than not talking to anyone. Companies with free products attract hobbyists whose feedback leads the product astray. Starting with a higher price filters for users who have the problem intensely enough to pay ([Building product](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7s-building-product)).

Seibel also describes "hijack users" -- people who try to use the product to solve a problem it was not designed for. At Justin.tv, some hijack users (video gamers) pointed to a better opportunity, while others (copyright pirates) actively harmed the network. Founders should evaluate each category: does the hijack user represent a larger group? Do the economics change? Does this user represent a better opportunity? ([User you don't want](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/67-user-you-don-t-want))

## Turning Users into Champions

Williams describes a champion as a user who talks about and advocates for the product. The easiest way to create champions is to delight users with an experience they will remember. At Teespring, Williams and his co-founder handled all customer service personally until the company reached $130-140K/month in revenue. Three channels matter: running customer service yourself, proactively reaching out to current and churned users, and monitoring social media and communities ([Getting started and doing things that don't scale](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JL-how-to-start-a-startup-getting-started-getting-press-and-doing-things-that-don-t-scale)).

Williams warns that one detractor who has had a terrible experience is enough to reverse the progress of ten champions. The solution: always make it right, even when the cost seems painful in the short term. Customers who were originally the most frustrated often become the biggest champions and longest-term users.

## Support-Driven Development

Hale describes Wufoo's practice of having every team member -- including engineers -- do customer support. At acquisition, 10 people supported 500,000 users with 7-12 minute response times during business hours. The practice created a feedback loop: developers who build the software also support it, so they fix recurring problems at the source rather than letting them accumulate ([Building products users love](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/JK-how-to-start-a-startup-building-products-users-love)).

John Gottman's marriage research provides an analogy. "Stonewalling" -- simply not responding -- is one of the worst things in any relationship. Many startups stonewall their users by not answering support requests, and this is one of the biggest causes of early churn.

## User Feedback and the MVP

The primary test for an MVP is simple: does it solve the problem? Seibel compares products to tools, not art. "If users don't find products useful, then the products are by definition not useful, and they're a waste of your time to build" ([Building product](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7s-building-product)). Watching users interact with the product -- either in person or through session recording tools -- reveals more than surveys or feature requests.

Alstromer recommends keeping early interviewees involved throughout the product development process. Creating a Slack instance or WhatsApp group gives early users an exclusive feeling and enables rapid feedback loops. Shipping features in response to user feedback builds trust and demonstrates responsiveness ([How to talk to users](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/Iq-how-to-talk-to-users)).
