# Six Founder Skills

[[Garry Tan]] identifies six skill areas that increase a founder's probability of success [1]. No single person can master all six, but being deep in one or two and "broad enough to be dangerous" in the rest is the target. "Any given single human being could probably only get truly great at one or two of these skills, but you need a team of people who are great at all of these in order to build a startup or business that really matters" [1].

## 1. Engineering

Technical prowess is the "ultimate moat" in startups. "When you have a piece of code or an algorithm figured out that nobody else has, when you have something like PageRank, that's the ultimate moat for Google, and it protects something that is worth billions of dollars. Someone can't just walk in off the street and take that from them" [1].

A CS degree helps but is not required. For non-technical founders, Tan recommends learning enough to be dangerous: "If you understand the craft and you can build a good but not perfect version of what you ultimately want to build, you can get the ball rolling. It'll immediately distinguish you against the sea of non-technical people who are also trying to find a [[Technical Co-Founders|technical co-founder]]" [1].

## 2. Product

Product is about knowing what to build and for whom. Tan frames it as "being able to run the process on a machine to continually find and refine [[Product-Market Fit]]. This isn't a one-time thing. This is a constant process" [1].

There is no obvious shortcut to learning product. Tan's recommendation: "Build something and release it. Release it to users and see if they stick with it. Talk to users and see why they did or didn't take to it" [1].

## 3. Design

Design has two components [1]:

- **Interaction design (UX)**: how it works. Where to put the buttons, what to say on them, how to break complex processes into manageable steps. This is "most directly related to product" and is the more important of the two.
- **Visual design**: how it looks. A skill like drawing or painting. Having "taste" -- knowing when a design works or does not -- is more important for founders than being able to execute visual design themselves.

Tan recommends Steve Krug's "Don't Make Me Think" as an entry point [1].

## 4. Sales and Marketing

[[Ryan Petersen]] of Flexport calls sales "the most underrated profession" [1]. Tan adds that "every business today is a meme. It's never been easier for a startup to access exactly the people who have that problem" [1]. Brand, attention capture, and the ability to sell are essential.

## 5. Finance

Not critical at the outset -- "engineering, product, sales, marketing are really the key to getting [[Product-Market Fit]]" [1]. But financial literacy becomes essential post-PMF. Tan cites Matt McGinnis from Rippling: "Once I understood that my company was worth the net present value of all future cash flows, I started making better decisions. Also, all the investor tropes started to make a lot more sense" [1].

Finance enables resource allocation decisions: should you hire an executive, invest in a new office, and when will those investments pay back? [1]

## 6. Leadership and Management

Leading by example is step one. But the harder skill is holding people accountable -- "having very crucial conversations, hard discussions about when things didn't go right, ideally as constructively as possible, while also having the impact you need to have" [1].

Tan cites Barbara Kellerman: "Followers are more important to leaders than leaders are to followers." Leading from empathy creates more connected, resilient teams [1].

For those still in school, Tan recommends finding leadership positions that require coordinating people toward numerical outcomes -- clubs, sports teams, or projects at work. He recommends Dale Carnegie's "How to Win Friends and Influence People" as a surprisingly practical guide [1].

## The Team Imperative

Founders are "the speck of dust that forms the crystalline structure that actually solves a real problem" [1]. The six skills explain why teams are so crucial -- and why founders should assess which skills they need in co-founders and early hires. Being deep in one or two areas helps a founder recognize who is truly excellent in the others [1].
