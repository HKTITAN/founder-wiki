# Startup Website Design

[[Garry Tan]] and [[Aaron Epstein]] review five AI startup websites in a design critique that distills actionable principles for any startup homepage [1]. The core insight: a website is a startup's first conversation with a potential user, and that conversation must answer two questions immediately -- "What is this?" and "Is it for me?" [1].

## The Two Questions

Every visitor arriving at a startup website asks two things in rapid succession [1]:
1. **What is this?** What does this product or company do?
2. **Is it for me?** Does this solve my problem? Am I the target customer?

If the homepage cannot answer both within seconds, the visitor leaves. Tan warns against clever taglines that sacrifice clarity: "You don't want to be clever if clever equals confusing" [1]. One reviewed startup had three different concepts in its hero section -- "AI generated game assets," "game development," and "GPT to game" -- leaving both reviewers unsure what the product actually did [1].

## Single Opinionated Call to Action

Multiple calls to action of equal visual weight confuse users. Epstein recommends identifying the primary KPI and making that the single prominent CTA, with secondary options as plain links rather than equally weighted buttons [1].

Tan reinforces this: "Usually you want a single call to action that you're driving people towards." When a site offers four different product links, "I don't know which to click" [1].

## Show, Don't Tell: The Feat of Strength

For AI startups in particular, Tan recommends showing a live demo or impressive output rather than describing capabilities abstractly: "Show the product and a feat of strength... Those are the types of demos that wow people. That's actually why people are so obsessed with this technology now -- the demos and the technological feats of strength that people are capable of putting out" [1].

## Don't Block the Experience

"Try it now" buttons that lead to login walls destroy trust. Epstein recommends letting users experience value before asking for commitment: "Don't force people to create an account. If somebody wants to save it or use it, that's when you prompt them to actually create an account because they're already invested in the product" [1].

Tan frames the competition: "You're always fighting the urge for the back button or to go look at Hacker News" [1].

## Specific Customer, Specific Language

Being too general about who the product serves is "a little bit problematic" [1]. Tan recommends writing the homepage for the ideal customer profile (ICP), not for "anyone who might need our technology" [1].

Epstein suggests a practical tactic: "Go talk to your existing users, ask them 'how would you describe my product?' And then take the exact phrase that they say and just put that up at the top of your site because that's ideally the same thing that will resonate for other potential customers" [1].

## Social Proof

Customer logos and quotes should be woven throughout the page rather than concentrated in one section. The most effective pattern shows a logo, a specific quote from a named person at that company, and their photo -- "it feels like real people rather than something that's made up" [1].

## Animation and Visual Design

Tan and Epstein caution against overusing animation. "If a lot of things are moving, then it just becomes overwhelming. Be selective -- what's the thing that you really want to draw people's attention to? Put animation around that" [1]. Animation should direct the eye to specific elements, not create sensory overload [1].

Spacing and fit-and-finish matter more than many founders realize. Poor spacing between elements "ruins the illusion that this is something that I can trust at the enterprise level" [1].

## The "How It Works" Section

Tan favors putting a "how it works" section early in the page because "it brings you to a much higher understanding of what's going on. It's kind of like when you do YC interviews and you get to do a demo" [1].

Concrete claims like "30 percent cheaper and 25 percent faster" are effective because they sound like they came from user conversations and proactively address concerns about using the product [1].

## FAQ as Trust Builder

A well-placed FAQ section that addresses the top two or three questions can be highly effective. "Within two clicks you can get the answers" creates a good experience and builds trust [1].
