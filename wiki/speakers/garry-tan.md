# Garry Tan

Garry Tan is President and CEO of Y Combinator. He was previously a YC partner for five years (working with 700+ companies) and founded Initialized Capital, a venture fund. Before that, he cofounded Posterous (2008), a blogging platform that experienced rapid growth before stalling. He hosts the rebooted "How to Build the Future" series, interviewing founders including [[Sam Altman]], [[Tony Xu]], [[Parker Conrad]], [[Bob McGrew]], and [[Elon Musk]].

Tan's personal channel features a mix of solo videos on startup principles and long-form interviews with founders he has invested in, including [[Brian Armstrong]] (Coinbase), [[Ryan Petersen]] (Flexport), [[Dylan Field]] (Figma), and Kai Stinchcombe (True Link Financial). He was the first investor in Coinbase, turning a $300,000 check into over $2.4 billion [13].

## Worldview

Tan's advice centers on several interlocking themes:

**Radical honesty about reality.** Tan's experience at Posterous -- where two years of 20-30% monthly growth suddenly went flat and he could not accept it -- left a deep mark on his thinking [2]. He now teaches that founders must confront hard truths, even when their entire worldview depends on denying them. He frames this as "the alchemy: going from the dreamland of an individual to creating the reality with a group of your smartest friends" [2]. See [[Confronting Reality]].

**Clarity and simplicity in communication.** Tan's YC application advice reduces to three rules: show rather than tell, use plain English instead of buzzwords, and structure communication as an inverted pyramid (conclusion first, proof second) [1]. He defines a great pitch as "very simple, understandable statements put side by side that, when you zoom out, you realize that is a really big idea" [1]. See [[Applying to YC]].

**Wealth over money.** Tan draws on [[Paul Graham]] and philosopher Alan Watts to distinguish between money (an integer in a database) and wealth (the ability to create value). "If you're focused on the money, you're gonna get stuck as a cog in the machine. You'll end up focused on that next promotion or the trappings of upward mobility" [12]. The path to wealth runs through acquiring rare skills, creating leverage through products that work while you sleep, and building "wealth machines" -- businesses that generate both money and the capacity to generate more money [12]. See [[Wealth vs. Money]].

**The builder ethos.** Across multiple videos, Tan returns to the idea that the most important credential is the ability to build. He funded Coinbase because Brian Armstrong was "a builder" who had coded the entire first version himself [13]. He describes his own career pivot from Microsoft as the moment he stopped consuming and started creating [12]. In his interview with Armstrong, he observes that "the common thread is being a builder, being very focused on... entering a flow state and then having this outcome" [17].

**DIY and authenticity over perfection.** In his video on movements, Tan draws a parallel between punk rock and startups: "Humans have a visceral reaction to experiencing authenticity, even at the cost of perfection. Everyone is so focused on being perfect these days, and to that I say, fuck perfection. Do it yourself" [19]. He points to Armstrong building Coinbase with Twitter Bootstrap, and the Ramones playing three-chord songs at CBGB, as examples of the same principle [19].

## Personal Story: The $200 Million Mistake

In 2003, fresh out of college at 23, Tan was working at Microsoft earning $72,000/year. His friends were starting a company with Peter Thiel. Thiel wrote Tan a personal check for his full annual salary and told him to quit: "Cash this check. Quit your job. This is a zero-risk opportunity for you" [14]. Tan said no -- "Thank you very much, Mr. Thiel, but I might get promoted to level sixty next year" [14]. The company was Palantir, now worth $20 billion+. The missed equity was worth at least $200 million [14].

Tan did eventually join as employee number ten and built one of the major product teams. But the story is a core teaching moment: the opportunity cost of staying at a big company when a high-conviction early-stage opportunity is available [14]. See [[Leaving Big Tech]].

He also turned down a Google job interview in 2004, noting that "it was obviously useful, but it wasn't clear that it was going to make money" [14]. He uses both experiences to frame the asymmetry of startup economics: Google's pure profit per employee is $1.6 million per year after all costs, but engineers creating that value capture only a fraction of it. "The only way you can really access that is by owning equity" [14].

## On the Coinbase Investment

Tan was one of the first checks in Coinbase's seed round, investing $300,000 that became worth over $2.4 billion at IPO [13]. Three factors drove his yes when nearly everyone else said no:

1. **He was primed for the problem.** He had tried to buy Bitcoin on Mt. Gox and experienced the terrible UX firsthand [13].
2. **He was primed for the idea.** Working at Peter Thiel's hedge fund Clarium Capital in 2005, he read "The Dollar Crisis" by Richard Duncan, which primed him for the possibility of fiat currency failure [13]. As Louis Pasteur said, "Chance only favors the prepared mind" [13].
3. **Brian was a builder.** Armstrong had been head of anti-fraud at Airbnb, essentially "being part of the special forces" against hackers daily, and could build and secure the first version of Coinbase himself [13].

Tan describes his investing philosophy in his Armstrong interview: "What gets me up in the morning is believing before people believe, and then helping people believe themselves" [17].

## On the AI Platform Shift

In his interview with [[Sam Altman]], Tan draws a direct comparison between the current AI moment and the mobile platform shift. He observes that "the world is still sleeping on all of this to such an astonishing degree," which is why he is excited for startups. The platform shift is always built by "the people who are young with no prior knowledge" [3].

He points out that even Facebook nearly missed mobile, having to acquire Instagram, attempt to buy Snapchat, and acquire WhatsApp to compensate. Dominant companies of one era routinely fail to lead the next [3].

## Startup Website Design Principles

In a collaboration with [[Aaron Epstein]], Tan reviews five AI startup websites and distills several principles [11]:

- **What is this? Is it for me?** These are the first two questions every visitor asks. Clever taglines that sacrifice clarity for cleverness fail: "You don't want to be clever if clever equals confusing" [11].
- **Single call to action.** Multiple CTAs of equal weight confuse users. Be opinionated about what you want the user to do [11].
- **Feat of strength.** AI startups should show a demo that impresses -- "show the product and a technological feat of strength" rather than describing it abstractly [11].
- **Don't block the experience.** "Try it now" buttons that lead to login walls destroy trust. Let users experience value before asking for commitment [11].
- **Social proof placement.** Weave logos and customer quotes throughout the page rather than dumping them in one section [11].

See [[Startup Website Design]].

## Six Founder Skills

Tan identifies six skill areas that increase a founder's probability of success [18]:

1. **Engineering** -- the "ultimate moat." PageRank protects billions of dollars. Non-technical founders should learn enough to be dangerous [18].
2. **Product** -- knowing what to build and for whom. A continuous process of finding and refining [[Product-Market Fit]], not a one-time event [18].
3. **Design** -- both interaction design (how it works) and visual design (how it looks). "Having taste" matters more than execution ability [18].
4. **Sales and marketing** -- Ryan Petersen of Flexport calls sales "the most underrated profession." Every business is a meme; capturing attention has never mattered more [18].
5. **Finance** -- not critical at the outset, but essential post-PMF. Matt McGinnis from Rippling: "Once I understood that my company was worth the net present value of all future cash flows, I started making better decisions" [18].
6. **Leadership and management** -- leading from empathy creates more connected teams, but accountability is the harder skill. Tan recommends "How to Win Friends and Influence People" [18].

A single person cannot master all six. "Any given single human being could probably only get truly great at one or two of these skills, but you need a team of people who are great at all of these in order to build a startup or business that really matters" [18]. See [[Six Founder Skills]].

## Better, Faster, or Cheaper

Tan argues that being first to market is overrated -- Facebook was the 20th social network, WordPress preceded Posterous by five years [20]. What matters is being better, faster, or cheaper than alternatives:

- **Better**: Google (technical excellence in search), Dropbox (better than USB drives), Discord (low CPU for gamers), Coinbase (better than Mt. Gox) [20].
- **Faster**: Flexport (replacing paper with software speeds freight by days), Uber (faster to find a car), Facebook (remained fast while Friendster used unoptimized MySQL) [20].
- **Cheaper**: Shopify ($29/month vs. running Magento), Lyft (citizen ride-sharing vs. Uber's initial black cars), Instacart (asset-light grocery delivery), Amazon ("their margin is our opportunity") [20].

See [[Better, Faster, or Cheaper]].

## First Principles Thinking

Tan frames the three prerequisites for [[First Principles Thinking]] as curiosity, empathy, and serenity [23]:

1. **Curiosity** -- using techniques like the Five Whys (Toyota Production System) to understand root causes rather than accepting surface explanations [23].
2. **Empathy** -- getting direct experience with users. Airbnb's founders hosted strangers themselves and discovered "the arc of a friendship gets contracted from a year to a day" [23].
3. **Serenity** -- accepting things you cannot change. DoorDash retreated to suburbs when Uber had 50x their marketing budget, found profitability there, then used those profits to win cities [23].

## On Building Movements

Tan argues that every billion-dollar startup begins as a movement -- "a team and a direction and a thing that helps millions and possibly billions of people" [19]. Drawing on Derek Sivers's TED talk about the "first follower," Tan observes that the leader needs guts, but "the first follower is what transforms a lone nut into a leader" [19]. New followers emulate followers, not the leader [19].

He traces the personal computer movement from the Homebrew Computer Club through Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak showing off at hobbyist meetings, to computers on every desk. Every major startup followed the same pattern: starting as a fringe movement among enthusiasts [19]. See [[Building Movements]].

## On Peer Groups

Tan's most significant personal insight from YC is the power of founder peer groups. He initially thought YC's value was having a respected figure (Paul Graham) who believed in you. The deeper value was the peer group: "The thing that I didn't understand until much later was it was the peer group of other people doing that." He advises young people to find their peer group as early as possible [3].

## Standing Behind Founders When It Is Unpopular

Tan describes backing [[Parker Conrad]] and Rippling "at a time when it was extremely unpopular" [5]. He had seen Conrad's work at YC firsthand and knew that Zenefits' enterprise value would not have existed without Conrad. The press narrative was "exactly the opposite of what you and I lived and experienced" [5].

## On the CEO Decision

Tan draws on his experience at Posterous to argue against co-CEO structures. In 2010, he and his co-founder told Andreessen Horowitz that they were both CEO. "The meeting might as well have ended at that moment" [6]. See [[CEO Role and the Co-CEO Mistake]].

## The Learn/Earn Framework

Tan proposes a career evaluation framework: at any point in your career, ask whether you are learning, earning, or both [7]. If neither, quit. See [[Learn, Earn, or Quit]].

## The Three "Why Nows"

Tan identifies three forces that create windows for startup opportunity: new technology, behavior change, and regulation [8]. See [[Why Now]].

## Key Frameworks

- **The Inverted Pyramid**: Conclusion first, proof second [1].
- **The Dreamland-Reality Bridge**: Hold contradictory beliefs -- visionary future and honest present [2].
- **The Idea Maze**: Know the full history of attempts in your space [2].
- **No Adults in the Room**: No one has all the answers [3].
- **The Co-CEO Trap**: Shared leadership is lethal for startups [6].
- **Learn, Earn, or Quit**: Two-axis career evaluation [7].
- **Three "Why Nows"**: Technology, behavior, regulation [8].
- **The Wealth Machine**: A business that generates both money and the capacity to generate more [12].
- **Above vs. Below the API**: Creators of systems vs. workers directed by them [12].
- **Better, Faster, or Cheaper**: The three axes for competitive differentiation [20].
- **Six Founder Skills**: Engineering, product, design, sales/marketing, finance, leadership [18].
- **Curiosity, Empathy, Serenity**: The three prerequisites for first principles thinking [23].

## Distinct Perspective

Compared to other YC partners, Tan draws heavily on philosophy, psychology, and storytelling. His advice cites Miyamoto Musashi, Karl Popper, Ray Dalio, Daniel Kahneman, Carl Jung, Alan Watts, Reinhold Niebuhr, Naval Ravikant, Venkatesh Rao, and references films, music (punk rock, Nirvana, the Ramones), and sports (Dennis Rodman's rebounding technique). This gives his advice a more personal, narrative quality while still arriving at concrete recommendations.

Tan is unusually candid about his own failures. The Posterous case study in [[Confronting Reality]] is a first-person account of founder denial [2]. The Palantir story is a $200 million lesson in opportunity cost [14]. The Microsoft years are framed as a consumptive cycle he had to break [12].

As an interviewer, Tan is notable for connecting founders' stories to broader principles. In his conversation with Tony Xu, he identified that DoorDash's suburban strategy appeared to be data-driven but was actually driven by customer obsession [4]. He draws the Coinbase investment story as a lesson in contrarianism and prepared minds [13]. He compares Flexport to an electrical utility -- a mundane metaphor that captures the ambition of making global trade as seamless as flipping a light switch [21].
