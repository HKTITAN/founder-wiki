# Alfred Lin

Alfred Lin is a partner at Sequoia Capital. Before Sequoia, he was the COO and Chairman of Zappos, the online shoe retailer known for its culture of exceptional customer service. At Zappos, he worked alongside CEO Tony Hsieh to develop and codify the company's ten core values [1].

## Worldview

**Culture is the primary input to company output.** Lin frames the company as a black box where results (financial, product, or otherwise) are the output. "One of the major inputs is the culture of the company" [1]. He cites research showing that the Fortune 100 Best Companies to Work For returned 11.8% annually from 1997 to 2003, nearly double the S&P 500 (6.8%) and Russell 3000 (6.04%) [1].

**Culture is a daily habit, not a statement.** Lin compares culture to fitness: "You just can't be fit if you don't do it as a daily habit. Eventually you get out of shape, then you get fat, and then you're like, 'I got to go on a crash diet to sort of get back into shape.' That doesn't quite work" [1].

**Values must be deeper than words.** When Zappos began codifying values, they started with 37 employee-submitted values and spent a year whittling them down to 10. The year-long process was necessary because single words like "honesty" or "teamwork" are meaningless -- "give me a break. Everybody wants the culture to be honest" [1]. Values require paragraphs of supporting definition.

## The Culture Framework

Lin defines company culture as: "Every day, the [core values] and [actions] of each member of the team in pursuit of our company [mission]" [1]. He cites Gandhi: "Your beliefs become your thoughts. Your thoughts become your words. Your words become your actions. Your actions become your habits. Your habits become your values. Your values become your destiny" [1].

### Why Culture Matters (FIRST)

Lin organizes the benefits of culture into an acronym [1]:

- **F**irst principles for decision-making
- **I**dentity alignment on values
- **R**esilience and stability to fall back on
- **S**howing what not to do (the more important function)
- **T**alent retention of the right employees

### How to Create Core Values

Lin's process [1]:
1. Ask the leader: what personal values are most important to you?
2. What values are most important to the business?
3. Who are the people you most like working with? What values do they have?
4. Who have you never liked working with? What values do they have? Consider the opposite.
5. Final check: values must support the mission, be credible, and be uniquely tied to the mission.

### Zappos Examples

**"Deliver WOW through service"**: Not generic customer service but a specific commitment to a "wow experience." The value was supported by a paragraph explaining exactly what "wow" meant for employees, customers, brand partners, and investors [1].

**"Be humble"**: Derived from the "opposite" exercise. The founders did not like working with arrogant people. Humility became a core value [1].

### The Five Dysfunctions of a Team

Lin references Patrick Lencioni's pyramid from "The Five Dysfunctions of a Team" to show how culture failures cascade [1]:

1. **Absence of trust** -- without trust, people cannot have honest debate
2. **Fear of conflict** -- without debate, teams cannot arrive at the right answers
3. **Lack of commitment** -- without good answers, people will not commit
4. **Avoidance of accountability** -- without commitment, people cannot be held accountable
5. **Inattention to results** -- without accountability, results suffer

### Culture in Practice

Lin advocates integrating culture into every operational process [1]:
- Interview for cultural fit, not just technical fit
- Include values in performance reviews
- Make culture a daily habit, not an annual exercise

At Zappos, the team philosophy prioritized company over department, department over team, team over individual. The goal was for everyone to improve any idea rather than fight over who was right: "The result is that the company gets a better idea, not that any individual person is right" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Lin is one of the few speakers in the YC canon who comes from an operational role (COO) at a company famous primarily for its culture. His contribution is translating abstract cultural principles into concrete, repeatable processes. The year-long Zappos values codification, the FIRST framework, and the integration of culture into hiring and performance reviews reflect an operator's mindset applied to a "soft" topic.
