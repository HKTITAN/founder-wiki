# Brian Chesky

Brian Chesky is the co-founder and CEO of Airbnb. He graduated from Rhode Island School of Design and co-founded Airbnb with Joe Gebbia and Nate Blecharczyk. The company originated when Chesky, broke and unable to pay rent ($1,000 in the bank, $1,150 rent), turned his apartment into a bed and breakfast during a design conference using three air beds -- the "Air Bed and Breakfast" [1].

## Worldview

**Culture must be designed, not discovered.** Chesky studied companies that endured -- Apple, Amazon, Nike -- and concluded they all had a clear mission and a clear sense of values. "Culture needs to be designed" [1]. He and Gebbia wrote Airbnb's core values before hiring their first employee, partly inspired by Tony Hsieh of Zappos, who told them: "I wish I didn't wait till I was 100 employees to write down our core values" [1].

**Founders are parents. The company is the child.** If the parents are dysfunctional, the child will be dysfunctional. The co-founder relationship -- the "DNA" of the company -- determines the culture that follows. Chesky describes the early Airbnb team as working "18 hours a day, 7 days a week" together, eating together, going to the gym together. "I felt like we were special forces or something" [1].

**Build the company that builds the product.** Chesky distinguishes between building the product (phase one) and building the company that builds the product (phase two). "It doesn't matter how great your original product or idea is -- if you can't build a great company, then your product will not endure" [1].

**A company should outlive its founders.** Chesky frames this through the parent-child metaphor: "It would be a tragedy for us to outlive our company and just watch it rise and fall" [1].

**Missionaries outlast mercenaries.** When the Samwer brothers cloned Airbnb in 2011, raising $90 million and hiring 400 people in 30 days (vs. Airbnb's 40 employees and $7 million raised), Chesky chose not to acquire them. "I felt like we were missionaries and they were mercenaries. I didn't think they were doing it for the beliefs. I thought they were doing it to make a lot of money very quickly." Chesky believed "the best revenge against an internet clone was just to make them run the company long term." More than 50% of Airbnb's revenue now comes from Europe [1].

**Brand is culture turned outward.** "Culture is the principles and the beliefs you have inside the company. Brand is the promise outside the company that everyone identifies with." Chesky argues they are "two sides of the same coin" -- whatever happens inside the company eventually comes out [1].

## Airbnb's Core Values

Chesky describes six core values, elaborating on two [1]:

### Champion the Mission

Airbnb's mission is "belong anywhere." Chesky illustrates this through a story: a London host named Sebastian told him that during the 2012 London riots, seven previous Airbnb guests called to check on him before his own mother did [1].

Chesky interviewed the first 300 Airbnb employees personally. He asked candidates: "If you had ten years left to live, would you take this job?" (Originally "one year," but he amended it because at one year, candidates should spend time with family.) The answer did not have to be yes -- he just wanted candidates to think seriously about what they would choose to do with finite time [1].

His framing: "There is a job and then there's a calling. We want to hire people that aren't just looking for jobs. They're looking for a calling" [1].

### Be a Cereal Entrepreneur

Airbnb's founding story includes creating Obama O's ("the breakfast of change") and Captain McCain's ("a maverick in every bite") cereal boxes during the 2008 presidential campaigns. General Mills refused to participate, so they found a Rice University alum to produce 1,000 boxes. The cereal earned $40,000 -- compared to $5,000 from the website that year. "My mom asked, 'So are you a cereal company now?' And the honest answer was, 'Technically yes'" [1].

This story became the basis for the core value of creative frugality: "We believe constraints bring out creativity." When employees request unnecessary spending, Chesky takes out a box of Obama O's as a reminder [1].

## Hiring for Culture

Airbnb uses dedicated "core values interviewers" who are never from the candidate's function. An engineering candidate is never assessed for cultural fit by engineers, to avoid bias toward technical skill over values alignment. "We've said no to a lot of really great people because we just didn't feel right about them being with us long term" [1].

Chesky spent four to five months finding Airbnb's first engineer, interviewing hundreds of candidates. His reasoning: "I kind of felt like your first engineer was like bringing in DNA to your company. There were going to be a thousand people just like him or her in the company" [1].

On diversity: "You want diversity of beliefs. You want diversity of backgrounds, age. You don't want diversity of values. You want a very, very homogeneous set of beliefs, and that's the one thing that shouldn't be diverse" [1].

## Scaling Through Unscalable Tactics

When asked how Airbnb grew, Chesky credits [[Paul Graham]]'s advice: "It's better to have 100 people that love you than to have a million people that just sort of like you" [1]. The 100 people will each tell 100 more.

Chesky and Gebbia went door-to-door in New York and Denver, staying with hosts. "When you buy an iPhone, Steve Jobs didn't come and sleep on your couch. But I did" [1].

The photography service illustrates the scaling pattern. A New York host could not figure out how to transfer camera photos to a computer. Chesky asked: "What if you could press a button and a professional photographer showed up?" She said that would be magic. "Next day I knocked on her door and said, 'I'm here.'" He personally photographed homes, managed the process via spreadsheets, then hired contract photographers, then had an intern manage them, then built the tools. By the time technology was built, they knew exactly what the service should look like. Airbnb now has 5,000 photographers worldwide and has photographed hundreds of thousands of homes [1].

## The CEO's Three Jobs

"What is the job of a CEO? You articulate the vision. You develop a strategy. And you hire people that fit the culture. If you do those three things, you basically have a company" [1].
