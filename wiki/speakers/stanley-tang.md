# Stanley Tang

Stanley Tang is a co-founder of DoorDash (YC S13). He graduated from Stanford in 2014 with a degree in Computer Science, building DoorDash while still a student. He and co-founder Andy Fang conceived the idea after interviewing small business owners in Palo Alto and discovering that delivery was a massive unsolved pain point [1].

## Worldview

Tang approaches startup building as experimentation. His three lessons from starting DoorDash [1]:

1. **Test your hypothesis.** Treat startup ideas like experiments, not commitments.
2. **Launch fast.** DoorDash's first version -- paloaltodelivery.com -- launched in under an hour with PDF menus and a personal cell phone number.
3. **Do things that don't scale.** Unscalable tactics are a competitive advantage in the earliest stage.

## The DoorDash Origin Story

Tang was passionate about building technology for small business owners. While interviewing Chloe, the owner of a macaron store in Palo Alto, she revealed "pages and pages of delivery orders" she had to turn down because she had no delivery infrastructure [1]. Over the following weeks, Tang and his co-founders talked to 150-200 small business owners. When they raised the idea of delivery, merchants "kept agreeing" that it was a huge unsolved problem [1].

The founders' concern: if delivery is so obvious, why has nobody solved it? They hypothesized that previous attempts may have failed because consumer demand did not exist. To test this cheaply, they built a landing page in an afternoon with PDF menus from Palo Alto restaurants and a phone number at the bottom -- their personal cell phones [1].

They were not expecting anything. Driving home, they received their first call: someone wanted Thai food. "We're like, we're not doing anything right now. Might as well swing by, pick up some Pad Thai, and deliver it" [1]. The customer turned out to be the author of a book called "Weed the People." The next day, two calls. Then five, then seven, then ten [1].

The validation signal: people were willing to endure a terrible user experience -- looking up PDF menus, calling a phone number -- to get delivery. "When people are willing to put up with all this, we knew we found the need" [1].

## Doing Things That Don't Scale at DoorDash

The co-founders were the delivery drivers, customer support, and marketers. They charged customers through Square, tracked orders in Google Docs, and monitored driver locations with Apple's Find My Friends [1]. At one point, Square shut down their account on suspicion of money laundering due to the rapid pace of small transactions. Co-founder Tony Xu, who had previously worked at Square, resolved it by emailing former colleagues [1].

Tang personally emailed every new customer each night: "How was your first delivery? How did you hear about this?" He personalized each email -- "If I see someone order chicken skewers from Orange Hummus, I would say, 'I love Orange Hummus. How were the chicken skewers?'" [1].

The unscalable work doubled as domain education: driving taught them how delivery logistics worked, dispatching taught them what the driver assignment algorithm should look like, and customer support provided real-time feedback [1].

## The Ice Cream Test

During YC, the co-founders received a text that they needed drivers on the road due to a demand spike. They had been about to try a new ice cream store. They debated for "maybe ten seconds" before choosing to deliver. "That became our motivation for scaling: if we can scale, then you could go get ice cream next time" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Tang is distinctive for demonstrating extreme speed-to-launch -- under an hour from concept to live product. His approach is empirical: build the cheapest possible test, observe real behavior, and only invest in technology after validating demand. His framing of startup ideas as experiments rather than commitments aligns with [[Dalton Caldwell]]'s framework on [[Creating Luck]] through rapid iteration.
