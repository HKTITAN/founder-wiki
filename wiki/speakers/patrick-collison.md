# Patrick Collison

Patrick Collison is the co-founder and CEO of Stripe, a payments infrastructure company. He and his brother [[John Collison]] started Stripe while both were in college. The company grew from two co-founders to over 170 employees by the time of this lecture and has since scaled to thousands. The Collisons are Irish and immigrated to the United States.

## Worldview

**Culture solves the bandwidth problem.** Collison views culture as the mechanism that maintains consistency as founders can participate in an exponentially shrinking fraction of company decisions. "Culture is kind of how you -- what the strands are that you want to have, the invariances that you want to maintain, as you can get specifically involved in fewer and fewer decisions over time" [1].

**Transparency as a first principle.** Stripe made radical internal transparency a core cultural value from its earliest days. Every outgoing email was BCC'd to everyone else unless the sender opted out. Collison's reasoning: if everyone shares the mission and has access to information about the current state of the company, "that gets you a huge amount of the way there in terms of working productively together. And it kind of forgives a lot of the other things that tend to break as you grow" [1]. As the company scaled past 170 people, Stripe invested in tooling (mailing list architecture, Gmail filter generation, Hackpad for document transparency, weekly all-hands decks) and cultural norms (when it is appropriate to jump into discussions, how to avoid drive-by criticism that discourages sharing) to maintain the practice [1].

**Think like a value investor when hiring.** The Collisons explicitly framed early hiring as looking for undervalued human capital: "You're looking for the human capital that's significantly undervalued by the market." Stripe's first designer was 18, in high school in Sweden. Their CTO was in college. "You can relax one constraint -- relax the fact that it's apparent that they're talented" [1].

**Time horizons grow with the company.** After one month of operation, a founder plans one month ahead. After four years, four years ahead. This has direct implications for hiring: early on, people must be immediately productive. Later, investing in people who will be ready in a year or two becomes necessary and beneficial [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Collison provides one of the clearest articulations of how to scale culture while acknowledging its inherent limitations. He describes a rapidly growing company as "a very unnatural thing" and frames the question not as "how do we do this perfectly?" but "what's the least bad way of managing that period of growth?" [1]. His candor about imperfection is distinctive: "You probably can't do much better than sort of adequate" [1].

His emphasis on the first ten hires as a 100-person decision is a multiplicative framing: "You're not just hiring those first ten people. You're actually kind of hiring a hundred people because you should think of each one of those people as bringing along sort of another ten people with them" [1].

## Key Contributions

- **Four dimensions of culture**: who you hire and what they value, what you do every day and why, what you communicate, what you celebrate (and punish) [1]
- **Work trials over interviews**: For the first ten hires, work with candidates for about a week before extending an offer. "It's pretty hard to fake it for a week" [1]
- **Three traits of great early hires**: genuine and straightforward, enjoy completing things (not just starting), care intensely about quality [1]
- **The "best among friends" test**: "Is this person probably the best out of all of their friends at what they do?" [1]
- **Growing leaders internally**: Several of Stripe's first ten people moved into leadership roles. "People don't necessarily come out of the womb being good at managing. Being able to develop that in people is a lot of work, but it's damaging if the company can't develop that skill" [1]
- **Startups are not as brutal as the mythology**: "Startups, especially in the earlier days, tend to involve somewhat longer working hours. But there's kind of this tendency to exaggerate it. It's the startup version of fishing" [1]
