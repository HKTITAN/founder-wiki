# Justin Kan

Justin Kan is a co-founder of Justin.tv (which became Twitch), Kiko (a YC-backed calendar company), Exec (an on-demand cleaning service), and Socialcam (a video-sharing app). He also served as a partner at Y Combinator. Across these ventures, Kan developed extensive experience with press strategy, which he codified into a tactical framework for early-stage startups [1].

## Worldview

**Press is not a meritocracy.** Founders often believe journalists are actively searching for the best stories. They are not. Getting press requires deliberate, structured outreach -- the same skills required for business development [1].

**Press without a goal is vanity.** Getting covered feels like success because successful companies are always in the press. But press is a means, not an end. "Getting in the news is nice because you can send it to your mom and say, 'Hey, I have a real job.' But if you don't have a business goal with it, it's really just not a good use of time" [1].

**Press is not a scalable user acquisition strategy.** It is a bootstrap: useful for getting the first 100 or 1,000 users, but people get tired of hearing about a company quickly. "The pull point about news is it's new" [1].

## The Press Framework

### Step 1: Define Your Goal

Kan identifies three examples from his own companies [1]:

- **Socialcam**: Goal was to be perceived as "video Instagram" by Silicon Valley investors and influencers. Targeted tech press.
- **Exec**: Goal was to get customers in San Francisco. National press was useless because 99% of readers could not use the service. Targeted SF Chronicle and local outlets.
- **Twitch**: Goal was to reach the gaming industry -- developers and advertisers. Targeted industry trades and game dev blogs.

### Step 2: Identify a Story

Seven types of startup stories [1]:
1. Product launches
2. Fundraising (reporters cover it even though "it's not very interesting")
3. Milestones or metrics
4. Business profiles (usually only when already successful)
5. Stunts (e.g., WePay dropping a block of ice with frozen money outside a PayPal conference)
6. Hiring announcements
7. Contributed articles (industry overviews, opinion pieces)

The critical filter: "If I wasn't the founder of this company, would I want to read a story about what I'm pitching?" [1]. Incremental feature releases are rarely interesting. The story does not have to be original -- just "original enough." Being the first video game console to raise $10 million on Kickstarter (Ouya) was huge news even though others had raised comparable amounts [1].

### Step 3: Get Introduced to Reporters

Warm introductions convert far better than cold emails. Kan's recommended approach: find entrepreneurs who were recently written about by the target reporter, and ask them for an intro [1]. This works because:
- The entrepreneur has nothing to lose (they do not need anything more from that reporter)
- They are doing the reporter a favor if the story is interesting
- The reporter treats the referral as pre-vetted

### Step 4: Invest the Reporter's Time

Get a face-to-face meeting if possible, a phone call if not. Email exchanges are the worst because they are easy to forget. The more time a reporter invests, the more likely they are to write the story (sunk cost effect) [1].

### Step 5: Prepare the Pitch

Write the ideal story in bullet points. Memorize them. Walk the reporter through a structured conversation that covers every point. "By preparing, you can actually much more easily control the conversation and not forget critical things like your co-founder's name" [1].

### Step 6: Follow Up

One to two days before launch, send an email with: launch timing, thanks for meeting, collateral (video, photos, screenshots), and correct spelling of names (bolded) [1].

### Step 7: Maintain Relationships

Keep a regular cadence of news -- space stories out at regular intervals. Go back to reporters who have written about you before. Help other entrepreneurs get coverage; the introductions will be reciprocated [1].

## On PR Firms

Kan spent between $5,000 and $20,000 per month on PR firms at various startups. His assessment: firms can help with contacts and logistics but cannot tell you what is interesting about your company. "You really have to be responsible for thinking about what's interesting about your company" [1]. Founders should do PR themselves before hiring anyone.

## The Twitch Plays Pokemon Example

Twitch Plays Pokemon -- a developer-created Pokemon game controlled by chat with millions of participants -- became a massive news story. Kan describes Twitch's role: they did not originate the idea (the community did) but gave it legs by making the company available to reporters, suggesting follow-up stories (beating the game, launching the next Pokemon version), and ensuring pre-existing coverage provided context when BBC and other outlets Googled "Twitch" [1].
