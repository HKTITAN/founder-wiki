# Emmett Shear

Emmett Shear is co-founder of Justin.tv and Twitch, and a YC Partner. Before Twitch, he co-founded Kiko Calendar (his first startup, sold on eBay) with Justin Kan right out of college. He then co-founded Justin.tv, a live streaming reality TV show that evolved into a general broadcasting platform. Shear led the gaming initiative that became Twitch, which was acquired by Amazon.

His contribution to the YC knowledge base spans two areas: product development methodology (how to decide what to build next) and user interview technique (how to learn what users actually need). His Twitch experience provides one of the most detailed case studies of user research in the YC canon.

## Worldview

**User interviews tell you where to go; data tells you where you are.** At Justin.tv, the team spent extensive time analyzing Google Analytics, Mixpanel, in-house tools, and traffic sources. Shear's conclusion: data is useful for understanding current behavior but "it doesn't tell you where you need to go. It doesn't tell you what the problems are you need to address." Without user interviews, "nine times out of ten, that idea turns out to be bad" [2].

**Who you talk to matters as much as what you ask.** Selecting the right interview subjects is the first and most consequential decision. For Twitch, the team determined that broadcasters (not viewers) were the strategic users, because viewers followed content to whatever platform the broadcaster chose [2]. "If we'd gone and talked to viewers only, we would have gotten a completely different set of feedback" [2].

**Listen for problems, not features.** Users will ask for polls, scrolling text, and ban list management. These are not the real problems. The real problems were: broadcasters wanted to make money, video quality was poor, and non-users could not start broadcasting because their computers were not powerful enough. Twitch "dumped almost all resources into things that no one ever mentioned in an interview, but those were the things that actually addressed the problem" [2].

**Validation is an anti-goal.** Shear is skeptical of idea validation: "Validating an idea you already have is an anti-goal. You can't improve an idea through any amount of validation" [1]. The goal of customer research is to generate better ideas, not to confirm existing ones.

## Distinct Perspective

Shear is rare in providing both a systematic product prioritization framework and a detailed user interview methodology. Most YC advice focuses on what to build (something people want) rather than how to decide what to build next or how to learn what users actually need.

His candor about failures is distinctive. He openly describes Kiko Calendar as a failure born from never talking to calendar users, and Justin.tv's stagnation from building based on data and brainstorms rather than user conversations. The contrast with Twitch -- where systematic user research drove three years of product decisions -- makes the case experientially rather than theoretically.

He is also the only YC speaker to provide a live user interview demonstration, walking through the process with a student volunteer to illustrate techniques like open-ended questioning, avoiding feature discussions, and digging into current behavior [2].

## Key Frameworks

**Three Prioritization Frameworks**: Built For Me (founder as exemplar user), Switch To Us (interview-driven competitive displacement), and Three Numbers Matter (metric-driven focus areas). Startups progress through them in order [1].

**Three Groups of Interviewees**: Existing users (reveal tolerable pain points), competitor users (reveal dealbreakers), and non-users (reveal barriers to market expansion). Each produces fundamentally different and complementary insights [2].

**The Three-Number Rule**: Three is the maximum number of focus areas people can keep in mind consistently [1].

**The Conversion Loop**: People interviewed during user research become the first product converts when their feedback is acted on, because "most people have never had that experience" of talking to a product team and seeing their input ship [2].

## Three Common User Interview Mistakes

1. Showing people your product (puts ideas in their head instead of drawing out what is already there)
2. Asking about a pet feature direction ("Would you use X?")
3. Talking to who is available rather than who is strategically important [2]
