# Jess Lee

Jess Lee is a partner at Sequoia Capital. She studied computer science at Stanford (graduating around 2004), worked as a PM for Google Maps, and then co-founded Polyvore, a fashion and shopping app where she served as CEO for eight and a half years before joining Sequoia [1].

## Worldview

**Grit is the most important founder quality.** Lee identifies multiple forms of grit: deep passion for the problem, demonstrated history of running through walls, naive optimism that keeps people going when they do not know how hard something will be, and a chip on the shoulder from being underestimated [1]. Starting a company is "almost irrational" because the odds are so bad.

**The problem must be explained before the solution.** Lee observes that many founders pitch solutions without explaining the problem. "It should be so crisp and clear what the problem is that you're solving that the solution almost just flows from that" [1]. She experienced this challenge firsthand at Polyvore, a fashion app pitched to investors who were 94% male. Telling them "imagine an app where you can buy dresses all day" did not work. The Polyvore team solved it by stacking September issue fashion magazines on the table and saying: "This stack of magazines is 300 million dollars of revenue in one issue. Imagine that on the internet" [1].

**Investor selection is a 10-year marriage you cannot divorce.** You cannot kick investors off your cap table. Lee advises founders to treat investor selection with the same gravity as choosing a co-founder: "You're partnering with a person who you're going to be married to for 10 years, except you can't get divorced" [1].

## On Fundraising

### What Sequoia Looks For

Three things [1]:
1. **A founder who is special in some way** -- a unique insight, demonstrated grit
2. **A clear understanding of the problem** -- not just the solution
3. **A really big market** -- line of sight to billions of dollars of market cap

### Running a Competitive Process

Lee recommends going all-in on fundraising rather than treating it as a side activity. Prioritize conversations based on desire to work with the investor multiplied by likelihood of success. Create a competitive dynamic by timing conversations to overlap and being transparent about timelines: "I'm hoping to get this done by this date" [1].

### Choosing the Right Investor

Beyond valuation and terms, Lee advises evaluating [1]:
- **The individual partner**: Will they be a shock absorber during lows and a sparring partner during highs?
- **The firm's network and founder community**: Peer support from other founders elevates everyone's game
- **Preparation**: If an investor walks into a meeting knowing nothing about you, that is a bad sign

Lee describes the ideal investor as someone who provides constructive feedback without being either a pure cheerleader or a critic. Her Sequoia partner Brian Shrier was "super supportive about the good things. And on the things that needed fixing, he didn't yell at me, but he pointed them out logically." Investors who just want to be best friends are not optimal. Investors who are totally uninvolved are also not optimal. And investors who are too prescriptive -- "my sister uses your product and thinks the buttons should go here" -- create different problems [1].

### Counterintuitive Advice

Lee advises against taking office space in your investor's offices. The investor's proximity inhibits experimentation: "If you know that your investor is going to come look at your spreadsheet or look at your product, you're not going to try the super weird, crazy idea" [1].

### When to Raise

At the friends-and-family stage, the tipping point is personal commitment: being okay with the near-certainty of losing the money. At later stages, milestones provide fuzzy guidelines -- for SaaS, roughly $1 million ARR growing 20-30% monthly might signal Series A readiness [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Lee brings the rare combination of founder experience (8.5 years as CEO of Polyvore) and venture capital perspective (Sequoia partner). She is particularly insightful on the challenge of pitching to investors who have no personal connection to the problem -- a challenge she experienced repeatedly pitching a fashion product to male investors. Her advice on investor selection carries the weight of having lived through a long founder-investor relationship.
