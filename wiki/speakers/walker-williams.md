# Walker Williams

Walker Williams is the co-founder and CEO of Teespring (YC W13), an e-commerce platform that allows entrepreneurs to launch products and apparel brands without risk, cost, or compromise. At the time of his 2014 talk, Teespring had grown to approximately 180 employees and was shipping tens of thousands of products daily, generating hundreds of millions of dollars in annual revenue [1].

## Worldview

Williams views doing things that don't scale as a startup's most fundamental competitive advantage -- not a phase to outgrow but a practice to maintain as long as humanly possible. "The moment you give it up, you're giving your competitors that are smaller -- that can still do these things -- that advantage over you" [1].

**There is no silver bullet for user acquisition.** Williams states that every CEO he has spoken with confirms this. "Those first users were impossibly hard to get" for nearly every successful company, regardless of how smooth the growth curve looks from the outside [1].

**Detractors are exponentially destructive.** "One detractor who's had a terrible experience on your platform is enough to reverse the progress of ten champions" [1]. This asymmetry means that making things right after mistakes is not optional -- it is existential.

**Optimize for speed over scalability.** Code quality and clean architecture are secondary to finding [[Product-Market Fit]]. "Necessity is the mother of invention" -- solve scaling problems when they become real, not before [1].

## The Teespring Origin

Williams ran into a personal pain point at Brown University: he wanted to create a memorial t-shirt for a dive bar that was shutting down. No existing solution met his needs. Teespring launched to solve that problem [1].

The early business was "ridiculously unsustainable": days of meetings per customer, free design work, days of revisions, launching the product on behalf of the customer, managing social media -- all to sell 50 shirts and generate $1,000 in revenue. "Anybody looking in would have said, 'You guys need to give up'" [1].

Williams's two biggest customer segments evolved over time: entrepreneurs building brands and businesses (over 1,000 people making their full-time living on Teespring) and influencers (YouTube stars, Reddit communities, bloggers) adding merchandise as a revenue stream [1].

## Three Phases of Unscalable Work

### 1. Finding First Users

Founders must spend personal time bringing in users themselves. "It could mean sending 100 emails a day, getting on the phone and just calling as many people as you can, going through a network" [1]. Williams compares it to pushing a boulder up a hill: the steepest incline is at the beginning, but it gets easier and eventually the boulder rolls on its own [1].

Williams advises against giving away the product for free. Free users treat products differently from paying users, and free distribution creates a false sense of security about conversion potential [1].

### 2. Turning Users into Champions

A champion is a user who talks about and advocates for the product [1]. The easiest way to create champions is to delight users with an experience they will remember -- "something unusual or out of the ordinary" [1].

Three methods for creating champions through direct engagement [1]:

- **Run customer service yourself.** Williams and co-founder Evan handled all customer service until Teespring reached $130,000-$140,000 per month in revenue. Williams describes an "emotional reaction where my stomach sinks" when opening the support portal because "it sucks talking to users who have had a terrible experience." But the learning is irreplaceable.
- **Proactively reach out to current, future, and departed customers.** Do not take current users for granted. When a user leaves, reach out to learn why -- sometimes the personal outreach alone brings them back. Even when it does not, the insight prevents future churn.
- **Monitor social media and communities.** Williams reads every tweet about Teespring and monitors all Teespring communities, describing himself as "probably a little bit OCD."

### 3. Finding Product-Market Fit Through Speed

The product at launch will almost certainly not be the product that scales. The founder's job is to iterate as fast as possible to reach that product [1].

Williams provides a concrete example: enterprise customers requested features that would take a month to build properly. Instead, co-founder Evan duplicated the entire codebase and database, built a separate product for enterprise customers in three to four days, validated which features mattered, and then integrated the winners into the core product [1].

"Only worry about the next order of magnitude": when you have 10 users, think about getting to 100, not 1 million [1].

Williams describes months-long stretches where Teespring's servers crashed every night. Every team member slept with their phone on loud under their pillow. "The reality is that it was worth it" [1].

## When to Stop Doing Things That Don't Scale

Never, if possible. Williams is emphatic: "There's not some magical moment. It's not the Series A. It's not when you hit a certain revenue milestone. As long as humanly possible, as long as it is a net positive, you need to be spending time talking to your users. It should be ripped from you" [1].
