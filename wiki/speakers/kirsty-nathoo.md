# Kirsty Nathoo

Kirsty Nathoo is the Managing Director of Finance at Y Combinator. She has worked with virtually every company that has gone through YC, from incorporation to fundraising to hiring to exits. She is a finance professional, not a lawyer, but her depth of experience across thousands of companies gives her unusual breadth on the mechanical and legal foundations of startup formation.

## Worldview

**Simple, standard, default.** Nathoo's overarching philosophy is that startup mechanics should not be an area of innovation. Delaware C Corp, standard vesting schedules, SAFEs for early fundraising, Clerky or Valley lawyers for paperwork. Deviating from defaults creates problems that surface at the worst possible moment -- during funding rounds or acquisitions -- and cost enormous time and money to fix [1].

**Think forward, not backward.** On [[Equity Split]], Nathoo's position is clear: co-founders should split equity near-equally because all the work is ahead of them. "If you've worked on it for three months, you've probably got five, ten, fifteen years of this company ahead of you. So think about forwards, not backwards" [1]. Equity disputes are the number one reason YC sees co-founders break up [1].

**Paperwork kills companies silently.** Nathoo tells multiple cautionary tales of companies where improperly filed documents -- a Connecticut LLC conversion done wrong, share purchases never properly papered, missing 83(b) elections -- created crises costing six months and half a million dollars or more. The 83(b) election is the single most critical piece of paperwork because it cannot be fixed retroactively [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Nathoo occupies a unique position in the YC knowledge base. While most YC speakers address strategy, product, and growth, Nathoo covers the unsexy but foundational mechanics that underpin everything else. Her talks are notable for their specificity: she walks through cap table math, SAFE conversion calculations, the difference between contractors and employees, and the exact tax implications of failing to file an 83(b) election [1].

Her horror stories -- the Connecticut conversion, the unpapered shares, the founder who took company money to Vegas -- serve as vivid cautionary tales that make abstract legal concepts concrete and memorable.

## Key Contributions

- **When to incorporate**: Not too soon (still brainstorming), not too late (creating significant IP or charging customers) [1]
- **Why Delaware**: Most developed corporate law, investor expectations, maximum flexibility, best privacy protections [1]
- **Vesting explainer**: Four-year vesting with one-year cliff, with clear reasoning for why it protects remaining founders, investors, and sets precedent for employees [1]
- **83(b) election**: The one unfixable mistake in incorporation. Must be filed with the IRS within 30 days [1]
- **SAFE mechanics**: Detailed walkthrough of how SAFEs convert to equity at a discount relative to the priced round [1]
- **Employee equity rule of thumb**: ~10% for the first 10 employees, on a sliding scale, with vesting on all grants [1]
- **Communication standard**: Founders must understand equity themselves before explaining it to employees. Always communicate both number of shares and percentage of company [1]
