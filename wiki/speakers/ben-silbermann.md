# Ben Silbermann

Ben Silbermann is the co-founder of Pinterest. Before Pinterest, he worked at Google. He left to build a product inspired by his childhood love of collecting things -- stamps, insects, and other objects. Pinterest grew into a visual discovery platform used by hundreds of millions of people.

Silbermann appeared alongside [[Patrick Collison]] and [[John Collison]] (Stripe) in the Stanford "How to Start a Startup" series, where Sam Altman moderated a discussion on [[Company Culture]] and building early teams [1].

## Worldview

**Culture is gardening, not architecture.** Silbermann rejects top-down cultural design: "People think culture is like architecture, when it's a lot more like gardening. You plant some seeds, and then you pull out weeds that aren't working, and they sort of expand" [1]. This inductive approach -- hiring people you want to work with, observing what works, pruning what does not -- defined Pinterest's early culture.

**Hire for quirkiness and curiosity.** Silbermann consistently sought people with wide-ranging interests and deep skill in one area. His first engineers were "probably some of the quirkiest people I've ever met" -- one had made his own board game with elaborate rules, another was into magic tricks and had coded a magic trick app with a self-produced demo video [1]. "Really creative, quirky people that are excited about many disciplines and are extraordinary at one tend to build really great products" [1].

**Pre-calibrate before interviewing in unfamiliar domains.** When hiring for roles he did not understand (head of finance, head of marketing), Silbermann first talked to people he knew were world-class in that discipline: "What are the key traits or characteristics that you look for? What are the questions that you ask? If you're looking for the next person that's as good as you, where are they working right now? And what's their phone number?" [1]. He argues that learning what good looks like during the interview process itself is prohibitively expensive.

**There is no wrong place to find people.** Silbermann hired early employees from Craigslist ads, random tech talks, bars, and weekly barbecues at the office ("bring your own food and drinks, and then we would just talk to folks"). The lesson: "Really good people are generally doing something else. You have to go seek them out rather than expecting that they're going to seek you out" [1].

## Distinct Perspective

Silbermann brings the perspective of a non-technical founder (he was a product manager at Google, not an engineer) building a consumer product where the user base -- initially 80% women -- did not match the founding team's demographics. This forced Pinterest to screen for ambition and excitement about the mission rather than requiring candidates to be lifelong users: "We screen for people that are ambitious and excited about the vision. They have to know exactly how our service works and have to have used it. But they may not be a lifelong user" [1].

He is distinctive in his emphasis on authenticity checks during interviews. When candidates say "I'm really passionate about what you're doing," he asks "Where else are you interviewing?" If they list seven unrelated companies, "that's a sign that they're probably not being authentic about what they care about. Those folks, often when things get really hard, they won't stick it out" [1].

## Key Contributions

- **"Gardening, not architecture"** as a mental model for culture building [1]
- **Reference checking methodology**: force scarcity by asking "top 1%, 5%, or 10%?" to overcome the natural tendency of references to be generically positive [1]
- **Pre-calibration ritual**: talk to known world-class practitioners before interviewing in any unfamiliar domain [1]
- **Scaling through autonomous teams**: each team at Pinterest has a strong designer, engineering leader, writer, and community manager, anchored on a single project with barriers removed [1]
- **Onboarding progression**: from "here's your computer, here's the problem" in a two-bedroom apartment, to a week-long program with 30-day satisfaction and productivity metrics [1]
