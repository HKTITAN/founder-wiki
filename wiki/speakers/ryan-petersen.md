# Ryan Petersen

Ryan Petersen is the founder and CEO of Flexport, a technology-driven freight forwarding company valued at $8 billion that helps nearly 10,000 companies move $19 billion of goods across 112 countries [1]. [[Garry Tan]] was an early investor through both Y Combinator and Initialized Capital [1]. Petersen is also referenced by Tan as exemplifying how unique personal experience leads to [[Startup Ideas]] [2] and how replacing paper with software makes a startup fundamentally faster [3].

## Background and Entrepreneurial Formation

Petersen comes from an entrepreneurial family. His mother is "probably the world's leading expert on food safety" and ran a company employing about 100 PhDs. His father was a computer programmer who taught him to code. As children, Petersen and his brother earned their allowance by buying sodas at grocery stores and reselling them to his mother's company -- "and then my dad wrote software to invoice her" [1].

Before Flexport, Petersen ran businesses for 11 years without venture capital. He co-founded [ImportGenius.com](http://ImportGenius.com), one of the largest providers of shipping manifest data to the global logistics industry. During the early years of that company, he held three flexible side jobs simultaneously -- writing business school case studies for Columbia, teaching the GMAT, and doing SEO consulting -- to sustain himself while the startup developed [1]. This approach reflects his philosophy of designing life so "you can't fail" rather than accepting the 18-month VC runway pressure [1].

Petersen spent four years as Flexport's only employee before applying to YC or raising venture capital. He had worked in international trade for a decade, including living in China for two years importing motorbikes -- giving him what [[Jared Friedman]] calls "10 out of 10 founder/market fit" [2].

## On Scaling: Process vs. Bureaucracy

Petersen's most detailed thinking concerns the transition from small startup to large organization. He identifies a key insight: "Hatred of bureaucracy is not enough to keep bureaucracy away. You also need good process, good systems, great people, and clear strategies to keep the company moving fast" [1].

He frames the tension as finding "the edge between order and chaos" and staying on that line as the company grows [1]. Early in his career, he considered process to be "really corporate" and avoided it. At scale, he realized "the reason that those exist is to enable you to move fast. You'll actually get more bureaucracy from not having any process and policies and standards than you will from having too much" [1].

**Technology architecture as organizational architecture.** Petersen draws a direct parallel between service-oriented architecture (teams communicating via APIs, not direct database access) and organizational design (people owning single-threaded metrics, not coordinating with everyone). Both slow you down initially but enable speed at scale. He cites the Jeff Bezos API memo as "probably the most important document in the technology industry history" [1]. The Marine Corps expression captures it: "Slow is smooth and smooth is fast" [1].

**Team of teams.** Petersen's organizational ideal is "a team of teams" where each small team operates like its own startup. The challenge: giving people clear ownership of a metric they can control. He uses the example of decomposing net revenue into three teams -- buying freight, algorithmic pricing, and sales -- each fully focused on one component rather than everyone wanting to "own all the things" [1].

## On Capital Discipline

Petersen describes a recurring pattern: after every major fundraise, Flexport lost spending discipline. After a $20 million Series A, he immediately committed $100,000 to a trade show booth. After a billion-dollar Series D, "immediately, lost discipline, started overspending. Everybody could hire as many people as they wanted" [1]. After raising a $985 million Series E (while profitable), his "big fear" was the same pattern recurring [1].

His advice: "Money wants to get spent." Founders need "really tight capital allocation, accounting controls, budgetary process" -- which he previously would have considered corporate language [1].

## On Vision

Petersen's vision expanded over time. When he applied to YC, his vision was to be "TurboTax for customs." He once believed planning far into the future was "a sign you're gonna fail" [1]. He now holds a ten-year product roadmap, with the broader vision of making global trade as seamless as an electrical utility -- "When you flip a light switch in your house, you're actually controlling a power plant somewhere" [1].

He advises against premature certainty: "If a founder is too certain of exactly what the vision is and what the product is going to look like and how it's all going to work, they're probably wrong. The world is super messy, and you've got to get out there and iterate" [1].

Tan quotes Petersen calling sales "the most underrated profession" [4].

## On Founder Meddling

Petersen considers himself strong at marketing and recognizes this as a paradox: "The area that I think I'm good at, I probably hold Flexport back" because he meddled too much, preventing the marketing team from developing independently [1]. He observes that many founders create the same dynamic: the function they know best becomes the least developed part of the company because they cannot let go [1].
