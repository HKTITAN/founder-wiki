# Brian Armstrong

Brian Armstrong is the co-founder and CEO of Coinbase, the largest cryptocurrency exchange in the United States, which went public at a nearly $100 billion market cap [1]. Before Coinbase, he worked at Airbnb as an engineer running anti-fraud, and before that ran a tutoring company that he sold. [[Garry Tan]] was one of the first investors in Coinbase, investing $300,000 that became worth over $2.4 billion [2].

## Worldview

Armstrong's thinking centers on a few recurring themes:

**Belief before proof.** Armstrong read the Bitcoin white paper and "kind of couldn't get it out of my head" [1]. He did not have a sophisticated TAM analysis -- "there was no market for it" -- but felt the probability was high enough to make a go of it. He tried to identify every reason it could fail (regulation, cybersecurity flaws) and could not find an obvious one [1].

Armstrong connects this to the broader founder disposition: "There is something about this desire or this ability to look at the world and say, 'Okay, this is how things are, but they could be different,' and not to just dismiss the idea out of hand because it's so ridiculous" [1].

**Building credibility intentionally.** Even when Coinbase was one person, Armstrong created credibility indicators: design quality, email addresses for press/sales (when he was the only employee), his real name on the about page, LinkedIn profile showing his background. "I'm not trying to hide here. I'm not trying to be anonymous" [1]. Getting into YC and getting the right investors were further credibility signals that accumulated to help break through the noise [1].

This was a conscious departure from the cypherpunk culture of early Bitcoin. Armstrong recognized that anonymity worked for individuals but not for a company asking people to deposit money: "If this thing is small, it can probably fly under the radar. But if I want to build a thing that helps the whole world use crypto... there's no way I can be anonymous" [1].

**The builder advantage.** Armstrong coded the first version of Coinbase himself, using Twitter Bootstrap for design. He frames this as both economic necessity and strategic advantage: "Almost you almost never get the first version of the product right. And so if you're overpaying some high-priced firm to build the app and then you get the first version wrong -- which everybody does -- and then you get the second version wrong -- which almost everybody does -- and you're on the third version, you're out of money and you're dead" [1].

**Thinking from the outcome backward.** Armstrong presupposed millions of users and worked backward from there on regulatory compliance, transparency, and trust-building. Tan observes this was "relatively rare" -- most founders "focus on looking like a startup" rather than building as if the outcome were certain [1].

## The Coinbase Origin

In March 2012, Armstrong sent Tan 0.05 bitcoin (worth about two cents) using his prototype called BitBank [2]. The idea was simple: "make a clean, well-lit place for people to buy and hold bitcoin" [2]. Unlike many startups, Coinbase never pivoted -- the only change was the name from BitBank to Coinbase [2].

Armstrong's insight came from working at Airbnb, where he saw how difficult it was to move money across countries -- "every single country of the world had their own little proprietary system with like a couple of entrenched oligopoly companies that were charging opaque fees" [1]. He drew an analogy to the internet: "It wouldn't make sense for each country to have their own proprietary internet and you pay a fee when you want to load a web page from another country. Let's all just run the world on one standard" [1].

## On Innovation at Scale

After Coinbase's IPO, Armstrong launched Coinbase 10 -- allocating 10% of time and resources to new projects, modeled partially after Y Combinator [1]. Products like Coinbase Wallet (self-custody) and Coinbase Commerce emerged from this program. Armstrong recognized the danger of being the bottleneck: "As we got bigger, it can actually be dangerous if I'm like the gatekeeper to creating these new kinds of applications. There have been instances where people inside Coinbase pitched me, I said no, they've left, and those have become huge companies" [1].

The program allows employees to pitch ideas with multiple outcome paths: stay at Coinbase and keep salary/equity (Coinbase owns the product), or leave and get funded by Coinbase Ventures and other investors [1].

## On Cryptocurrency's Three Phases

Armstrong describes crypto's evolution in three phases [1]:

1. **Crypto as investment** -- Bitcoin as digital gold, a scarce asset class.
2. **Crypto as financial system** -- DeFi reinventing lending, insurance, and exchanges in a decentralized way.
3. **Crypto as application platform** -- new identity systems, social media, governance, and DAOs.

He is particularly interested in DAOs as "the Delaware C corp of the cloud." His project Research Hub uses a DAO structure with a traditional C corporation as a technology vendor, exploring how research funding and scientific publication could be reinvented [1].

## On Failure and Persistence

Armstrong tried approximately ten different startups before Coinbase. Eight of nine failed. One "rounded to basically zero but didn't completely fail" [1]. His advice: "Try something, try anything. It almost certainly won't work, but you'll learn something. Try something else. It almost certainly won't work. Try it again" [1].
