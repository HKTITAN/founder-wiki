# WeChat: Growth and Platform Strategy

WeChat reached 889 million monthly active users in under six years. The average user spends 50+ minutes per day across 9-11 separate sessions. Hariharan analyzes the growth strategy behind these numbers ([How WeChat grew to be the #1 app in the world](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/3t-how-wechat-grew-to-be-the-1-app-in-the-world)).

## Build Your Own Competition

Tencent already dominated Chinese social with QQ (650M monthly active instant messenger accounts in 2010). Rather than adapt QQ for mobile, Tencent assembled a skunkworks team of seven engineers from the QQ Mail team. This team built the first version of WeChat in three months. WeChat launched in January 2011 as a simple messaging and photo sharing app. The decision to let WeChat compete with the company's own products mirrors Amazon's cannibalization of its book business with the Kindle and Uber's move from town cars to UberX.

## Designing for Group Behavior

Allen Zhang, WeChat's founder, built the product around "Group Effect" -- the observation that individual behavior differs from group behavior. WeChat paid attention to how features would be adopted by groups, not just individuals.

To solve the cold-start problem of zero friends on day one, WeChat launched "People Nearby," a location-based feature that let users see other WeChat users around them. This boosted daily user acquisition to 100K+. "Shake" had users shaking their phones to find a random person to chat, generating 100 million uses in its first month. These features served [[Early-Stage Tactics|early-stage growth]] needs -- they became less important once most Chinese users were already on WeChat.

After launching account linking between WeChat and QQ, registered users reached 50 million by November 2011. By the end of 2011, daily new user acquisition hit 200K.

## Features from Inner Desires

WeChat Moments launched as a photo-sharing feature inspired by Path, limited to mutual friends rather than friends-of-friends. A team of 10 spent 4 months producing over 30 versions before launch, exemplifying lean [[Product Development]]. The design drew on an old Chinese philosophy called "circle cultures" -- smaller circles have stronger ties at the core, and ties weaken as circles expand.

WeChat Official Accounts allowed celebrities and brands to send content that looked like regular WeChat messages, creating a feeling of personal conversation. This outcompeted Weibo for celebrity content and later expanded to businesses for direct customer communication -- booking, customer care, and Q&A.

## WeChat Red Packets and Payments

WeChat Red Packets originated from Tencent's own need: managers had to give each employee a red envelope with cash after Chinese New Year, and as the company grew, this became exhausting. A team of 20 built the first version in 3 weeks. Over 5 million users sent Red Packets in the first deployment. By 2015, over 1 billion Red Packets were sent during the holiday.

Red Packets drove mobile payments adoption through tight social circles first -- friends and family willing to connect bank accounts. This trust-based approach preceded expansion to merchants and offline stores, an instance of the [[Do Things That Don't Scale|contained fire]] strategy applied to payments. By 2016, Tencent had 600 million mobile payment monthly active users.

## Measure What You Value

WeChat does not measure [[Growth]] only by user count or messages sent. The team also tracks how deeply the product integrates into daily life -- the number of tasks WeChat can help users accomplish each day. Allen Zhang compared the ideal product to Google: once the user finds the right link, they leave. WeChat limits marketing content on Moments and restricts broadcast messages from brands to one per day, without push notifications.

The app has maintained only 4 tabs since launch. Experiments with a fifth tab showed 20-30% conversion drops on the leftmost tab.
