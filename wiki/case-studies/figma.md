# Figma: Ten-Year Overnight Success

Figma is the collaborative design tool that replaced Photoshop as the dominant design platform. Adobe acquired the company for $20 billion [1]. Co-founded by [[Dylan Field]] (19 years old at the time) and Evan Wallace in August 2012, Figma's journey illustrates how new technologies create startup opportunities, why management is a skill that must be learned, and why the path to a billion-dollar outcome requires navigating thousands of individual decisions over a decade [1].

## Origin: Technology Plus Problem

In 2011, WebGL 1.0 launched -- a new way for websites to draw and render graphics inside a web browser. Field and Wallace knew they wanted to build something with this technology and cared about making creative tools accessible. They agreed with Bret Victor's statement that "if you can't use a tool because it's too complex, it's a moral wrong" [1].

[[Garry Tan]] frames this pattern as common: Instagram started from experiments with new APIs (HTML5 meets social location sharing). The frontier technology is not enough on its own -- Field also knew he wanted to walk toward the problem of design tools being expensive and hard to use [1].

## The Meme Generator Detour

After exploration mode, Field and Wallace briefly built a meme generator with WebGL. Field describes the moment of realization: "After like a week of working on this meme generator, I remember looking in the mirror and being just like, 'Holy -- I dropped out of Brown for this? What am I doing with my life?'" An hour later, his co-founder took off his headphones and said, "Dude, I just can't do this" [1].

Tan uses this as a cautionary tale about technology-for-its-own-sake: "Meaningless products that don't solve real-world problems but seem adjacent to hot buzzwords" can consume founders. The solution is self-knowledge -- "if that's not yours, you won't be able to recruit, build, and do what it takes to create day in and day out" [1].

## The Management Crisis

A year into Figma, Field was struggling with management. He made hires that were poor cultural fits and was micromanaging rather than empowering people: "When you hire smart people, you got to give them a way to really contribute and to blow you away and to let them do things that you would never think of. And if you micromanage and give people the exact recipe for what to do, they're not gonna appreciate that if they're the right people to hire" [1].

The crisis peaked when employees confronted him directly: "People were really unhappy with me. It was a moment where they said, 'We really need you to get help to make this work'" [1]. This occurred while Figma had not yet launched and while Field's father was dying of cancer [1].

The resolution: hiring the company's first outside manager, from whom Field learned management skills. Field distinguishes management from leadership: "Management is a skill that you have to build just like any other skill. There's muscles and different things you have to figure out about it" [1].

Tan draws the universal lesson: "Every highly talented technical founder who starts and wants to stay in that CEO position for the long haul will end up having to learn what Dylan learned: how to be a manager." He recommends executive coaches, great managers as peers, and investors who can help with management development [1].

Tan also connects this to his own experience at Posterous: "I had reached a point where I needed to switch into a people manager, and I wish I could say I succeeded. But in fact, I ran away from it and I just coded harder. It was the wrong move for Posterous" [1].

## Slow Is Smooth, Smooth Is Fast

Tan frames Figma's later success through the Navy SEAL maxim: "Slow is smooth, and smooth is fast." Reducing chaos and helping the executive team be productive makes things smooth, and "only then can you create something for a billion people" [1].
