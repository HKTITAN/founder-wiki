# Box: From Consumer Cloud Storage to Enterprise Platform

Box is a cloud content management and file-sharing platform for enterprises. Founded in 2005 by Aaron Levie and three co-founders while they were college sophomores and freshmen, the company pivoted from consumer to enterprise in 2006-2007 and grew to 71,000 customers and 63% of the Fortune 500 [1].

## The Origin: Personal Pain Point

In late 2004, accessing and sharing files was difficult. USB thumb drives, self-emailing files, and FTP accounts were the norm. During an internship, Levie also encountered enterprise content management software that was "way too complicated" [1].

The founders identified three technology trends that could solve the problem: mobile ubiquity (though their original vision was "the entire planet would start to be using Blackberries everywhere" -- "we were wrong"), dropping cost of computing and storage (not yet called "cloud computing"), and increasing internet interconnectedness [1].

They launched box.net with a simple proposition: upload files and access them from any browser or mobile device. Within months, thousands of people signed up. The signup flow required only an email address -- "actually pretty disruptive at the time to traditional IT" where employees needed corporate approval for technology [1].

## The Garage Phase

The founding team dropped out of college and moved to the Bay Area. They lived and worked out of a renovated garage (technically zoned as a garage, "so it was actually pretty illegal") [1]. Levie describes the psychological gap between the glory narrative of dropouts -- "Bill Gates dropped out. Steve Jobs dropped out" -- and the reality of four people iterating on a business model daily, "pivoting almost every single day" [1].

## The Consumer-to-Enterprise Pivot

The critical decision came when the team had to choose between consumers and businesses [1].

**The consumer problem:** The maximum a consumer would pay was about $10 per month. Google, Facebook, Apple, and Microsoft were clearly going to provide free storage. "Not a great business model when you only have about 35% of the market willing to upgrade" [1]. Advertising was considered and rejected: "You really don't want to see ads when you're looking at your tax documents" [1].

**The enterprise opportunity:** Companies were spending millions on legacy content management software. Box could deliver equivalent or better functionality for a fraction of the cost -- potentially a 10 to 20x improvement [1].

The pivot felt unappealing. The founders were 22 and 23. "There's a long list of things that you could do that aren't very cool. Somewhere near the bottom of that list when you're 23 is starting an enterprise software company" [1]. Enterprise software carried connotations of slow innovation, expensive technology, complex interfaces, pushy sales reps, and rare updates [1].

The resolution: "If we're going to forego all the fun and all the exciting times we would have as a consumer company, then we have to find a way to build an enterprise software company that operates and acts and innovates and moves like a consumer company" [1].

## Why Enterprise Was Ripe for Disruption

The traditional enterprise software model had structural weaknesses that new technology trends could exploit [1]:

- Complex technology sold through salespeople (no self-service)
- Only affordable by the largest companies
- Customer bore the security risk (buying separate security layers)
- Customer bore the financial risk (paying for licenses upfront regardless of usage)
- Proprietary, closed platforms

The new model inverted each: simple and end-user-focused, available to all sizes, security baked in, vendor bears the risk (subscription model), and open systems [1].

## The Disruption in Practice

Box's initial product was absurdly simple by enterprise standards. Legacy competitors "would have died laughing" at a product where "all it does is you upload a file and then you access the file" [1]. But that simplicity was the disruption. Enterprise vendors had assumed value correlated with feature density. Box proved that "the fewer features, the simpler set of functionality in one place is actually going to be our value proposition" [1].

## The Ongoing Enterprise Opportunity

Levie argues that the best opportunities in enterprise software are still ahead (as of 2017) [1]. Two factors drive this:

1. Most enterprise technology remains legacy -- "old, slow, complex, expensive" -- and must be retired
2. Every company in every industry recognizes the need to modernize because of digital disruption: "If you're a media company, you're freaking out because of Netflix. If you're a financial services company, you're freaking out because of Stripe" [1]
