# True Link Financial: Profitability Through Underserved Markets

True Link Financial is a neobank that provides financial services for people with special needs -- the elderly, the autistic, those recovering from addiction, and others who need caregivers to help manage their financial lives. Founded by Kai Stinchcombe (YC/Initialized), True Link claims to be the first neobank to cross profitability [1]. [[Garry Tan]] presents the company as a case study in how targeting a specific underserved audience creates structural advantages in customer acquisition, loyalty, and unit economics [1].

## Origin: Personal Experience with the Problem

Stinchcombe's grandmother had Alzheimer's and was struggling to bank: "If you can't remember what you did yesterday, that means you can't remember if you spent any money, which means your budget is always full. Every person with Alzheimer's will at some stage in their condition have that problem" [1].

## The Power of a Specific Underserved Customer

Investors pushed Stinchcombe to broaden the target: "They say we want you to be for everyone. It's a bigger TAM" [1]. Stinchcombe resisted, arguing that specialization creates advantages rather than limitations: "We don't have to go out and try to be a better version of Chase than Chase is, which is really hard. You're boiling the ocean. We have to find stuff that Chase overlooked, which is very easy when you find an underserved audience" [1].

Serving this specific audience meant True Link had to understand domains that no generic bank would: qualifying for benefits, checking into and out of recovery centers, the role of in-home caregivers, types of residential care facilities [1]. Stinchcombe says the first quality they look for in hiring is humility: "If you are going into this thinking you know all the answers, you've just never walked in every single set of shoes" [1].

## Life-Changing Product as CAC Advantage

The depth of understanding led to features with disproportionate impact. One example: if a person in addiction recovery makes an unexpected late-night cash withdrawal, a caregiver receives a text message and can intervene. Stinchcombe: "There's people who say, 'Oh my God, you saved my life,' which is a neat thing to be able to say as a financial services startup" [1].

Tan frames the unit economics: "When you get the target customer right -- that they are underserved and they have a real need and you can solve it -- that's priceless. You can get in front of them for far less. Lifetime value is super high and cost of customer acquisition is super low" [1].

Stinchcombe makes the distribution point explicit: "If you are targeting the same customer everyone else is -- 'Oh, we want affluent, two-income young professionals' -- that's going to be the most expensive customer to advertise to on the internet. What if we want to go chase down a million people with Alzheimer's? Who's advertising to that person?" [1].
