# Coinbase: From Bitcoin at Five Dollars to a Hundred Billion Dollar Company

Coinbase is the largest cryptocurrency exchange in the United States, going public at a nearly $100 billion market cap [1]. Founded by [[Brian Armstrong]] in 2012, the company started when Bitcoin was worth approximately $5 and almost no one believed in it [1]. [[Garry Tan]] was one of the first investors, turning a $300,000 seed check into over $2.4 billion [2].

## Origin: Solving a Problem the Founder Experienced

Armstrong's path to Coinbase began with a specific frustration. He downloaded the Bitcoin desktop client, which had a command-line interface and required syncing a node on his MacBook. "I knew that just wasn't going to scale to a large number of people" [1]. He drew analogies to email servers (nobody wants to run their own; they want Gmail) and Git (hard to use until GitHub made it easier). He recognized that "someone's going to make the hosted wallet in the cloud thing for crypto" [1].

He initially tried to resist the idea because of its scope: "That's a really big responsibility. You're going to be storing people's money. You're going to have to have regulation" [1]. His first prototype was a Bitcoin wallet for Android phones, but it required running a full Bitcoin node on the phone and took hours to sync even on WiFi. He realized the cloud version was inevitable and "couldn't help myself" [1].

In March 2012, Armstrong sent Tan 0.05 bitcoin (worth about two cents at the time, worth about $2,500 by 2021) using his prototype, then called BitBank [2]. The only thing that changed from day one was the name -- unlike most startups, Coinbase never pivoted [2].

## Why Tan Said Yes When Everyone Else Said No

Tan identifies three reasons he invested when nearly everyone else passed [2]:

1. **Primed for the problem.** Tan had tried to buy Bitcoin on Mt. Gox, the first major exchange, and experienced a terrible UX. The bad design "felt so wrong, and I knew that couldn't be the thing that brought bitcoin all the way" [2].
2. **Primed for the idea.** In 2005, working as an engineer at Peter Thiel's hedge fund Clarium Capital, Tan read "The Dollar Crisis" by Richard Duncan, which described the consequences of Nixon taking the U.S. off the gold standard in 1971. "Twenty-five percent of all dollars in circulation today were printed just in the last year, in 2020" [2]. This had primed Tan for Bitcoin's thesis.
3. **Brian was a builder.** Armstrong had been head of anti-fraud at Airbnb, which Tan describes as "being part of the special forces" -- he was attacked by hackers daily. Armstrong could build and secure the first version himself [2].

## Being Better: The Clean, Well-Lit Place

Tan categorizes Coinbase as a "better" startup in his [[Better, Faster, or Cheaper]] framework [3]. Compared to Mt. Gox (the dominant option in 2012), Coinbase was dramatically better: Mt. Gox required wiring money via Western Union to an overseas bank, and the website had originally been built to trade Magic: The Gathering cards -- that is why it was called "Mt. Gox" (Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange) [3].

Armstrong's first version of Coinbase used Twitter Bootstrap and "wasn't by any means the most beautiful site, but it was functional and it had something about it that wasn't sketchy" [1]. He describes the vision as making "a clean, well-lit place to actually buy this stuff and interact with it" [1].

## Building Credibility From Nothing

Armstrong made a conscious decision to be transparent and non-anonymous, breaking from the cypherpunk culture of early Bitcoin. He put his real name on the about page, linked to his LinkedIn, created email addresses for press and sales (even though he was the only person), and sought credibility signals like YC and established investors [1].

He framed this as thinking from the outcome backward: "If I want to build a thing that helps the whole world use crypto... there's no way I can be anonymous. I need to do this above board from day one" [1]. Tan observes that this backward-from-outcome thinking was rare: most founders "focus on looking like a startup" rather than building as if success were certain [1].

## Innovation at Scale: Coinbase 10

Post-IPO, Armstrong launched Coinbase 10, allocating 10% of time and resources to new products, modeled partially after Y Combinator [1]. Products including Coinbase Wallet (self-custody) and Coinbase Commerce emerged from this program.

Armstrong recognized the danger of founder-as-bottleneck: "There have been instances where people inside Coinbase pitched me, I said no, they've left, and those have become huge companies" [1]. The program lets employees pitch ideas with multiple paths: stay at Coinbase, leave and get funded, or hybrid arrangements [1].

## Three Phases of Crypto

Armstrong describes crypto's evolution [1]:
1. **Investment** -- Bitcoin as digital gold.
2. **Financial system** -- DeFi reinventing lending, insurance, exchanges.
3. **Application platform** -- new identity, social media, governance, DAOs.

## Key Lessons

- **Be contrarian and right.** Find something fringe but ultimately correct. "Every crazy idea will have its day, but only if it has a truly compelling pitch" [2].
- **Be a builder.** "The more you can do, the less you leave to chance, and the more people will want to fund you, join you, or use your product" [2].
- **Send a compelling first email.** Armstrong sent Tan 0.05 bitcoin with his prototype -- "he literally sent me free money. It got my attention, and it showed me Brian was serious about building something genuinely useful" [2].
