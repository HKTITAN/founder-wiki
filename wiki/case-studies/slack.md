# Slack: Twice Pivoting from Failed Games to Iconic Products

Slack is an enterprise messaging platform that grew past $200 million in GAAP revenue (as of 2017) while still growing rapidly. Stewart Butterfield, Slack's co-founder and CEO, arrived at Slack through two failed attempts to build web-based massively multiplayer games -- the first producing Flickr, the second producing Slack [1].

## The Original Vision: Online Games

Butterfield's fascination began in 1992 when he first used the internet in college -- IRC, Usenet, email. The idea of a game that "lived online" and used "play as a pretext for social interaction" seemed compelling [1].

### Attempt 1: Game to Flickr (2002-2004)

The first game company started in 2002, after the dot-com crash, 9/11, and the Enron accounting scandals. The NASDAQ was down 80% from its peak. "No one wanted to invest in internet-facing stuff" [1]. The team raised friends-and-family money and spent a year building a prototype. The prototype was well-received, but finishing it required another year and a half, and "we literally couldn't raise any money at all" [1]. At one point, only the team member with children was being paid.

The pivot to Flickr reused existing technology. The game client had inventory slots along the bottom of the screen; these became photo slots. The game's messaging server enabled real-time photo sharing. But the initial version was "a terrible product because you had to be online at the same time as someone you cared about" to use it [1]. It demoed well but failed in practice because "you didn't care about any of the people who were actually online" [1]. The product evolved quickly from there.

### Attempt 2: Game to Slack (2009-2013)

The second attempt started in 2009 with $17.5 million in venture capital, plus AWS, better hardware, and many more people online. None of those external improvements changed the underlying problem: "there wasn't a big enough market" for a web-based multiplayer game [1]. The game, called Glitch, attracted an engaged but small user base with a "leaky bucket" -- very difficult to add new users [1].

Butterfield felt stuck between contradictory narratives: "Good entrepreneurs are resilient. When everyone else thinks it's a bad idea, it's probably really a good idea" versus the reality that "I'm the CEO. I'm the chairman of the board. I'm sure that this is not going to work" [1]. The decisive factor: "I had exhausted all of the ideas we had to fix the problems." The team had focused on new user experience and retention. Ideas that once filled whiteboards had been reduced to three bad ones. "When you're out of ideas about how to make it work" is the signal to move on [1].

## The Three-and-a-Half-Year Unconscious Design Process

Slack emerged from the internal communication system the game team had been using [1]. The foundation was IRC, which Butterfield describes as "Slack with all of the things that were missing when it was originally designed in 1989" [1].

The development followed a pattern:

1. IRC could not store and forward messages, so the team built a logging bot
2. With messages in a database, they built search
3. No good iPhone IRC clients existed in 2009, so they built an HTML5 mobile frontend
4. Once they could view messages on mobile, they wanted to post from mobile

Each improvement addressed "whatever problem is the most irritating" with minimal effort, then the team returned to building the game [1]. This continued for three and a half years with 45 people generating real-world usage feedback.

Butterfield argues this process was superior to normal software development because it eliminated ego and speculation. In typical product discussions, "people are very ego-laden" and arguments center on speculation ("I think this could be really valuable"). The proto-Slack development was purely reactive: "an opportunity that was so obvious that we couldn't help but take advantage of it" [1].

The result: "a fully designed product that we were using that was a terrible implementation. Not what you would do if you started from scratch, but it was obvious that this was something that had enormous value. Everyone who worked at the company agreed they would never work without something like this again" [1].

## Underestimating the Market

When pitching Andreessen Horowitz (one of the game's investors) on the pivot, Butterfield projected that Slack could eventually become "a hundred million a year revenue business -- valued at a billion dollars" and thought that was "a decade out" representing 100% of the total addressable market [1]. The company passed that figure in year two. By 2017, it projected $200 million in GAAP revenue while still growing rapidly, with a revised market estimate of "ten billion in revenue, like a hundred billion dollar company" [1].

As of 2017, Slack had 38,000 paid customers. The sales team had never talked to more than 90% of them because they could not reach that many people -- adoption was almost entirely product-driven [1].

## Saying No to Social

Roughly one-third of Slack teams created were for social (non-business) usage. Multiple people proposed pursuing this: "Slack for groups" or pivoting to become "a private version of Twitter" [1]. Butterfield resisted: "You can only do so many things well and there's a lot of shiny objects like that that are very distracting" [1]. He describes himself as "chief no-sayer" [1].
